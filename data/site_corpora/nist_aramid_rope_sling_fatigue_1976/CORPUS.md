# Historical evidence digest: aramid-fiber rope sling fatigue and weatherability (NBSIR 76-1159)

## Use boundary: read this first

This is a manually written evidence digest of a **September 1976 prototype test program for helicopter external-cargo sling legs**. It is not a current equipment standard, an operational manual, or a validation of any rope system. The central result is that the manufacturer-installed end fittings were inadequate: those inadequate end fittings prevented a thorough evaluation of the rope bodies and left the report with observations rather than fully substantiated conclusions. [NBSIR-76-1159: title page; abstract; §8 Concluding Remarks]

Nothing here transfers the prototype findings to bondage rope, human suspension, natural-fiber rope, knots, uplines, body contact, care or retirement rules, load ratings, end-fitting construction, or helicopter operation. The source studied aramid-fiber prototype sling legs with manufacturer-installed industrial terminations. Its material, construction, termination, loading, and service context differ from those other domains. [NBSIR-76-1159: §1 Introduction; §2 Specimens]

The digest intentionally omits numerical loads, capacity ratings, loading recipes, fitting dimensions, apparatus construction details, vendor claims, obsolete procurement information, photographs, figures, and source tables. The retained value is historical and methodological: it shows how termination failures, sparse sampling, test-control anomalies, and uncombined environmental stresses can prevent apparently extensive rope tests from answering the intended material question. [NBSIR-76-1159: §3 Preliminary Tensile Tests; §4 Fatigue Studies; §5 Simulated Weathering Studies; §7 Discussion]

## Source identity and date

The report is *Fatigue and Weatherability Studies of Aramid-Fiber Rope Slings*, NBSIR 76-1159, by Nixon Halsey and Leonard Mordfin of the National Bureau of Standards. The report itself is dated September 1976 and identifies a U.S. Army air-mobility laboratory as sponsor. Its DOI is `10.6028/NBS.IR.76-1159`. The current NIST landing page lists the same authors, report number, and DOI, while giving January 1, 1976 as its publication date; the September date is the one printed on both the report title page and bibliographic sheet. [NIST-LANDING: publication metadata; NBSIR-76-1159: title page; bibliographic data sheet]

The work belonged to a program seeking lighter, easier-to-handle external-cargo slings for helicopters. This phase explored whether a then-new synthetic aramid material might be useful. It was a prototype-development study, not certification of a finished sling family and not a general study of rope. [NBSIR-76-1159: §1 Introduction]

## What was tested

The program tested 26 prototype sling-leg specimens. Each specimen combined an aramid-fiber rope body with an attached end fitting, which means the experimental object was the complete specimen rather than bare rope. That distinction is decisive because the terminations often governed the observed failure. [NBSIR-76-1159: abstract; §2 Specimens; §8 Concluding Remarks]

The specimen set crossed two rope configurations with three nominal capacity families. In the **parallel-strand** configuration, individually formed strands were gathered without cabling the collection together. In the **cabled-strand** configuration, strands were assembled around a core in a cabled structure. The report labels the capacity families low, medium, and high; this digest preserves those category names but not their historical numerical ratings. Jacket materials and end-fitting variants introduced further differences, yielding six basic rope styles and nine specimen styles. [NBSIR-76-1159: §2 Specimens; §2.2 Parallel-Strand Rope Construction; §2.3 Cabled-Strand Rope Construction; §2.4 Jackets; §2.5 End Fittings]

The ropes were aramid-fiber constructions with protective jackets. Jacket families included treated braid, extruded polymer, and untreated braid. Those differences matter as experimental confounders: configuration, capacity family, jacket, fitting, and potting practice were not varied as one clean, balanced factorial design. A result associated with one specimen style therefore cannot automatically be assigned to the rope configuration alone. [NBSIR-76-1159: §2 Specimens; §2.4 Jackets; §7.3 Weatherability]

All specimens arrived with manufacturer-installed potted compression-type terminations. Some fittings were commercial types associated with other rope classes; a small subset used experimental fittings. The report's own discussion warns that a fitting developed for steel wire rope or a lower-modulus synthetic rope should not be presumed suitable for aramid rope. This is retained only as an explanation of confounding in the historical experiment, not as fitting-selection or design advice. [NBSIR-76-1159: §2.5 End Fittings; §7.4 End Fittings]

## The study paths

### Preliminary tensile tests

The preliminary tests loaded specimens repeatedly and then continued to failure. Every tested specimen failed near an end fitting. Potting resin had moved beyond the fitting into adjacent fibers and then cured, removing normal flexibility at the transition. Because the termination region failed first, the tests did not determine the true strength of the rope bodies and could not support a strength comparison between parallel- and cabled-strand constructions. [NBSIR-76-1159: §3 Preliminary Tensile Tests; §7.1 Breaking Loads]

This is a classic censored-measurement problem. A machine can record a precise failure load while still failing to measure the target property. Here, the observed endpoint was often the capacity of a defective rope-to-fitting transition, not the intrinsic capacity of the rope free length. The report accordingly treated even apparently strong results cautiously. [NBSIR-76-1159: §3 Preliminary Tensile Tests; §7.1 Breaking Loads]

### Increasing-load-spectrum fatigue

One fatigue path used three successively more demanding blocks. Five specimens entered this path. Only one completed it without failure; the other four stopped early because of end-fitting failures rather than identified rope-body deficiencies. The automatic spectrum programmer also malfunctioned, so some specimens received cycle histories that deviated from the specified spectra. Both facts weaken cross-specimen comparisons: the dominant failure site was outside the intended material question, and the delivered histories were not perfectly uniform. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4.1 Increasing-Load-Spectrum Tests]

The defensible conclusion is not that four ropes failed the spectrum. It is that four complete sling-leg specimens failed, with the failures attributed to terminations. The rope-body fatigue endurance for those specimens remained unresolved. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4.1 Increasing-Load-Spectrum Tests; §7.2 Fatigue Endurance]

### Alternating-block-spectrum fatigue

The second fatigue path alternated a repeated baseline block with brief higher-load interruptions. Seven specimens entered this path: two parallel-strand and five cabled-strand. One parallel-strand specimen pulled out of a fitting prematurely, while the other completed its test. Among the cabled-strand specimens, three completed without observed failure, one sustained a partial strand failure but completed the scheduled history, and one developed a free-length fatigue failure relatively early. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4.2 Alternating-Block-Spectrum Tests]

These mixed outcomes show why survivor counts alone are inadequate. "Completed," "partial rope damage," "free-length failure," and "termination failure" represent different evidence states. Pooling them into a single pass/fail number would erase the distinction between an interpretable rope event and a censored termination event. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4.2 Alternating-Block-Spectrum Tests; §7.2 Fatigue Endurance]

Across both fatigue paths, twelve specimens were fatigue-loaded. Only one failed to endure its imposed spectrum because of damage identified in the rope itself; the others either completed their histories or stopped at an end-fitting inadequacy. That single free-length event did not establish a construction-wide weakness because another specimen of the same broad configuration and capacity family completed the same spectrum. The study also lacked high-capacity parallel-strand fatigue specimens, preventing a direct configuration comparison in that family. [NBSIR-76-1159: §7.2 Fatigue Endurance]

### Residual-strength follow-up

Seven specimens that had completed or otherwise survived their fatigue histories were later pulled in tension. Two then failed in the rope free length, although one had already shown strand damage during fatigue and the other may have been damaged by recoil from a fitting failure. The other five failed in or near an end fitting. Thus most follow-up tests again measured a termination-limited endpoint rather than clean post-fatigue rope residual strength. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4.3 Residual Strength Tests]

The report could not turn these follow-ups into a robust residual-strength distribution. Prior damage state differed among survivors, the survival process selected which specimens reached follow-up, and the terminations censored most final tests. A survivor that later reaches a high force before a fitting event is not proof that the rope retained a particular fraction of its original strength. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4.3 Residual Strength Tests; §7.2 Fatigue Endurance]

### Simulated weathering

Seven specimens were held under tension during accelerated cycles combining moisture, heat, and ultraviolet exposure. The weathering and fatigue programs used different specimens, so the study did not test their combined effect. The environmental apparatus also changed after the first test when unexpectedly high temperatures were observed, and the source records additional timing or airflow anomalies for individual runs. These controls make the weathering series exploratory rather than a uniform exposure dataset. [NBSIR-76-1159: §5 Simulated Weathering Studies; §5.1 Test Equipment; §7.5 Synergistic Effects]

Three specimens, all parallel-strand varieties, pulled out at the end fitting exposed inside the chamber. Their potting material had softened excessively during the simulated environment. Four specimens completed the exposure. When those four were subsequently tension-tested, only two failures occurred in the rope free length and therefore reflected rope residual strength more directly. The other failure modes did not support a clean comparison between configurations. [NBSIR-76-1159: §5.2 Tests and Results; §7.3 Weatherability]

One weathered cabled-strand specimen with a braided jacket showed visible fiber discoloration in the exposed region; the report did not observe the same change in the other specimens. That observation was based on one specimen style and was entangled with jacket differences. It is evidence of a particular historical observation, not a general weathering law or a visual retirement criterion. [NBSIR-76-1159: §5.2 Tests and Results; §7.3 Weatherability]

### Permanent elongation

The report calculated permanent or quasi-permanent elongation from free-length measurements taken around the tensile, fatigue, weathering, and follow-up tests. It observed length changes after loading, a tendency for larger changes with larger rope diameter, and a qualitative tendency for fatigue-related elongation to be greater in the cabled-strand group than in the parallel-strand group. Weathered specimens also exhibited creep-like length change. [NBSIR-76-1159: §6 Permanent Elongation]

The authors explicitly described these elongation conclusions as approximate and unsubstantiated because the dataset was sparse. Measurement opportunities and histories differed, and the design was not adequate to isolate configuration, diameter, treatment, and termination effects. Accordingly, elongation in this report is a descriptive outcome, not a service limit, inspection threshold, care instruction, or retirement rule. [NBSIR-76-1159: §6 Permanent Elongation]

## Why the end fittings control the interpretation

The report identifies several termination-related mechanisms. Potting material could migrate into the adjacent rope and cure into a stiff transition. Environmental exposure could soften a different potting system enough to permit pullout. A comparatively hard potting material could leave a sharp stiffness transition and local stress concentration even without visible seepage. Failures appeared adjacent to fittings, within fittings, and by pullout. These are distinct mechanisms, but each can prevent a test from reaching a rope-body endpoint. [NBSIR-76-1159: §3 Preliminary Tensile Tests; §5.2 Tests and Results; §7.4 End Fittings]

The study therefore does not support ranking parallel-strand against cabled-strand rope by intrinsic breaking strength. It also does not establish that either construction met a general fatigue or weatherability requirement. Most failures were directly attributable to fitting inadequacies, only 26 prototypes were tested, some cells were absent, and the remaining rope-body events were too sparse and inconsistent for complete evaluation. [NBSIR-76-1159: §7.1 Breaking Loads; §7.2 Fatigue Endurance; §7.3 Weatherability; §8 Concluding Remarks]

The authors offered cautiously favorable observations about what future, adequately terminated prototypes might be capable of, but they explicitly framed them as observations offered without fully substantiated conclusions. Those conditional statements are not evidence that the tested specimens were qualified, and they are not modern recommendations. [NBSIR-76-1159: §8 Concluding Remarks]

## Evidence that can and cannot be carried forward

The durable methodological lesson is that a rope test must classify *where and how* failure occurs before using the result to infer a rope-body property. Termination-limited failures are not interchangeable with free-length failures. Test-history deviations, prior damage, survivor selection, jacket differences, missing comparison cells, and separately applied stressors must remain visible in any analysis. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4 Fatigue Studies; §5 Simulated Weathering Studies; §7 Discussion]

The historical evidence supports only a narrow account: these particular aramid-fiber prototype sling legs showed a mixture of termination failures, a small number of rope-body fatigue events, a few interpretable residual-strength events, visible weathering in one specimen style, and approximate permanent elongation. The program did not isolate a universal material law. [NBSIR-76-1159: §4.3 Residual Strength Tests; §6 Permanent Elongation; §8 Concluding Remarks]

Do not use this digest to choose equipment, set a load, construct an end fitting, plan an aircraft lift, judge body-contact safety, or infer the performance of a modern rope. Any such use would exceed both the study design and this corpus's admitted scope. [NBSIR-76-1159: §1 Introduction; §8 Concluding Remarks]
