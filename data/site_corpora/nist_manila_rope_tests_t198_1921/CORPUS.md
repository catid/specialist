# NBS Technologic Paper T198: bounded historical rope-construction and test-design evidence

Historical non-operational rope evidence: *Results of Some Tests of Manila Rope* is Technologic Paper T198 of the United States Bureau of Standards. Ambrose H. Stang, identified on the report as an associate physicist, and Lory R. Strickenberg, identified as an assistant mechanical engineer, are the named authors. The report was issued in 1921 and is preserved here only as historical evidence. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 1]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The official NIST year catalog identifies the work as T198, gives Stang and Strickenberg as its authors, and assigns DOI 10.6028/nbst.6078. That catalog also warns that its page is no longer maintained and may be out of date, so it supports identity rather than current technical guidance. [NIST-CATALOG-T198, T198 entry and page-status notice]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The cover gives an issue date in September 1921, while the closing line dates the underlying report from Washington earlier that year. These dates describe publication history; they do not make the measurements current or establish continuity with any later rope product. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 1 and 13]

## What material the study actually covered

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The evidence concerns commercial three-strand, regular-lay Manila rope obtained chiefly through government purchase-order testing around the study period. The report says that most specimens came from rope manufacturers under purchase orders for government departments and that the accumulated test data came from the preceding few years. It therefore describes a historical procurement stream, not a newly recruited or randomly sampled product population. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The reported pool contained 368 specimens, but that count does not make it representative of modern rope. Manufacturer, purchase specification, nominal size, construction, and period procurement context were entangled. The report itself says departmental purchase specifications had not all been identical and that the tests were not part of an investigational program. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: All specimens were described as commercial three-strand regular-lay rope. A separate subset of 18 specially hard-laid specimens supplied the elongation observations. Findings from that special subset are not interchangeable with findings from the full commercial pool. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4 and 11]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: The original diameter range is intentionally omitted because it could invite unsafe transfer or reconstruction of the paper's performance rules. This digest retains the construction class and sampling distinction, not the report's size-dependent outputs. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4]

## Construction vocabulary

Historical non-operational rope evidence: In the report's construction hierarchy, yarns are the smaller constituent elements grouped into strands, and strands are the larger elements assembled into rope. The paper notes that yarns may also be called plies or threads in period cordage vocabulary. These are structural terms, not statements about fiber quality or suitability. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The paper uses *lay* at two levels. Rope lay is the axial distance over which the strand structure completes one turn around the rope axis; strand lay is the axial distance over which yarn structure completes one turn around the strand axis. A lay measurement therefore needs its structural level stated explicitly. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 7]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The report calls a construction regular lay when yarns turn around the strand axis in one direction while strands turn around the rope axis in the opposite direction. This is a construction description only. It does not establish strength, security, service life, or fitness for a task. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 7]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The period text associates hard-laid and soft-laid descriptions with the amount of twist used in forming strands. Those labels are relative construction vocabulary in this source, not standardized modern product grades and not permission to infer behavior from a label alone. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 7]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The authors observed that ropes of the same nominal diameter from different makers could contain different numbers of yarns. They also reported a general tendency for smaller historical ropes to use smaller yarns. The first observation is useful as a warning that nominal external size does not uniquely identify internal construction; the second remains bounded to this historical sample. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: Yarn count, strand count, rope lay, and strand lay are distinct descriptors. Treating any one of them as a complete representation of a rope would discard construction information that the original investigators recorded separately. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 4 and 8]

## Sampling, preparation, and measurement distinctions

Historical non-operational rope evidence: Breaking response was observed for the full tensile-test pool, while mass per unit length, yarn count, and rope and strand lay were recorded only for a less precisely described subset. Elongation belonged to the much smaller hard-laid subset. The resulting fields therefore have different coverage and should not be treated as a complete rectangular dataset. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The laboratory used a fixed test procedure across procurement submissions, but a fixed procedure is not the same thing as controlled sampling. The report expressly says that many variables affecting rope properties were not determined because the tests were not organized as an investigation. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: Tensile specimens used period-specific spliced end terminations and conditioning intended to shift rupture into the rope body and limit termination slip. Despite those precautions, roughly half reportedly failed at or near a splice. This is important negative evidence that the termination and specimen-preparation system materially affected observed failure location. No splice or conditioning recipe is retained here. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 7]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: More than one capacity of test machine was used, with some smaller specimens assigned to a different machine. The report presents a common loading procedure, but machine assignment is still part of the measurement context and should not disappear when interpreting the historical records. Machine capacities, rates, fastening details, and operating instructions are excluded. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 7]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The paper separates tensile failure measurement from mass-per-length measurement and from size and construction measurements. Mass and size were assessed under a prescribed period load state, while lay and constituent counts were observed directly. These are method distinctions, not a modern test protocol, and this digest omits the load rule and all handling steps. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 7–8]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The report derived size from a circumference measurement made around the loaded specimen rather than documenting a direct diameter measurement at multiple orientations. It does not present a modern uncertainty budget for this transformation or for the effects of irregular natural-fiber geometry. The latter limitation is an inference from what the measurement description does and does not report, not a quantified source result. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 8]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The full paper does not document modern lot traceability, botanical or geographic fiber provenance, production date, prior storage history, treatment chemistry, moisture state before receipt, randomized sampling, blinded measurement, or independent replication. Their absence prevents attribution of between-specimen variation to any one cause. [NBS-T198-1921, whole-PDF absence audit, pp. 3–13]

## Negative evidence and limits on inference

Historical non-operational rope evidence: Manufacturer-to-manufacturer differences appeared even among ropes grouped by nominal diameter. Because source, construction details, and procurement specifications varied together, the report cannot isolate a causal manufacturer effect or a universal diameter-to-construction relation. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF p. 4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The special hard-laid subset did not show a single constant elastic response over its observed loading path, and the authors reported no well-defined proportional limit. This is a qualitative historical result for that subset. It is not evidence that every natural-fiber rope behaves identically and supplies no modern threshold. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 11 and 13]

Historical non-operational rope evidence: The source fitted empirical relations to its observations, but its own yarn-count discussion acknowledges substantial individual departure around a fitted average relation. The formulas, coefficients, plotted values, calculated values, error value, and every proposed use of those relations are excluded from this corpus. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 9–13]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: The paper's period claim that its formulas could be used safely within a specified construction and size range is not retained as safety guidance. It depended on historical commercial material, period procurement specifications, an incompletely controlled sampling process, and test-system effects that included frequent termination-region failure. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4, 7, and 13]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: Tables of sample records, breaking loads, yarn counts, calculated values, government specification values, plots, equations, coefficients, and raw numerical results have all been removed. The surviving prose cannot be reversed into a breaking-strength estimate, working-load rule, safety factor, tolerance, acceptance threshold, or product ranking. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 8–13]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: The two photographs and three plots are excluded in full, including captions, labels, visual comparisons, and image-derived claims. The image authorship and component rights are not established by the report, and the plots carry operationally hazardous numerical performance content. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 5–6 and 10–12]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: The cited historical government specification and its requirements are not reproduced or adopted. References to purchase orders, a named specification, and period laboratory practice document context only; they are not current procurement, testing, or acceptance rules. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4, 9, and 11]

## Absolute transfer boundary

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: This 1921 source cannot be transferred to modern rope products; 6 mm jute or hemp; rope care, cleaning, hygiene, drying, storage, inspection, or retirement; knots or frictions; working loads or safety factors; body contact; uplines; anchors; bondage; or human suspension. It did not study those materials, practices, configurations, or use cases. [NBS-T198-1921, whole-PDF scope audit, pp. 3–13]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: The historical sample was commercial three-strand regular-lay Manila rope acquired around its study period, chiefly through government purchase-order testing. A shared natural-fiber label, similar diameter, similar appearance, or similar strand count does not bridge the century-scale product, process, condition, and use-case gap. [NBS-T198-1921, PDF pp. 3–4]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: Nothing here approves, selects, sizes, rates, tests, certifies, installs, inspects, retires, or evaluates any rope, knot, upline, anchor, hardpoint, bondage arrangement, body-support system, or human-suspension system. No operational calculator or rule can be generated from this corpus. [NBS-T198-1921, bounded adaptation decision]

Historical non-operational rope evidence boundary: A current safety decision needs current product-specific evidence, current governing requirements, the actual condition and configuration, and appropriately qualified evaluation for the intended domain. T198 can contribute historical vocabulary and lessons about experimental confounding, but it cannot answer a present-day fitness-for-use question. [NBS-T198-1921, bounded adaptation decision]
