# Jacketed HMPE ship-mooring failure: a rights-filtered failure-analysis digest

## Source identity and boundary

This digest covers the Marine Accident Investigation Branch report *Report on the investigation of the failure of a mooring line on board the LNG carrier Zarga while alongside the South Hook Liquefied Natural Gas terminal, Milford Haven resulting in serious injury to an officer on 2 March 2015*. It was issued as Report No. 13/2017 in June 2017. The retained evidence concerns an industrial ship-to-shore mooring system using a jacketed high-modulus polyethylene rope. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 1, 15 / report cover and p. 1]

This single industrial maritime failure does not validate or certify bondage rope or human suspension. The report is a casualty investigation of a particular HMPE line, vessel arrangement, operating history, and maritime load path. Its findings are not a general certification of ropes, a current survey of monitoring technology, or an inspection procedure for another setting. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 15, 98–111 / report pp. 1, 84–97]

The report uses HMPE as a convenient label for high-modulus polyethylene and ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene in its maritime discussion. Its subject line had a long-lay, low-twist HMPE load-bearing core enclosed by a tightly fitted braided jacket. That construction is central to the failure analysis; conclusions about it should not be detached from the material, architecture, or ship-mooring application. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 27–28 / report pp. 13–14]

## What kind of evidence the report provides

MAIB combined accident-site examination, vessel and maintenance records, operating records, internal examination of the failed line, laboratory work, comparison with an unused line, and a review of sector documents. Much of the laboratory, vendor, standards, and industry material in the factual record is separately credited. This digest does not reproduce those external components; it retains only MAIB's Crown-authored synthesis and its description of the investigation's evidentiary structure. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 54–76 / report pp. 40–62] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 98–111 / report pp. 84–97]

The evidence has case-study limits. The investigated line was observed after failure, and some internal evidence required destructive access or specialized laboratory imaging. The report can support a causal account for this industrial failure and identify blind spots in the management system, but it cannot by itself establish population-wide failure rates, universal material behavior, or a generally validated monitoring method. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 59–75 / report pp. 45–61] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 105–107 / report pp. 91–93]

MAIB's final synthesis treated axial-compression fatigue as the predominant mechanism in this case while also recognizing progressive contributions from cyclic loading, bending, twisting, contact, frictional effects, and prior loading history. The final tensile parting event was therefore the endpoint of accumulated internal damage, not evidence that a nominal line specification alone described the line's actual condition. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 98–100 / report pp. 84–86]

## Construction changes what can be seen

The jacket served structural and protective functions. A long-lay, low-twist core depends on its jacket to keep the rope architecture together, and the jacket shields the load-bearing material from some external contact. At the same time, it hides the core from ordinary external viewing. Protection of the core and observability of the core are therefore different properties. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 36–38 / report pp. 22–24] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 101 / report p. 87]

The jacket was not mechanically neutral. MAIB's analysis describes a tightly bound jacket applying radial compression to the core. That confinement restricted relative axial movement among yarns and fibers, made load equalization harder, and promoted local kinking and internal contact. A feature intended to maintain construction and provide external protection could consequently participate in an internal damage pathway. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 100–103 / report pp. 86–89] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 111 / report p. 97]

Low twist also mattered. Components with little helical freedom had less ability to redistribute imposed deformation. Where neighboring elements restrained a component forced into axial compression, the component could buckle sharply instead of flexing gently. Repeated movement at those localized bends could create kink bands and eventual yarn failures hidden inside the jacket. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 38–40 / report pp. 24–26] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 99–101 / report pp. 85–87]

The report also describes HMPE as viscoelastic: stiffness and deformation response depend on loading intensity, duration, cycling, and environment. Those dependencies mean that material identity alone does not determine behavior. Construction, loading history, temperature, and the way the line interacts with the surrounding system all affect the meaning of a condition observation. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 35–36 / report pp. 21–22] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 107 / report p. 93]

## Axial compression inside a rope that is in tension

The central mechanical lesson is that a rope can be under overall tensile load while individual internal components experience axial compression. Unequal component movement, cyclic loading, twisting, or bending can force a yarn or fiber into compression while adjacent material restrains it. The whole-rope load state and the local component load state are not interchangeable descriptions. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 38–40 / report pp. 24–26] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 99 / report p. 85]

Under repeated compression, restrained components can form sharp kinks. These local geometric discontinuities act as sites for repeated flexing and concentrated contact. MAIB linked the investigated pattern of internal kinks and yarn failures to axial-compression fatigue and emphasized that this mechanism can arise from the combination of manufacturing variation, rope design, and operating application. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 99 / report p. 85] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 103, 111 / report pp. 89, 97]

Cyclic loading creates more than a repeated whole-rope tension. It can induce tension-tension fatigue while also making load distribution within a high-modulus synthetic-fiber construction uneven. At portions of a cycle where the overall load is low, individual components may still be compressed or move relative to their neighbors. The report therefore rejects a simple assumption that tensile service excludes compression damage. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 99–100 / report pp. 85–86]

The report's causal picture is interactive. Axial compression can create kinks; kinks can concentrate flexing and contact; a tight jacket can limit redistribution; repeated bending can renew those local interactions; and friction can contribute heat and internal abrasion. Progressive loss of load-bearing capacity can emerge from the sequence even when no external feature uniquely identifies it. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 99–103 / report pp. 85–89]

## Bending, contact, and the complete load path

A ship's mooring line is only one element of a load path. The investigated system included the HMPE main line, a more elastic tail, winch handling, roller fairleads, shipboard routing, the shore connection, and a variable maritime environment. Each element could change line tension, curvature, contact, cyclic response, or where a repeatedly worked section appeared in the path. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 27–31 / report pp. 13–17] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 100–105 / report pp. 86–91]

Bending a loaded rope around fittings produces a local state different from straight, point-to-point tension. Curvature changes how components share load, while surface contact produces friction and can generate heat. Repeated passage of a section through a bend adds flex fatigue and internal abrasion to the axial-compression mechanism. Low-twist, tightly jacketed construction made those combined effects especially relevant in this case. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 100 / report p. 86] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 101–103 / report pp. 87–89]

The elastic tail also formed part of the dynamic system. Changing one component's elasticity can change the time history and cycling experienced by the main line; it does not erase pre-existing internal damage or guarantee that every component shares load uniformly. The report's system framing therefore resists assessing a line only by its nominal material and straight-line capacity. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 28 / report p. 14] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 99–100 / report pp. 85–86]

MAIB concluded that line and fitting compatibility had not been evaluated as a complete system. The installed routing repeatedly bent the line through fittings that were not compatible with the rope construction, while procurement and earlier reviews treated rope and deck equipment too independently. The report explicitly reframed mooring-deck design as a system problem rather than a collection of separately acceptable components. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 31, 104–105 / report pp. 17, 90–91]

This full-system view also changes the meaning of a component specification. A nominal value established in a different test arrangement does not capture cyclic environment, bend history, friction, tail behavior, fitting geometry, or internal construction. Fitness for a maritime load path is an interaction claim, not a material label or isolated certificate. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 102–109 / report pp. 88–95]

## Why external visual inspection was insufficient

The line received repeated external visual examination and was repeatedly described as being in good overall condition. Later internal examination found damage that the intact jacket had hidden. In this construction, an apparently acceptable cover did not establish that the load-bearing core was intact. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 15, 51–53 / report pp. 1, 37–39] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 105–106, 110 / report pp. 91–92, 96]

Several signs of core degradation discussed in the report could be assessed only after opening or removing jacket material. That access changed or destroyed the examined section. Even internal viewing could reveal yarn-scale kinks without directly establishing the condition of molecular-scale fiber structure; specialized imaging was needed for some filament evidence. The report therefore distinguishes cover appearance, core appearance, and filament-scale evidence. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 60–64 / report pp. 46–50] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 106–107 / report pp. 92–93]

Localized stiffness was not a reliable stand-alone answer. A tightly jacketed construction is naturally stiff across its section, and judgments about abnormal stiffness were subjective and dependent on assessor knowledge. Conflicting interpretations of stiffness further weakened its use as a unique indicator of hidden condition. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 105–106 / report pp. 91–92]

As framed in 2017, MAIB found no recognized nondestructive method capable of assessing the overall condition of high-modulus synthetic-fiber ship-mooring ropes. External vision, tactile stiffness, internal destructive examination, and laboratory imaging observed different scales and left different blind spots. This historical finding should not be presented as a claim about every technology available today. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 106 / report p. 92] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 108 / report p. 94]

The operator possessed operating-hours, environmental, and terminal line-load records, yet the information was not integrated meaningfully into a condition assessment. External assessors also did not routinely receive the complete operational history they said they needed. The failure was thus not only a sensor problem; it was also a data-integration and information-flow problem. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 53–55 / report pp. 39–41] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 106 / report p. 92]

## Procurement and information-flow failure

The builder's procurement description emphasized a small set of nominal rope characteristics without supplying the full deck-fitting and working-system context. Rope suppliers did not obtain the omitted information, and the rope manufacturer was not directly consulted about compatibility with the installed fittings. MAIB treated the resulting knowledge gap as shared across builder, supplier, and operator interfaces rather than as a property of the rope alone. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 27, 31 / report pp. 13, 17] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 108, 111 / report pp. 94, 97]

The absence of complete information persisted after installation. Operating history, environment, routing, and load data were fragmented across organizations or not supplied to those assessing the line. Earlier failure reviews also omitted the compatibility between the rope and deck fittings from their scope. A technically relevant fact can exist somewhere in the system and still be unavailable at the decision boundary where it matters. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 53–58 / report pp. 39–44] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF p. 105 / report p. 91]

The report's lesson is epistemic as well as mechanical: a line cannot be evaluated from a procurement label when construction, fitting geometry, routing, cyclic environment, and operating history are missing. Builder, supplier, operator, and assessor each need a common system description before evidence from any one component can be interpreted. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 104–109 / report pp. 90–95]

## Why offshore test evidence did not transfer directly

The report contrasts controlled offshore tether applications with ship-mooring service. Offshore evidence emphasized controlled, relatively direct load paths and application-specific engineering. The shipboard line experienced bends, twists, repeated contact, variable cyclic loading, different environmental exposure, and a load path assembled from several interacting elements. These are different boundary conditions for the same broad material family. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 81–89 / report pp. 67–75] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 108–109 / report pp. 94–95]

MAIB found that ship-mooring procurement had treated newer synthetic ropes too much like direct substitutes for established wire systems and had relied too heavily on test concepts developed for offshore service. A method validated for straight or controlled loading does not automatically characterize a jacketed line undergoing combined bending, twisting, contact, and cycling. Transfer requires matching construction, load path, environment, and failure mechanisms. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 101–102 / report pp. 87–88] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 108–109 / report pp. 94–95]

This is not a finding that HMPE is inherently unsuitable. It is a finding that application suitability cannot be inferred from material identity or an offshore test result alone. The evidentiary mismatch arose because the system in service differed from the system represented by the borrowed method. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 108–109 / report pp. 94–95]

## Uncertainty and appropriate use

The report resolves a causal interpretation for the investigated event, but it also documents incomplete histories, changing external interpretations, and limitations in observing hidden damage. Its conclusions are strongest for the specific combination of jacketed low-twist HMPE construction, cyclic ship-mooring service, repeated bending and contact, and fragmented condition information. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 54–76 / report pp. 40–62] [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 98–111 / report pp. 84–97]

The digest provides no operational thresholds or service-decision rules. It supports a conceptual model: construction affects observability; overall tension can coexist with local compression; contact and cycling interact; external appearance can miss core damage; and evidence transfers only when system boundary conditions match. [MAIB-ZARGA-2017, PDF pp. 99–109 / report pp. 85–95]
