# Historical evidence and limits: Manila-rope fiber color is not serviceability

## Source and study context

Historical source evidence: Genevieve Becker and William D. Appel authored *Evaluation of Manila-Rope Fiber for Color*, Research Paper RP627, in the *Bureau of Standards Journal of Research*, volume 11, December 1933. The article occupies printed pages 811–822 and is identified by DOI `10.6028/jres.011.057`; its closing dateline is Washington, September 16, 1933. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 811–822; NIST-VOLUME-11]

Historical source evidence: Becker is identified as a research associate at the Bureau of Standards for the Cordage Institute, while Appel is identified as chief of the Bureau’s Textile Section. The project arose from a request by the Cordage Institute and a Federal Specifications Board committee to develop a way to evaluate the color of constituent fiber in commercial Manila rope. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 811–812]

Historical source evidence: The paper thanks a group of cordage manufacturers and the Boston Navy Yard for furnishing experimental rope. It also describes a commercial, multi-manufacturer rope set. That is supply context for a laboratory color study, not evidence that the samples represented rope in service, the modern market, or every manufacturing process. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 812 and 815]

Historical source boundary: “Manila rope” in this article means rope made from abaca fiber. The evidence does not concern jute or true hemp, and similarity in natural-fiber appearance does not license transfer among those materials. [NIST-RP627, printed p. 811]

## What the study observed

Historical source evidence: The article distinguishes **constituent fiber color** from **finished-rope surface appearance**. Its analytical target was a prepared fiber specimen derived from rope, not the intact outside surface that a user sees. The authors explicitly say that finished-rope surface color would be a different measurement target. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 812–813]

Historical source evidence: The authors explain that an intact rope-surface result would depend on rope construction, yarn size, strand arrangement, twist, and dirt or dust. Surface appearance therefore combines fiber properties with construction and environmental history; it cannot be treated as a direct reading of the underlying fiber color. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 812–813]

Historical source evidence: The study aimed to characterize fiber color before lubrication rather than the color as it actually appeared in finished rope. This difference in target matters: a laboratory result after preparation is not interchangeable with a visual judgment of an in-use rope’s exterior. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 812 and 818]

Historical source evidence: Lubricant can change apparent color according to the lubricant’s own color, dust accumulated by it, and bleaching or darkening during exposure to light and air. The paper also found that removing lubricant did not necessarily return the fiber to its original response. These observations make lubricant and exposure history confounders, not corrections that reveal an unquestionable intrinsic color. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 818–820]

Historical source evidence: The introduction notes that abaca fiber can be bleached, even though the authors describe bleaching as outside ordinary practice at that time. A visible lightness or darkness can therefore reflect treatment history as well as source fiber, which further blocks a simple appearance-to-quality inference. [NIST-RP627, printed p. 812]

## Serviceability was not tested

Historical source evidence: The authors state directly that their work was **not concerned with the relationship between fiber color and rope serviceability**. The paper developed and compared color-evaluation methods; it did not test whether a rope remained fit for use, retained strength, resisted deterioration, or was safe under load. [NIST-RP627, printed p. 812]

Historical source evidence: The article lists strength and resistance to deterioration influences as matters relevant to serviceability, but it does not measure those outcomes against color in this study. Those examples define what the study omitted; they are not a validated model connecting color to remaining performance. [NIST-RP627, printed p. 812]

Historical source evidence: The authors report an industry opinion that rope made from darker fiber was less serviceable, then mention an unspecified Navy Department indication that some darker rope might perform as well as a Navy comparison rope. No design, sample description, endpoint, result table, or independently auditable citation for those Navy experiments appears in the article. [NIST-RP627, printed p. 812]

Historical source boundary: The dark-rope passage supports only a narrow historical caution: the paper itself did not establish that darkness implies poor serviceability, and the brief Navy remark prevents treating the industry opinion as settled. It does not establish that dark rope is safe, equal, undamaged, or suitable for any present use. [NIST-RP627, printed p. 812]

Historical source boundary: Rope furnished by manufacturers or the Boston Navy Yard remained material for this color study. Supply by those organizations is not Navy qualification, service validation, certification, or evidence that the study’s samples were used in the separate Navy experiments mentioned by the authors. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 812 and 815]

## Absolute non-transfer boundary

Historical source boundary: This source supplies no color threshold, light-to-dark scale, visual ranking, measurement value, or surface-appearance rule. Its findings cannot be used to accept, reject, retire, clean, grade, or load a rope. Color alone cannot establish material identity, internal condition, strength, remaining life, contamination, hygiene, or serviceability. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 811–822]

Historical source boundary: Nothing here evaluates or approves jute, hemp, rope cleaning, hygiene, drying, storage, retirement, working loads, body contact, knots, frictions, uplines, anchors, hardpoints, bondage, restraint, body lowering, or human suspension. No current inspection, procurement, maintenance, or safety recommendation may be derived from this historical color study. [NIST-RP627, printed pp. 811–822]
