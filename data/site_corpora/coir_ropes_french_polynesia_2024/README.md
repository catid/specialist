# Coir ropes in French Polynesian pearl farming (2024)

This package is a concise, manually rewritten evidence-and-limitations digest of the 2024 article *Coir ropes as a low-tech circular alternative to synthetic ropes in French Polynesia pearl farming*. It preserves the useful distinction between individual coir fibers and constructed ropes while preventing laboratory, retting, and life-cycle results from becoming unsupported safety or product advice.

The sole content-evidence body is the official Ifremer-hosted version-of-record PDF:

`https://archimer.ifremer.fr/doc/00914/102595/113967.pdf`

DOI CSL metadata retrieved through `https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clcb.2024.100111` supplies an independent identity and license cross-check. Neither source body is redistributed.

## Retained scope

`CORPUS.md` retains only attributed evidence and limitations concerning:

- the authors, 2024 publication identity, DOI, and CC BY 4.0 license;
- the French Polynesian pearl-farming study context;
- individual coir fibers versus machine-twisted and hand-braided finished ropes;
- dry versus water-saturated laboratory states;
- construction quality, rope-level wet retention, and the limit of predicting rope behavior from fibers;
- the study-specific absence of a significant retting effect;
- the lack of in-situ aging evidence;
- the production-and-transport-only life-cycle boundary;
- the absence of a funding statement, without inferring absence of funding;
- Polyacht material supply, the employee-author relationship, and the authors’ no-influence statement; and
- the data-on-request statement.

## Excluded scope

The package excludes all experimental and life-cycle values, equations, sample labels, product comparisons, ratings, selections, recommendations, recipes, retting and soaking schedules, extraction and preparation procedures, instruments, software workflows, figures, tables, plots, photographs, microscopy, diagrams, publisher branding, acknowledgements, author-role details, reference entries, and cited-source bodies.

Nothing is transferred to jute, hemp, hygiene, cleaning, care, drying, storage, retirement, body contact, knots, frictions, working loads, uplines, anchors, hardpoints, bondage, restraint, body lowering, or human suspension.
