## Retained evidence and its limits

The paper studies coir rope in a French Polynesian pearl-farming context. It distinguishes tests on individual coir fibers from tests on finished machine-twisted and hand-braided ropes, and it distinguishes dry from water-saturated laboratory states.

Within this study, individual-fiber tensile strength did not show a significant saturation effect, while the assembled ropes—especially the tested machine-twisted constructions—showed rope-level change. Braiding homogeneity and construction quality affected stress distribution and wet retention, and the two hand-braided specimens did not behave identically. The retained lesson is a prediction boundary: isolated-fiber behavior, fiber length, and a coarse construction label cannot alone predict a finished rope.

The authors’ inter-fiber-sliding explanation is retained only as their interpretation. No formula, mechanism guarantee, or transfer to another rope is made.

The retting finding is restricted to the tested batches and conditions: the measured rope properties showed no distinctive trend or significant change attributable to retting. All media, times, preparation steps, and process instructions are excluded. The result is not converted into a production recommendation.

The authors explicitly identify the absence of in-situ aging under the pearl-farming service environment. Laboratory testing did not resolve long-term exposure, combined stressors, or service life. The life-cycle model also covered production and transport only, not use or end-of-life, and the authors caution about generalized data for a remote archipelago. No impact ranking or product comparison is retained.
