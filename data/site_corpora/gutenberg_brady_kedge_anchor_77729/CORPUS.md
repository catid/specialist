# A historical ropework lexicon and teaching map from Brady (1852)

## Source boundary

Historical ropework evidence: William N. Brady’s *The kedge-anchor: or, Young sailors’ assistant* identifies itself as the sixth edition, published in New York by the author in 1852, with an earlier 1847 copyright entry. Its title page places knotting, splicing, blocks, purchases, running rigging, and cordage tables inside a much larger seamanship manual. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 16–51]

Historical ropework evidence: Brady presents the book as an introductory aid for young naval and merchant sailors and describes his own standpoint as practical marlinespike experience. That declared audience explains the unusually granular progression from rope-yarn to compound ropework; it does not make the book a modern standard or current safety authority. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 63–82]

Historical ropework boundary: This corpus retains a quotation-light historical vocabulary and the book’s decomposition of ropework into teachable families. It intentionally does not reproduce a complete tying, splicing, rigging, loading, inspection, or equipment procedure. Numerous entries depend explicitly on plates, and the transcription’s illustration blocks carry information that cannot be recovered safely from prose alone. ## Teaching architecture

Historical ropework evidence: Part I begins below the level of a finished rope: first a rope-yarn is joined, then yarns are reorganized into a fox, Spanish fox, or knittle. Only after those components does the sequence introduce familiar knots, hitches, and bends. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 835–947]

Historical ropework evidence: The contents expose the author’s instructional map. Elementary knots and bends lead to splices and eyes; those lead to worming, serving, and seizing; strand-end structures lead to sennit, gaskets, mats, and grommets; block anatomy then precedes specialized block forms. This is a component-to-family progression, not a random glossary. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 191–261]

Historical ropework evidence: Several lessons reuse an earlier structure as a transformation primitive. A later form may begin from an earlier splice, combine walling with crowning, or add parallel material by “following the lead.” The reusable lesson is the decomposition itself: name the base form, identify what is added or rearranged, and distinguish the derived form by that change. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1076–1137; 1271–1306; 1342–1347]

Historical ropework boundary: The source sometimes says that hands-on practice communicates a form better than verbal explanation. Together with constant references to plates, that warning means these descriptions are evidence for historical vocabulary and pedagogy, not standalone instructions that a learner should execute from this corpus. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 984–1035]

## Yarn-level and small-cordage vocabulary

Historical ropework evidence: A **fox** is Brady’s name for a small cordage component made by twisting several rope-yarns together. The source associates foxes with later structures such as gaskets, mats, plaits, and temporary seizings, so a fox functions as an intermediate unit between yarn and finished flatwork or binding. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 852–858]

Historical ropework evidence: A **Spanish fox** is structurally different from an ordinary fox: the source starts with a single rope-yarn, untwists it, and retwists it in the contrary direction. The historical name therefore describes a reversal transformation applied to one yarn, not merely a different use for an ordinary fox. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 861–865]

Historical ropework evidence: A **knittle** is a small group of rope-yarns laid together by hand against the yarns’ existing twist. Brady later repeats that construction relationship while discussing pointing, which shows that “knittle” is a component name used across lessons rather than a one-off exercise title. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 868–873; 1417–1438]

Historical ropework evidence: **Sennit** is plaited rope-yarn work. In the source’s hierarchy, rope-yarns can become foxes or knittles, and yarn-level components can then be plaited or interwoven into gaskets and mats. Modern sources may spell the word *sennet*; this transcription uses both “sennit” in headings and “sennet” in an illustration caption. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1366–1414; 1533–1538]

## Knots, hitches, bends, and historical word use

Historical ropework evidence: Brady groups overhand and figure-eight knots, half-hitches, the reef or square knot, bowline variants, timber hitch, fisherman’s and rolling bends, Carrick bend, cat’s-paw, sheet or becket bend, Black-wall hitch, and rolling hitch as adjacent elementary lessons. The grouping is useful historical taxonomy, but the corpus omits their plate-dependent tying sequences and claimed applications. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 876–996; 1038–1044]

Historical ropework evidence: **Sheet bend** and **becket bend** are presented as alternate names for the same lesson. The name changes with whether the receiving opening is described as another rope’s bight or a block’s becket, while the plate caption shortens the label to “Sheet Bend.” [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 975–981; 998–1035]

Historical ropework evidence: The later sea-term glossary demonstrates polysemy rather than a strict modern knot taxonomy. It calls a **bend** a kind of knot or a seizing and uses the verb **to bend** for making fast; it defines a **bight** both as any rope portion between its ends and as a collar or eye formed by rope. These are source-bounded historical usages, not universal definitions. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 11840–11857]

Historical ropework evidence: The glossary reduces **to hitch** to making fast, **to seize** to making fast or binding, and **to splice** to joining ropes through their strands. Those terse verb definitions sit beside the much more decomposed Part I families and help separate the historical action words from the named finished forms. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 12240–12240; 12398–12423]

## Splice and eye families

Historical ropework evidence: A **short splice** and a **long splice** are taught as different strand-path strategies. The short form interleaves the opened strands from two rope ends near their meeting point. The long form opens a greater length and replaces the path vacated by one strand with an opposing strand before the separated joins are finished. No tuck counts, dimensions, or execution sequence are retained here. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1076–1114]

Historical ropework evidence: An **eye splice** works the opened end strands back into the standing part so that the rope forms an eye. A **cut splice** is described instead as producing a collar in the bight by bringing two rope parts together through splice-like strand work. These are historical form distinctions; neither paragraph supplies a load-bearing recipe. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1117–1137]

Historical ropework evidence: A **Flemish eye** is built at yarn level after the end strands have been opened and their yarns separated. An **artificial eye** instead removes one strand temporarily, forms the eye from the remaining rope structure, and relays the removed strand through the vacant strand path. The contrast is between yarn redistribution and strand-path reconstruction. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1140–1160]

Historical ropework evidence: A **grommet** is a closed ring made from a single unlaid strand that follows the rope’s lay around the ring until the strand forms the complete multi-part circumference; Brady relates its closure to a long splice. This is a structural definition only, with the plate and working directions omitted. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1446–1451]

## Worming, parceling, serving, and seizing

Historical ropework evidence: Brady distinguishes three successive surface operations. **Worming** fills the valleys between a rope’s strands to make the exterior rounder; **parceling** adds a wrapping layer; **serving** adds the outer wound binding, called the **service** as a noun. The corpus retains that layered model but not the source’s materials, tools, widths, directions, or unsupported strength claim. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1163–1184]

Historical ropework evidence: In the glossary, **to serve** means winding material around a cable or rope to oppose chafe, whereas **to seize** means binding or making fast. This lexical contrast matters: serving is a covering operation, while seizing joins or constrains parts. It should not be converted into a present-day prescription about materials or suitability. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 12398–12401]

Historical ropework evidence: Brady’s seizing lesson separates initial turns, **riding turns**, and **cross-turns**. It distinguishes an **end seizing** by its position at an end, a **middle** or **quarter seizing** by placement on two parts below an end, and a **throat seizing** by the absence of the final crossing described for the other form. Numerical turn counts and tightening directions are omitted. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1187–1209]

Historical ropework evidence: **Snaking** is the source’s name for an alternating under-and-over securing path across the lower and upper turns of a seizing when the seizing surrounds a single rope part and therefore cannot receive ordinary cross-turns between two parts. This is preserved as a historical construction distinction, not a field procedure. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1440–1443]

## Strand-end composition and flatwork

Historical ropework evidence: **Walling** and **crowning** are taught as separate strand-end operations and then combined. “Double” forms are generated by following the existing lead, so the vocabulary records composition: wall, crown, wall-and-crown, and derived doubled arrangements are related structures rather than unrelated knot names. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1271–1306]

Historical ropework evidence: A **sea gasket** is plaited from foxes, begins with an eye, and is tapered by withdrawing components as the work progresses. A **harbor gasket**, also called **French sennit** in the heading, differs in the source by interweaving the outside foxes over and under toward the middle instead of repeatedly bringing the outside component across to the middle. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1371–1384; 1408–1414]

Historical ropework evidence: A **panch or wrought mat** is assembled from paired fox elements and finished with a selvedged boundary; the source then adds short strand fibres as thrums. The illustration caption spells the same family “punch-mat,” so *panch*, *punch*, and *wrought mat* must remain linked to this particular transcription rather than silently normalized. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1387–1405; 1533–1538]

Historical ropework evidence: A **sword mat** is woven by using a flat tool called a sword to open alternating sets of stretched elements so that a separate warp can cross them. The useful historical concept is shed alternation in rope flatwork; all tool dimensions, hand sequence, and figure-dependent execution have been removed. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1466–1478]

## Block anatomy and contrasting block forms

Historical ropework evidence: Brady decomposes a sheaved block into **shell**, **sheave**, and **pin**. The shell encloses the moving part and carries external scores for a strap; the sheave is a grooved wheel inside the shell; the pin passes through shell and sheave and supplies the sheave’s axis. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1488–1508]

Historical ropework evidence: The text uses **score** and **groove** for different receiving surfaces: scores on the shell admit the external strap, while the circumferential groove on the sheave receives the moving rope. That distinction is more informative than treating every channel in a block as the same feature. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1496–1508]

Historical ropework evidence: The basic block names derive from sheave count: single, double, treble, and four-fold. The source’s later descriptions treat the added sheaves as modifications of the same shell–sheave–pin architecture. This is naming anatomy, not a mechanical-advantage or loading rule. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1491–1522]

Historical ropework evidence: A **fiddle block** is compared to two vertically arranged single blocks, with the upper portion larger so its rope path clears the lower one. A **shoe block** also stacks two sheaves but turns the upper and lower sheave directions relative to one another. A **sister block** places two sheave holes one above the other and shapes the shell and scores to sit against and be seized to a shroud. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1571–1597]

Historical ropework evidence: Brady catalogs several grooved, perforated forms next to sheaved blocks even though they do not share the ordinary rotating-sheave anatomy. A **dead-eye** is an externally grooved disk with several lanyard holes; a **bull’s-eye** is a thick thimble-like form with a central opening and outside groove; a **heart** has a larger central opening and an outside groove for a surrounding rope. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1600–1623]

Historical ropework evidence: A **shoulder block** adds a projecting shoulder intended to keep the reeved rope from entering the gap beside the block. A **tail block** has a flexible ropework tail rather than only a closed strap. A **snatch block** has an opening controlled by a clasp so that a rope bight can be placed into the block without reeving an end through the entire system. [BRADY-77729-TEXT, lines 1525–1531; 1672–1682; 1717–1728]
