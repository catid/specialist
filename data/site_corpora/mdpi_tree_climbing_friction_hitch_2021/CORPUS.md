## Limitations first

This was a preliminary laboratory study of two particular synthetic arborist rope systems paired with two friction hitches. It was not a field trial and did not place a person on the system. It did not test recreational rope bondage, human suspension, natural-fiber rope, rescue loads, anchors, harnesses, bodies, nerves, circulation, consent, or emergency release. Nothing in the study certifies a hitch, rope, combination, or complete life-support system. [MDPI-PDF: Abstract; §2 Materials and Methods; §4 Conclusions]

The 36 runs were slow pulls on semi-static rope in a traction bench with a short 250 mm machine stroke. The apparatus paused at fixed displacement points. This is a quasi-static, constrained bench condition. There were no dynamic drops, falls, catches, shock loads, repeated work cycles, oscillating bodies, live climbers, or cyclic hysteresis trials. The authors explicitly called for future dynamic falling-body and cyclic-loading experiments. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods; §3 Results and Discussion]

The authors adapted a standardized fall-protection-rope test method rather than following it unchanged. They assumed their modifications did not materially alter results, but this experiment did not establish equivalence between the modified setup and the unmodified method. The assembly also included terminal and connecting components, so the results describe the tested bench system, not the isolated intrinsic strength of a hitch or rope. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

The machine stopped after its programmed stroke. Reported loads are loads observed within that stroke, not universal breaking loads. Reported maxima are neither working-load limits nor safety factors. Statistical significance inside these data does not produce a safe rating, recommended hitch, field clearance, or prediction for other equipment. [MDPI-PDF: Abstract; §2 Materials and Methods; §3 Results and Discussion]

Only the named synthetic specimens, diameters, hitch cord, tie patterns, assembly, laboratory environment, pull history, and measurement method were observed. Different material, construction, age, wear, contamination, moisture, temperature, UV history, tying variation, dressing, loading rate, geometry, hardware, or repeated use can change behavior. The article discusses several such sources of variability and says more data are needed before larger-scale application. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

## Research question and stated purpose

The study examined how a work-rope and friction-hitch system behaved under high slow-pull stress. The authors wanted to observe load, hitch movement, rope deformation, and heat while comparing two rope models and two hitch types. They described the work as preliminary because scientific evidence for these combinations was limited. [MDPI-PDF: Abstract; §1 Introduction]

The authors expressly said the goal was **not** to define the best knot. Their purpose was to begin a series of studies about interaction between hitches and ropes and to provide comparative information for future research. Any claim that the experiment selected a universally superior hitch contradicts its stated purpose. [MDPI-PDF: §1 Introduction]

The title uses *tensile strength*, but the method primarily observed the response of assembled rope-and-hitch treatments during a bounded stroke. The article reported system load, slip, diameter change, and temperature rather than a general material strength value that can be transferred to another rope or hitch. [MDPI-PDF: Abstract; §2 Materials and Methods]

## Exact experimental factors

The two tested work-rope specimens were:

- **System A:** the article's Axis model, an 11 mm synthetic rope listed as polyester/polyamide;
- **System X:** the article's XTC 16 model, a synthetic polyamide rope listed as 13 mm in the specimen table. [MDPI-PDF: Table 1]

Both systems used an 8 mm polyester/Technora hitch cord. Manufacturer identities and declared catalog breaking loads are omitted because they are not experimental outcomes and could be mistaken for ratings of the tested assembly. The results narrative later calls the larger rope 12.7 mm, whereas the specimen table rounds or lists it as 13 mm; this digest preserves that source discrepancy rather than silently resolving it. [MDPI-PDF: Table 1; §3 Results and Discussion]

The hitch factor had two levels: **Prusik** (`P`) and **Valdotain tresse** (`T`). Crossing two ropes with two hitches produced four treatments:

- `AP`: Axis with Prusik;
- `AT`: Axis with Valdotain tresse;
- `XP`: XTC 16 with Prusik;
- `XT`: XTC 16 with Valdotain tresse. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

The labels are experimental treatment identifiers. They do not specify how to tie, dress, set, connect, or use either hitch. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

## Replication structure

There were 36 traction tests: four treatments with nine runs per treatment. The authors describe three pieces of each rope and three cords for each rope/hitch combination, with three traction tests carried out on each set of sections and cords. Thus, “nine replicates” should be read together with the reported reuse or repeated-test structure; it is not described simply as nine independently sourced, fresh, complete systems. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

The factorial identity is straightforward: two work ropes × two hitches × nine runs equals 36 tests. The relatively balanced design supports comparison among the four treatments inside this apparatus. It does not expand the number of rope models, material families, hitches, operators, environments, or loading modes represented. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

## Bench protocol without an operational recipe

Each run used a traction machine with a 250 mm available stroke. The study recorded an initial state and five later positions separated by 50 mm of machine travel. The machine was stopped at each observation point. This digest retains displacement and sampling cadence because they define the evidentiary window, but omits terminal-knot construction, preloading procedure, connector arrangement, and equipment brands so the paper is not repurposed as a climbing or suspension recipe. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

At the displacement points, researchers measured:

- applied tensile load on the assembled system, `C`, in kilonewtons;
- friction-hitch slip along the work rope, `S`, in centimeters;
- change in work-rope diameter, `Δf`, in millimeters. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

They also measured temperature near the hitch at the beginning and end of a run with thermal imaging. Ambient temperature and humidity were recorded before testing. Temperature was therefore an endpoint comparison, not a continuous measurement of a rapid thermal peak. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]

The experiment did not continuously measure internal fiber temperature, melt onset, body motion, fall distance, impact force, energy absorption, recovery between cycles, or long-term wear. A small endpoint temperature change under slow pull cannot establish that heating is harmless under a fast slide or catch. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods; §3 Results and Discussion]

## Statistical plan

The authors checked distributional assumptions and variance homogeneity before their main comparisons. Ambient conditions and start-to-end rope temperature were assessed with one-factor analysis of variance. The main pulling outcomes were analyzed with factorial multivariate analysis of variance using rope (`A` or `X`) and hitch (`P` or `T`) as factors and load, slip, and diameter change as responses. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Statistical Analysis]

Post-hoc treatment comparisons used a family-wise 95% confidence level with an analysis threshold of `p = 0.05`. Regression models examined load as a function of slip and diameter change, and diameter change as a function of slip. These models describe covariation in the observed bench data; they are not field-load calculators. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Statistical Analysis]

The statistical unit and the physical specimen structure must be kept visible together. Multiple displacement observations came from each pull, and repeated pulls were described across a limited set of rope pieces and cords. A large number of recorded points does not create the material diversity of a large independent equipment sample. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods; §2 Statistical Analysis]

## Observed system-level extrema

Across all recorded observations, the abstract reports an observed maximum load of 18.7 kN, maximum hitch slip of 9.6 cm, and maximum diameter change of 3 mm. These are extrema from this apparatus and test window. They must not be relabeled as minimum breaking strengths, safe working loads, rescue ratings, user-weight limits, or acceptance criteria. [MDPI-PDF: Abstract]

The mean load trends at the end of the stroke were reported as approximately 11.9 kN for `AP`, 15.1 kN for `XP`, 9.3 kN for `AT`, and 8.6 kN for `XT`. The individual 18.7 kN maximum and these treatment means describe different summaries; neither is a rating. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

The Valdotain-tresse treatments showed more movement by the final point: about 7.17 cm for `AT` and 8.97 cm for `XT`. `AP` was reported at about 4.73 cm, roughly half the larger values. For diameter change at the final point, `AT` had the smallest reported mean reduction, about 1.72 mm, while `XP` had the largest, about 3 mm. These are treatment observations under the same bounded stroke, not tolerances for field use. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

## Hitch-related relationships

In the factorial analysis, hitch type had a statistically significant effect on load and on slip, with reported `p < 0.001` for each. Within these systems and this stroke, the Prusik treatments developed greater grip, produced higher machine load, and slipped less than the Valdotain-tresse treatments. The authors interpreted this pattern as greater friction from the Prusik. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion; Table 3]

That comparative statement does not mean “more grip is safer” or “less slip is better” in every system. Greater grip co-occurred with greater load and more diameter reduction in parts of this experiment. A hitch can change several coupled outcomes at once, and this study did not define an optimal balance for a person-support application. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

The effect emerged as the short pull progressed; the four treatments were not clearly separated at the earliest observation. The relationship therefore belongs to the tested displacement history, rather than being a single context-free property attached to a hitch name. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

## Rope-related relationships

Rope type had a statistically significant effect on diameter change, reported at `p < 0.001`. The larger XTC 16 specimen showed greater diameter deformation than the Axis specimen, regardless of hitch in the authors' summary. They suggested that material, braid, and diameter might contribute, but the two-rope experiment cannot isolate which of those correlated specimen differences caused the effect. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion; Table 3]

Because each rope model bundles several properties—nominal diameter, material description, construction, and model-specific geometry—the rope factor is not a clean test of diameter alone or polymer alone. “Rope type mattered for deformation” is supported; “this polymer or diameter always causes the difference” is not. [MDPI-PDF: Table 1; §3 Results and Discussion]

The paper's broader discussion mentions environmental deterioration, material variation, braid geometry, and nonlinear time-dependent synthetic-rope behavior, but those issues were not experimentally crossed in the four treatments. Literature discussion is not evidence that this bench dataset measured those factors. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

## Regression relationships

Separate regressions reported adjusted coefficients of determination of about `0.445` for load versus diameter reduction, `0.418` for load versus hitch slip, and `0.378` for diameter reduction versus slip. A combined model using slip and diameter reduction to describe load had adjusted `R² = 0.535`. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion; Tables 4–5]

The slip relationships included nonlinear terms, so the coefficients should not be reduced to a universal straight-line conversion. The adjusted `R²` values indicate partial explanation within the observed data; substantial variation remained. They do not validate extrapolation beyond the stroke, predict failure, or establish causality among load, slip, and deformation. [MDPI-PDF: Tables 4–5]

The authors summarized the coupled pattern as follows: more hitch friction corresponded to greater load and less slip, while less friction corresponded to lower load and more slip; diameter deformation was associated more strongly with rope type. This is the defensible comparative lesson when it remains attached to the exact specimens and quasi-static method. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

## Temperature observations

The reported start-to-end temperature differences near the hitch were approximately 6.2 °C (`AP`), 6.8 °C (`AT`), 6.7 °C (`XP`), and 3.1 °C (`XT`). The one-factor analysis did not find a significant difference among treatments. Ambient temperature and humidity also did not show a significant treatment effect in the authors' analysis. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]

“No significant difference among these four endpoint changes” is not the same as “no heating occurred” or “temperature is unimportant.” All four treatments had a measured change, the method sampled only the beginning and end, and there was no dynamic slide, fall, or repeated cycle. The paper itself distinguishes its static data from dynamic events and calls for further testing. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods; §3 Results and Discussion]

## What the experiment supports

The study supports these limited conclusions:

1. A balanced `2 × 2` slow-pull bench experiment can reveal coupled differences in load, hitch slip, rope deformation, and endpoint temperature among specified rope/hitch treatments. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods]
2. In these treatments, hitch type was associated primarily with load and slip, while rope type was associated primarily with diameter deformation. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]
3. The Prusik treatments gripped more, carried higher observed machine load, and moved less than the Valdotain-tresse treatments over the short stroke. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]
4. The XTC 16 specimen deformed more in diameter than the Axis specimen in this experiment. [MDPI-PDF: §3 Results and Discussion]
5. Load, slip, and diameter deformation were related but none of the reported regression models fully explained the other outcomes. [MDPI-PDF: Tables 4–5]
6. The preliminary comparison raised research questions; it did not identify a best knot. [MDPI-PDF: §1 Introduction; §4 Conclusions]

## What the experiment does not support

The study does not support any of the following:

- a breaking-strength value for every Prusik, Valdotain tresse, Axis, XTC 16, or hitch cord;
- a safe working load, user-weight limit, safety factor, retirement threshold, or inspection rule;
- a recommendation to choose the hitch that showed more grip, less slip, or higher load;
- a field claim about falls, dynamic catches, repeated cycles, cyclic hysteresis, shock loading, rescue, or swinging bodies;
- transfer to a different rope, cord, diameter, material, braid, tie, dressing, connector, anchor, or loading geometry;
- transfer from synthetic arborist equipment to natural-fiber rope;
- transfer from occupational tree-climbing equipment to rope bondage, shibari, kinbaku, recreational suspension, or any other human-suspension system;
- a claim about anatomy, circulation, nerve safety, skin pressure, consent, emergency release, or medical response;
- certification, standards compliance, legal approval, manufacturer endorsement, or suitability for life support.

Those exclusions follow from the studied system and method, not from pessimism about the data. A well-executed narrow experiment can answer a narrow comparative question while remaining unsuitable for a different load case. [MDPI-PDF: §2 Materials and Methods; §3 Results and Discussion; §4 Conclusions]

## Interpretation discipline

When using this paper, keep five labels attached to every result: **synthetic arborist specimens**, **two ropes**, **two hitches**, **slow semi-static pull**, and **250 mm bench stroke**. If any label is dropped, the result is easier to overgeneralize.

Keep descriptive values separate from design values. An observed load is not a rating; measured slip is not an allowed limit; diameter reduction is not a discard threshold; and a nonsignificant temperature comparison is not evidence against dynamic heating.

Keep factor effects separate from causes. The hitch comparison changed load and slip, and the rope comparison changed diameter deformation, but each named specimen carries multiple material and construction properties. The experiment did not isolate every mechanism.

Keep significance separate from portability. A small `p` value addresses compatibility with a statistical null model in these data. It does not enlarge the sample to other equipment or certify a field decision.

Keep preliminary purpose separate from recommendation. The authors sought relationships and future research directions, not a best-knot ranking. This adaptation goes further by refusing to turn the observed combinations into instructions or safety advice.
