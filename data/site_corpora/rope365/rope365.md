# Rope365: a floor-rope learning, safety, technique, and practice guide

Retrieved 2026-07-16. This is an original, condensed guide to the substantive public text on Rope365. It is not a mirror of the site and not medical advice. It preserves the source's important distinctions, cautions, checks, and exercises while removing menus, comments, credits, repeated resource lists, sales details, announcements, and unfinished image placeholders. A named tie is never a guarantee of safety: construction, placement, tension, duration, body position, health, and the people involved all matter.

Rope bondage can cause nerve injury, loss of circulation, falls, breathing obstruction, joint injury, burns, and death. Keep a practiced cutting tool immediately reachable, agree on verbal and non-verbal stop signals, remain with a bound partner, and favor positions and techniques that can be released promptly. The source includes some high-risk explorations to explain risk; their presence here is not a recommendation to attempt them.

## Learning, consent, and a shared safety system

### Learn, practice, explore

Source: https://rope365.com/learning/

Rope365 treats ties as combinations of a few building blocks rather than fixed pictures to reproduce. A useful progression is: learn sound technique from capable teachers and multiple media; repeat it until handling becomes automatic; then explore deliberately and reflect on what the participants learned. In-person teaching can convey tension, touch, body response, and other details that text or a finished photograph cannot. Practice may be partnered, self-tied, done on a dummy, rehearsed slowly in “lab mode,” or visualized, but repeating a poor or hazardous method merely engrains it.

The curriculum is floorwork. Its four broad phases move from foundational knots and classic ties, through body positions, then technique and creativity, and finally historically informed or higher-intensity subjects. It does not teach suspension. Skip anything outside the participants’ interests or risk profiles, revisit fundamentals, and judge progress by control and understanding rather than how many patterns are memorized. Online images show outcomes without necessarily showing negotiation, construction, adaptation, or the exits that made them possible; bodies of every size, age, ethnicity, gender, and ability require their own solutions.

### Consent and communication before, during, and after rope

Source: https://rope365.com/communication/

Consent depends on desire, adequate information, a real ability to decline, and continuing respect for boundaries. For a new partner, meet publicly or among trusted people, consider references and a safety call, and discuss how each person handles difficult situations. Self-tying still requires an internal negotiation and risk check; nearby people also need to know what is happening and what to do in an emergency.

Keep language simple, especially when somebody is nervous, overwhelmed, intoxicated, aroused, or under intense sensation. Power differences—including teacher/student, financial, reputational, and D/s relationships—can make an apparent yes less reliable. Before tying, cover:

- relevant injuries, joint instability or hypermobility, allergies, cardiovascular or breathing conditions, current soreness, medications, and how the person feels that day;
- desired and unwanted body areas, positions, touch, sexual contact, fluids, marks, and dynamics;
- each person's experience, uncertainties, expectations, and what enjoyable distress versus an actual problem looks like;
- safewords and non-verbal signals, including what to use if speech or breathing becomes difficult;
- aftercare, follow-up, safer-sex and hygiene expectations, and the plan for stopping.

An affirmative allowlist is clearer than “anything except…”. Extreme blanket permission is not meaningful without testing edge cases. Consent may be withdrawn at any time, and something welcome in theory may feel wrong once it begins. Monitor words, breath, movement, skin, motor function, and changes in affect. Hormones and altered mental states can invite boundary expansion that is regretted later, so do not treat mid-scene enthusiasm as automatic authorization for a materially different activity. Preserve the relationship, not the planned scene.

After the rope comes off, ask what is needed now rather than assuming. Water, food, warmth, affection, quiet, stretching, or solitude may each be right; the person who tied may also need care. Feedback can take time, so reopen the conversation in the following days.

### Core risk model and emergency preparation

Source: https://rope365.com/safety/

Safety is a continuing practice, not a product. Carry safety shears or a purpose-built rescue hook where they remain reachable in the actual position, train on expendable rope, and know that ordinary knives and household scissors can add puncture or control hazards. A blunt marlinspike can help loosen a jammed knot but cannot replace a cutter. Before starting, imagine specific failures: a faint, a fall, a hand injury, panic, a jammed tie, equipment failure, or loss of speech.

Nerve compression is among rope's most consequential common injuries and can be painless. Sensory injury may leave numb skin; motor injury can produce weakness such as poor grip or wrist drop. Pressure can come directly from rope or a knot, indirectly from a hard floor or swelling, and can accumulate across repeated sessions. Use broad, even, appropriately loose wraps; avoid joints and known superficial nerve sites; keep bulky knots away from vulnerable areas; and repeatedly check movement and sensation. Persistent numbness or weakness needs medical assessment.

High-consequence hazards include breathing obstruction, positional asphyxia, neck compression, falls, and ambition that exceeds skill or equipment. Avoid fixed pressure on the front or sides of the neck. Chest compression, gags, forward folds, and backbends may also impair breathing. Never leave a bound person alone. Self-tying that could obstruct breathing needs an immediately present spotter; remote monitoring cannot release a choke in time. Tie standing only with close support or a sound anchor, clear trip hazards, prevent rolling from beds or tipping furniture, hydrate and eat, and do not lock knees.

Circulation restriction more often affects an entire limb distal to the pressure and may resolve with pins and needles once pressure is removed; nerve symptoms may be patchier or include motor weakness, but the two can overlap. Color varies too much between skin tones to be the sole check. Grey coloration, continuing numbness, weakness, or unusual pain calls for prompt release. Rope marks from even pressure differ from burns caused by fast dragging; pull gently or shield skin with a hand. Discuss visible marks in advance, especially on the face, neck, and forearms.

### Vocabulary and naming distinctions

Source: https://rope365.com/vocab/

Terminology varies by era and community. Rope365 uses **bight** for a U-shaped fold, usually at the middle; **tail** for unused rope away from the bight; **loop** for rope crossing itself into a circle; and **overhand knot** for the elementary stopper. A **hitch** attaches rope to rope or another object. A **friction** gains stability from rope contact and usually needs tension. A **cinch** passes between columns or wraps to keep them separated and reduce rolling.

**Shibari** derives from Japanese “to tie”; **kinbaku** means tight binding. In English both often refer to erotic Japanese rope bondage. **Floorwork/newaza** stays supported by a floor or similar surface; **partial suspension** leaves some body contact with the floor; **suspension/tsuri** lifts the body from an overhead structure. A **predicament** leaves agency only between difficult outcomes. A **pillar/hashira tie** attaches a person to a vertical post.

Roles include top or person doing the tying, bottom or person receiving it, switch, self-tyer, and rigger. “Nawashi,” “bakushi,” and “kinbakushi” are honorific/proficiency terms rather than synonyms everyone must adopt.

Common tie names describe positions, not one mandatory pattern. A **box tie/gote/takate-kote/TK** generally puts bent arms behind the back. **Strappado/armbinder** usually keeps arms parallel behind the body. A **reverse prayer** puts palms together behind the back. A **demon/tengu** puts wrists near the shoulders with elbows down; related “T-Rex” and “angel/tenshi” labels describe different elbow directions. A **butterfly** brings soles together. A **shrimp/ebi** folds the torso toward ankles or legs. **Hishi** describes diamond forms, **karada** is often used for an open-diamond body harness, and **kikkō** describes hexagonal “turtle-shell” forms.

Source: https://rope365.com/naming-things-and-progress-update/

The same topology may acquire different names in sailing, climbing, macramé, and bondage. Rope365 distinguishes a static **crossing hitch**, meant to secure crossing lines, from a **Munter/Italian hitch**, meant to add controllable friction to a moving belay line. “Working end” and “standing end” are especially confusing when tying from a folded rope, so “tail” is clearer here. Lark's head and cow hitch name closely related forms assembled differently; “chain sinnet” is more precise than the colloquial daisy chain. The guide retains widely understood bondage terms but describes function whenever a name could mislead.

## Foundational rope handling: Days 1–14

### A non-tightening single-column cuff

Source: https://rope365.com/single-column-tie/

A single-column tie anchors around one “column”—a wrist, ankle, thigh, torso, or post—without tightening when its tail is loaded. Rope365 begins with the Somerville bowline because it works with many materials, can be formed from the bight without pulling the entire rope through, allows one or several pressure-spreading wraps, and leaves the bight accessible for release. Under heavy load it can compact and become hard to untie; a cutter remains the emergency exit. Pulling the tail through the bight locks the normally two-ended release option.

The finished cuff should have flat, equally tensioned wraps, roughly a two-finger gap appropriate to the body and context, no accidental twists, and a compact knot that does not deform when bight or tail is pulled. Build fluency by varying wrap count and bight length, tying behind the back, one-handed, non-dominant-handed, on a vertical post, or with eyes closed—without sacrificing inspection. The person in rope can test comfort, small movement, and escapability rather than remaining passive.

### Friction, pulling rather than pushing, and improvisation

Source: https://rope365.com/frictions/

Rope-on-rope contact can stabilize a tie without a hard knot. A full turn begins the contact; reversing direction holds parallel wraps while tension remains; an extra turn creates a half hitch; additional turns increase grip; and crossed directions form a strong X friction. More turns trade speed for stability, and slick materials require more structure. Fold rope neatly into the friction so contact surfaces compact rather than bunch.

When threading a narrow gap, hook the rope with one or two fingers and pull it through as if crocheting. Pushing slack rope is inefficient and can damage fibers. Drill different frictions on a rope skirt, then test how direction, material, pull, spacing, and untying speed change their behavior.

Source: https://rope365.com/improvisation/

One-rope improvisation uses a sound single-column anchor and basic frictions, then follows the body's position, the rope's angle, and the partners' dialogue rather than forcing a memorized final picture. Negotiate touch and body areas first, stay loose near joints, and avoid the front of the neck. Alternate starting points, movement, direction, and methods of untying. A useful exercise is to let each new friction determine the next natural angle. Patterns are learning tools; once their components are embodied, attention should return to the person.

### Locks, extensions, cinches, and storage

Source: https://rope365.com/clove-hitch-lock/

A clove hitch is fast and memorable but can slide. Two half hitches tied after a change of direction are more secure because the turn limits travel. Test any lock by loading and moving it before trusting it. After locking, remaining rope can strengthen or extend the tie, form a leash, add sensation, wrap an existing structure, or be tucked between tensioned lines. A knot that is compact under load may also be slow to release; practice untying under realistic tension.

Source: https://rope365.com/extending-rope/

Joining another rope is a contextual choice. A lark's-head/cow-hitch style extension is quick when end knots prevent pull-through; a square-knot connection is more compact but requires correct orientation and sufficient tails. Put extensions where their bulk will not press into nerves, joints, the armpit, or skin against the floor, and avoid burying a join where it weakens a critical friction or obstructs an attachment point. Practice extending with either hand and deliberately place the joint rather than waiting until only centimeters remain.

Source: https://rope365.com/cinches/

A cinch passes between two columns or bands. It keeps wraps from rolling together, separates limbs, and can make a loose-looking two-column structure more stable. Cinches also concentrate force: keep them flat, avoid pinching tissue in a narrow gap, place them away from joints and superficial nerves, and do not assume tighter is better. Test the relationship between wrap tension, cinch tension, gap size, movement, and release.

Source: https://rope365.com/coiling/

An overhand hank folds rope repeatedly and uses the bight or tail to secure the bundle. A useful coil deploys without tangling, exposes whichever end the next tie needs, does not introduce damaging kinks, and suits transport versus immediate performance. Keep tension even while coiling and inspect the entire rope as it passes through the hands.

### Hitches in the middle of a rope

Source: https://rope365.com/half-hitch/

A half hitch is a directional turn around an object or line; under continuing pull it is fast and useful for reverse tension, ladders, and temporary structure. By itself it is not a complete lock. Study which direction opens or compacts it and how one additional turn changes grip.

Source: https://rope365.com/clove-hitch/

A clove hitch is two half hitches in the same direction. It grips and tightens as the tail is pulled, so it is appropriate on objects and only selectively on well-protected body areas. Avoid wrists and exposed nerves. It may be threaded, preformed and slipped over an accessible end, or stacked into decorative/compressive sequences. Stacked hitches should share direction, tension, spacing, and alignment; compression that may feel acceptable on a calf can be hazardous on an arm.

Source: https://rope365.com/crossing-hitch/

A crossing hitch secures two lines at an angle while continuing in the incoming direction. It is asymmetric left/right and over/under; in three dimensions those choices affect whether it rolls open. Use folded, compact contact surfaces and orient the hitch against the expected load. A rope-net drill adds one whenever lines cross, while keeping crossings off joints and vulnerable nerves.

Source: https://rope365.com/reversed-crossing-hitch/

The reverse crossing hitch has related topology but exits the opposite way. It is useful for upward work, mirrored designs, and loads that roll a regular crossing hitch open. Hold the loop base while tightening to preserve tension; an added twist can increase friction and reduce directionality. In a rope cage, alternate regular and reverse forms according to load rather than appearance alone. Standing cages require stable support against falls.

Source: https://rope365.com/inline-cuff/

An inline cuff captures a column mid-rope. A half-hitch version stays stable only while both origin and exit retain tension. Repeating cuffs in a zigzag produces a mayfly pattern; crossings can be reinforced with crossing hitches. Each cuff needs flat equal wraps, suitable slack, and a compact friction. Movement can transfer force or tighten an upstream segment, so test the whole system.

Source: https://rope365.com/cow-hitch/

A cow hitch is two symmetric/opposed half hitches, unlike a clove hitch's same-direction pair. It can change direction, hold tension, or form a more stable inline cuff, but that cuff may still tighten when loaded from its origin. In a crotch rope, negotiate contact and pressure explicitly and consider offsetting the line from genitals. When used in a hip harness, load it in several directions and observe which face is flatter and which pull opens it.

Source: https://rope365.com/quick-release/

A quick release based on a slipped half hitch avoids pulling the whole rope through and releases one-handed. It needs friction or a change of direction on its origin and usually a second half hitch to retain the slipped loop. It trades structural certainty for speed and can open accidentally. A quick-release cuff locks its exit but still needs origin tension.

The shrimp/ebi exercise demonstrates why rapid exits matter: forward folding can compromise breathing, and arms behind the body add nerve risk. Keep every cuff away from joints and nerves, monitor hand movement, and never let the romance of a “quick release” replace a cutter or a practiced full exit.

## Chest structures: Days 15–21

### Horizontal ladders, diagonal weaving, and diamonds

Source: https://rope365.com/chestladder/

A ladder harness uses broad horizontal wraps, commonly above and below pectoral tissue, joined by a stem; shoulder straps resist downward slippage. Opening the front stem into a V may move pressure away from clavicles, while the same framework can draw a star or other shape. Keep wraps flat and evenly tensioned, test the stem and wraps in all expected directions, and adapt placement to breathing and anatomy. Arms-up construction can become tighter when arms lower because the rib cage changes shape.

Source: https://rope365.com/weavings/

Diagonal lines can be woven over and under at crossings. Individual lines remain mobile, but collectively the fabric-like field gains stability. The teardrop is one application; the general exercise is to choose a diagonal origin, keep spacing and tension regular, then test the harness through breath, arm movement, spinal flexion, and different body shapes. Weaving is not the same as a compact friction: stability emerges from the entire field.

Source: https://rope365.com/chest-diamond/

An open-diamond/karada begins with a central sequence of knots or hitches and opens the paired lines sideways. Opening progressively adds chest tension, so leave allowance and monitor breathing. The between-the-legs route requires explicit agreement; a waist route is an alternative. Diamonds may also be drawn with half hitches and crossing hitches or by overlaying opposing spirals. Angled load can feel gentler than a straight ladder, but lines near the collarbones and neck still require careful placement.

### Decorative knots, serpentine release, and asymmetry

Source: https://rope365.com/chest-knots/

Two half knots make either a square or granny knot depending on orientation. The square is generally easier to untie; the granny is less predictable, and neither is a critical-load knot. A double-coin/Josephine knot is related to the carrick-bend family and joins two ropes in a broad decorative form. A triple-crown button knot supplies loops or “petals.” Decorative knots add bulk and pressure, so compactness, placement, and an exit remain functional concerns.

Source: https://rope365.com/serpentine/

A serpentine catches horizontal lines alternately around a central stem. If deliberately constructed for it, withdrawing the stem can collapse the structure. Equal spacing and progressive stem tension make the lightning-like form predictable. Lines along the lower ribs can affect diaphragm motion or press the solar plexus; map the rib edge and individual response before loading that area.

Source: https://rope365.com/chestasymmetry/

Asymmetry can begin off-center, distort an initially symmetric harness, or balance unequal lines into a stable whole. Inspect from several angles, test movement, and distinguish visual imbalance from uneven or hazardous tension. It is also a practical way to accommodate one-sided injury or mobility.

### Shaping rather than merely decorating

Source: https://rope365.com/sculpture/

Rope can frame, lift, compress, or redirect soft tissue and can support feminine, masculine, or personally meaningful forms. A chest binder needs an underlying vertical structure and broad lines that move tissue upward before compressing it toward the rib cage, avoiding folded or twisted skin. Long-duration binding has considerations beyond a short scene. Define the desired identity, sensation, and marks together, make several temporary studies, and choose the version the person wearing it actually values.

## Folded limbs and frog ties: Days 22–28

### Make the position fit the knee

Source: https://rope365.com/frog-frictions/

A frog tie/futomomo holds a calf against its thigh. Bring the leg only as far as its comfortable range allows before laying the rope; if flexibility increases later, a partially folded start will loosen. Existing knee injury, pressure on the tibia, circulation restriction, and lateral cutaneous or common peroneal nerve pressure all require attention. A firm but removable spacer between calf and thigh can stop a fragile knee from folding farther.

A spiral version starts with an ankle single-column, travels around the folded leg, and secures the spiral with compact frictions. Keep the knee free of lateral shear, place lines on muscle rather than the fibular head near the outside of the knee, check distal sensation and foot movement, and slowly unfold after a long hold. Vary wrap count, spacing, direction, and the stem only after the base position is sustainable.

Source: https://rope365.com/frog-starting-points/

The ankle is only one possible origin. A waist, foot, toe, chest, or hidden mid-rope origin changes sensation, handling, and where force enters the structure. Pressure may be distributed into the thigh while kept gentle over the shin. Compare stability, long-term comfort, emotional response, and what happens when the leg relaxes; the most elegant start is not useful if it worsens nerve pressure or permits the wrap to roll.

### Cinches, patterns, and deliberate irregularity

Source: https://rope365.com/frog-cinches/

Cinches between calf and thigh keep wraps near the knee from flipping and let the tie tolerate more pulling, but narrow gaps make bunching and pinching likely. Place wraps to preserve space, feed rope through the larger ankle-side opening and slide it toward the knee, keep multiple cinches parallel, avoid extensions in the gap, and cinch from knee toward ankle while securing the ankle-side wraps early enough to preserve position. Solidity carries a cost in pressure and removal time.

Source: https://rope365.com/frog-diamonds/

Diamonds on a folded leg can be opened from a central line, drawn with hitches, or produced with opposing spirals. Anticipate the added tension created when a diamond opens. The knee's taper makes slippage more likely than on a torso, so compare shape size, count, construction, and load through movement. Numbness on the outer calf or top of the foot can indicate common peroneal involvement; stop and release rather than accepting an unfamiliar sensation as ordinary pins and needles.

Source: https://rope365.com/frog-chaos/

A chaotic version changes direction instead of building clean parallel wraps, then gathers selected lines into one or two friction stems. Unevenness may be intentional, but the same knee, nerve, circulation, and release checks apply. Repetition across several days should include warm-up, position changes, honest permission to stop, gentle unfolding, and light movement afterward.

### Apply folded-limb ideas to arms and the whole body

Source: https://rope365.com/chicken-wing/

A chicken-wing tie applies folded-leg ideas to a bent arm. Softer tissue and superficial nerves in the armpit, elbow, wrist, and upper arm make it less forgiving. Some people develop ulnar symptoms from elbow flexion alone; tingling in the ring or little finger is a signal to open the angle. Keep the design sparse, monitor opening and closing of the hand, and preserve a rapid exit. Remaining rope can position the arm only after the local cuff is demonstrably safe.

Source: https://rope365.com/assembling-ties/

Two folded arms can form bunny, T-Rex, angel, demon, or riffle-like positions; two folded legs and two arms can be combined into seated, kneeling, side, back, or prone restrictions. Treat every limb separately: check sensation, movement, circulation, and positional strain before linking it to the others. Then test how spinal flexion, extension, or torsion distributes force. A full-body construction multiplies exits, so keep combinations simple enough to release.

## Box-tie family: Days 29–35

### First rope: restraint from structure, not a tight wrist cuff

Source: https://rope365.com/box-tie-one-rope/

The one-rope box tie begins with a loose cuff around the forearms or, for limited shoulder mobility, at the waist or one wrist. An upper torso wrap then prevents the elbows from escaping and is secured to a centered stem. The wrists should rest rather than carry the structure. Keep pressure off wrist joints and hands; place upper-arm wraps on the lower portion of the deltoid while avoiding the softer radial-nerve region below and avoiding a line so high it can slide toward the neck. A chest-only wrap is a lower-nerve-risk alternative.

Continuously compare hand opening, closing, and sensation at the top of the thumb. Adapt the arms one at a time: low hands generally reduce shoulder stress, while high hands open the elbows and may be inaccessible. Rolling a shoulder forward, backward, or leaving it neutral can change sustainable range; guide with gentle force only. The finished cuff is stable but loose, the stem supports the arms, the wrap has equal tension, and pulling the back center in several directions returns it to position. A chair, stuffed garment, chest-only pattern, or spotted self-tie can be used for low-load rehearsal.

### Second rope: lower wrap, cinches, and safer joins

Source: https://rope365.com/box-tie-two-ropes/

A lower wrap beneath the pectorals and around the arms limits elbow movement and makes the structure more stable. Lower-wrap cinches are structurally important; top-wrap cinches are a safety trade. They may prevent upward movement toward the neck but can press the brachial plexus in the armpit. Omit them when anatomy, strong wrap tension, limited motion, and downward loading already prevent slippage. Consider them when round shoulders, space at the armpit, vigorous movement, or upward load makes sliding plausible, but keep them flat and loose enough that they add no armpit tension.

The top wrap sits on stronger upper chest/arm tissue and may be firmer; the lower wrap should be slightly looser and placed between the radial vulnerability above and ulnar vulnerability near the elbow, often deflecting under the pectoral. Lift soft chest tissue rather than trapping a fold. Keep rope joins in the back away from the central stem: never bury an extension between arm and torso, in the armpit, on an arm that will contact the floor, or inside a critical friction. Lower cinches may shape the wrap modestly; connecting lines lie flat outside the stem. Recheck every first-rope criterion after adding complexity.

### Third rope and structural variations

Source: https://rope365.com/box-tie-front-v/

Shoulder straps can connect a back stem to a front V, shape chest tissue, and create a front attachment point. Keep shoulder lines loose; high pressure near the clavicle can affect superficial sensation or the axillary nerve, and doubled lines can spread unavoidable load. A tight kikkō/hexagonal net in back can integrate the wraps while the front remains adjusted to the person's preferred openness. Alternate designs include a wider “mountain” X, a close-neck X, and side-pulled straps that clear clavicles.

Source: https://rope365.com/box-tie-chest/

Hybrid designs put one or both horizontal wraps on the chest rather than the arms. They are more escapable but reduce arm load and can permit longer play or arm-position changes. A chest harness may be completed before wrists are added. A kimono/tasuki base crosses over shoulders and forearms in front and back; it can support wrists, then serve as the stem for later chest or arm wraps. It still inherits every relevant box-tie nerve and breathing concern.

Source: https://rope365.com/box-tie-cuffs/

Inline cuffs can replace a wrap, exposing more chest but concentrating force on smaller upper-arm areas. Radial vulnerability toward the outside upper arm and ulnar vulnerability toward the elbow leave a narrow safe-placement problem; use only enough tension to retain the cuff. Combining chest wraps with cuffs shifts load away from arms. Cuffs may also add forearm support or decoration to an existing box tie, but adding rows toward elbows increases risk and removal time.

Source: https://rope365.com/box-tie-open-diamonds/

An open-diamond box tie starts with extra front slack, because spreading the front lines later adds tension. It preserves a strong back stem while angling pressure around the chest. Continue to monitor hand motor and sensory function, keep cinches flat and clear of the armpit, leave the lower wrap gentler, and use later rope to clear the neck where needed. A chest-only adaptation retains the diamond exercise without binding arms.

Source: https://rope365.com/box-tie-variations/

“Box tie” may narrowly mean crossed horizontal forearms or broadly mean bent, outward elbows behind the back. The broader definition accommodates people who cannot cross wrists. Hands may remain separate or hold each other; origins can move away from the wrists; serpentine or asymmetric structures may replace straight wraps. Historical prisoner forms often start at the neck or target nerves and therefore need substantial redesign, not literal reproduction.

## Hogties and whole-body links: Days 36–42

Source: https://rope365.com/hogtie-wrists-to-ankles/

A minimal hogtie connects wrists and ankles with one or more cuffs and a locked joining line. Minimalism concentrates load on wrists: place cuffs on forearms, retain enough room for circulation but not accidental escape, and test median/ulnar hand closing plus radial sensation and hand opening. Separate versus shared wrist and ankle cuffs change access and restriction. Pulling the connector upward is a new load case and requires a fresh inspection.

Source: https://rope365.com/hogtie-chest-harness/

Attaching ankles to a chest harness or box-tie stem spreads force over a broader structure. Chest-loaded harnesses more often affect breathing; arm-loaded box ties more often affect nerves. Attach at several candidate points and observe slippage and distribution rather than assuming the central stem is always best. Connecting lines must avoid exposed nerves.

Source: https://rope365.com/hogtie-shoulders-to-ankles/

Shoulder straps can take pressure that would otherwise reach wrists. A single-column around both shoulders or a bight-built pair can form the base, with underarm lines pressed toward torso rather than into the armpit. An armbinder/strappado then places arms parallel behind the back and carries significant median, ulnar, radial, and internal shoulder-mechanics risk. Keep wraps broad and modest, check thumb/index closing, ring/little-finger closing, and full hand opening, adjust armpit skin, and return arms forward slowly at weakness. Favor a quick exit and be ready to cut.

Source: https://rope365.com/hogtie-ankles-crossing/

Crossed ankles force knees apart. Leave room because changing ankle angle can tighten a shared cuff painfully. A chest structure or folded-leg ties may hold the position without excessive ankle pressure. Test knees together and apart and ask whether the position can be achieved without loading the ankle bones directly.

Source: https://rope365.com/hogtie-legs-crossing/

Crossing knees and then positioning ankles introduces torsion through hips, knees, and ankles. Find the position without rope first and never force a knee to accept rotation. Use separate lines if needed to maintain angles without one over-tight junction, compare left-over-right and right-over-left, and keep the minimum structure that reliably holds.

Source: https://rope365.com/hogtie-asymmetry/

Asymmetry may come from different limb ties, body position, tension, or micro-bondage at feet and toes. Small digits need gentler pressure and easy inspection. Deliberately unequal sensation is different from an unintentionally overloaded side; move and load the tie to reveal the difference.

Source: https://rope365.com/hogtie-flipping/

Gravity can turn a loose supine hogtie into a tight prone one. Begin with enough slack for the most demanding orientation, use a bed or mat without an edge to fall from, and pivot through a knee or elbow rather than loading a wrist or ankle. In asymmetry, each roll direction creates a different pinning and pressure pattern, so evaluate both before play.

## Choosing, conditioning, and maintaining rope: Days 43–49

### Size, material, and construction

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-size/

Some kits use varied lengths so a tie has few joins; others use one standard length and extend as needed. A useful standard is long enough to make progress without constant joins but short enough to pull efficiently—often near the tyer's arm span and commonly 7–9 m. Short pieces of roughly 0.3–4 m solve finishes and local additions. Bondage diameters commonly fall around 4–8 mm, with 6 mm a typical compromise: thin rope makes compact knots and greater local pressure; thick rope spreads contact but creates bulk. Measure preferences by repeating the same tie with gradually shortened and differently sized rope.

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-material/

Compare materials by skin-drag burn speed, grip, texture, weight, strength, price, water response, cleaning, and allergy risk. Cotton is inexpensive, washable, and moderate in most traits. Nylon and MFP are strong, washable, colorful, and often slick; their stretch, melting behavior, and friction depend on construction. Jute is light, low-stretch, grippy, and fast but often abrasive when new, difficult to wash, and weaker. Hemp is heavier, stronger, grassy-smelling, and high-friction. Abrasive sisal or manila sold for hardware use may contain skin-unsafe treatments. Bamboo/silk blends can be soft; labels matter because “silk” may actually be synthetic. Hempex and POSH imitate natural texture with water-tolerant synthetics; paracord is strong and small for detailed work. Elastic cord should not encircle skin where unpredictable contraction can create a tourniquet.

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-construction/

Twisted rope separates into strands, yarns, and sometimes plies. Three strands are common and stable. More plies often add durability and stiffness; loose lay tends toward flexibility and compact knots but can high-strand, while tight lay is firmer and more forgiving. Braids vary from solid to single and double forms; tightness controls flexibility and stretch. A core may be rope, another fiber, or plastic and can deform separately from the sheath. Kernmantle construction puts strength inside a protective braid and is often too stiff for body tying, though small paracord is an exception. Cut expendable samples and inspect strands, core, material, and relative ply length rather than judging only the exterior.

### Ends, processing, color, and ongoing care

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-ends/

Synthetic ends can be melted—preferably before and after cutting—but molten plastic burns and drips, and overheating damages rope. Whipping with thread is smooth and color-codeable. A compact overhand stopper is quick and supports fast extensions and end tucks. Crown-derived stoppers, shrink tube, and splices offer other tradeoffs. Compare snagging, construction effort, solidity, ease of reopening for maintenance, extension method, and compatibility with the fiber.

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-care/

Condition only to achieve a defined result; every aggressive process can shorten life. Natural rope may arrive stiff, hairy, chemically treated, and dirty, while many synthetics need only cleaning. Jute batching oil can cause odor or allergy and cannot be assumed completely removable; rope spun without it is the clear option when exposure is unacceptable.

- Breaking/polishing rope over a smooth, round carabiner increases flexibility but produces dust and weakens fibers; ventilate and use suitable eye, hand, and respiratory protection.
- Singeing removes surface fuzz from natural fiber. Keep rope moving through a clean flame; never singe synthetic rope, which melts.
- Boiling, machine washing inside a bag/chain, or a no-heat dishwasher may clean compatible rope. Wet jute and other shrink-prone rope must dry straight under continuing tension, retightened as it dries.
- Low-temperature baking may reduce moisture, germs, or volatile oil but can make rope brittle and release hazardous vapor; ventilate and understand the material.
- Natural rope needs sparing compatible oil. Mineral oil is relatively stable and hypoallergenic; other oils may turn rancid or trigger allergy. Oil before wax so wax does not seal a dry core.
- Sun or freezing is not sterilization and does not remove dirt; sun can dry and weaken fiber.

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-maintenance/

Hygiene is part of consent. Natural-fiber rope cannot be made completely sterile without damage, so separate partner-specific or fluid-contact kits when cross-contamination is unacceptable. Wash washable synthetic or cotton rope as needed; minimize wet cycles for jute/hemp and accept earlier retirement if frequent cleaning is required. Keep natural rope from drying and oil after cleaning.

Inspect by running the whole length through the hands. Light high-stranding in twisted rope may relax through firm massage in both directions; larger imbalance may require opening an end to shorten a stretched strand or locally untwisting and retwisting. Uneven yarns inside a strand can indicate poor construction and lower confidence for load-bearing use. Retire rope whose damage or history falls outside the intended risk profile.

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-color/

Color can carry aesthetic, cultural, or identity meaning; historical Japanese color codes varied by time, school, season, rank, and police jurisdiction, so they are context rather than a universal modern key. Match dye chemistry to fiber. Existing color and mixed-material cores affect results; bleaching severely weakens rope. Most dyeing wets rope, so dry shrink-prone material under tension, rinse until transfer is acceptable, and expect fading. Dye plies before rope-making when core saturation matters.

### Buying and adapting rope without brand trivia

Source: https://rope365.com/rope-shopping/

For a first kit, inexpensive untreated cotton or nylon around 6 mm and 7–10 m is sufficient to learn preferences and can be washed. Japanese-influenced, friction-led handling often favors similarly sized jute or hemp pieces with knotted ends; decorative or knot-heavy western styles often favor washable synthetic/cotton rope in several lengths with smooth whipped or melted ends. Try samples before investing. Ask about fiber, treatment, lay, core, strength testing, water response, and maintenance instead of assuming a specialized seller guarantees suitability.

Source: https://rope365.com/knis-rope-materials-primer/

A practical mixed kit may contain several 6–9 m pieces and a few 3–5 m pieces. POSH offers a grippy, water-tolerant polyester option; nylon ranges from relatively firm solid braid to stretchier, lower-friction forms; MFP is light, non-absorbent, low-melting, and wear-prone; Hempex resembles raw hemp without absorbing water. Natural rope is a maintenance commitment: hemp is durable and tactile; jute is light and fast but less strong and more demanding. One-ply and loose-lay rope need more careful handling and tension drying; more plies and tighter lay generally tolerate beginners better. Published breaking strengths are product-specific, not transferable to every rope sharing a material name.

Source: https://rope365.com/tying-shibari-with-slippery-rope/

Low-tooth rope changes structure, not merely feel. Stretch can erase carefully set harness tension under load, and slick rope may need extra length and more turns. A sheet bend can be more reliable than a collapsed lark's-head/square join when correctly oriented; a last bend may be easier to release than a compacted water knot. Somerville or surgeon-based columns resist collapse better than many square-knot cuffs. Build a double column from a stable single column and add the cinch. Reinforce an X friction's free upper wrap with an L friction, replace a half-moon with a full-moon when it creeps, and give a half hitch another turn. Re-test every adaptation in the exact material and avoid heavily loading a stretchy box tie merely because the same pattern worked in jute.

## Body knowledge and higher-consequence areas: Days 50–56

### Nerve injury: response and upper-limb checks

Source: https://rope365.com/nerves/

Compression, swelling, or shear can injure a nerve. Symptoms occur downstream from the injury and may include tingling, numbness, burning, electrical pain, or weakness, but warning may be absent and may appear only after untying. Damage is often cumulative. On a suspected injury, smoothly remove pressure without twisting the compressed body part, avoid massage or renewed pressure, rest it, and seek a clinician if normal movement or sensation does not return promptly. The source discusses ice, anti-inflammatory medicine, and nutrition but also notes changing evidence and contraindications; those choices belong with qualified medical advice.

Upper-limb nerves emerge from the cervical spine and reorganize in the brachial plexus near shoulder and armpit. Particularly relevant checks are:

- **Radial:** opens wrist and hand; sensation on the top/thumb side. Upper-arm injury may cause wrist drop. Check full finger/wrist extension and light sensation over the thumb.
- **Ulnar:** closes and spreads the little-finger side and supplies its sensation. It is vulnerable at the armpit, “funny bone,” and little-finger side of wrist. Check ring/little-finger closing and side movement.
- **Median:** closes and spreads the thumb side; it is vulnerable inside the arm/elbow and at the palm-side thumb wrist. Check thumb/index closing.
- **Axillary:** lifts the arm; heavy shoulder pressure can weaken abduction. **Supraclavicular** injury can leave a numb patch over the collarbone. The **long thoracic** nerve affects scapular control.

Establish a baseline before tying. A bear-paw or duck-like hand position can combine extension and flexion checks. Repeat during and after ties, and do not make the bound person solely responsible for remembering. Gentle palpation can help map an individual's superficial spots, but intentionally provoking a nerve is itself risky and should never be done over a recent injury. Nerve glides are rehabilitation tools that can also irritate; use only gentle, limited range and stop at the first neural sensation.

Box ties can affect wrists, outer upper arms, armpits, and elbows. Bunny/riffle positions stretch the ulnar nerve. Strappado can create unseen internal shoulder compression even without a poorly placed rope. If changing the arm position is the only relief, release the entire relevant structure rather than adjusting a small line around a compromised nerve.

### Lower-limb nerve map

Source: https://rope365.com/nerves-lower/

Leg nerves are often deeper, so rope injuries more commonly produce a numb patch than motor loss, but foot drop is possible. Relevant regions include:

- hip lines deep above the hip bone can affect iliohypogastric/ilioinguinal branches; a line just inside the front hip bone can affect the lateral femoral cutaneous nerve and numb the outer/front thigh;
- deep groin pressure can affect obturator or femoral nerves; prolonged buttock contraction can entrap cluneal branches;
- the saphenous branch is vulnerable toward the inner knee/calf;
- deep and superficial peroneal/fibular branches are vulnerable behind the knee and around the outer fibular head. A higher injury can weaken foot lifting; a lower injury may numb the top of the foot or the space between first and second toes;
- coccyx history matters for crotch ropes, and pre-existing sciatica changes which movements are tolerable.

Check active foot lifting against light comparison pressure and periodically touch different sensory zones. At a change, loosen and then fully remove the relevant rope without sudden shear, protect the region from further pressure, and seek medical help if function does not return quickly.

### Touch and pressure points

Source: https://rope365.com/touch/

Skin receptors differ by depth, speed of adaptation, and body density: fingers and mouth distinguish close contacts that the torso experiences as one broad band. Vision, crossed limbs, expectation, and attention can change perception. Map each person's response to light versus deep, brief versus sustained contact instead of assuming a universal “sensitive spot.”

Pressure points may be exposed nerves, cartilage, tendon, vessel-adjacent tissue, muscle, or soft tissue against bone. A dramatic response does not mean the method is safe. Use light, steady, controlled pressure; avoid impact to nerve clusters, nose/ear cartilage, sternum, solar plexus, joints, or the head; and never use a bulky knot to hold prolonged force over a fragile point. Pain-compliance movement adds fall risk once balance or hands are restricted. Head, forearm, hip, thigh, foot, and toe controls should be negotiated and rehearsed without rope first. Prefer broad muscle-based massage or easily released hand pressure to fixed precision pressure.

### Upper-limb mobility

Source: https://rope365.com/mobility/

The shoulder's several joints permit rotation, lifting, and pulling down through different muscle groups; it is generally stronger under downward than upward load. The elbow hinges in flexion/extension while radius and ulna enable palm rotation. Wrists and fingers have limited multi-axis range and are easily overextended.

Warm up with controlled shoulder circles, arm lifting, elbow bends, wrist rotations, rapid hand opening, and chest/back movement. Deep stretching belongs after general warm-up and can temporarily weaken a muscle, so do not use it to force an immediate rope position. Partner stretches require slow force and ongoing feedback. Hypermobility calls for active muscle engagement, not deeper passive range.

For an elbow pointing upward, rotating an arm from the front often reaches a more vertical, sustainable line than abducting from the side; align close to the head to reduce gravity, and move to the floor when fatigue begins. For hands behind the back, lower wrists are generally easier. Compare forward-rolled, backward-rolled, and neutral shoulders one side at a time. Palm rotation can lock the elbow; apply it gradually because wrist control transmits force through the entire arm.

### Spine, lower-body mobility, and circulation while still

Source: https://rope365.com/mobility-lower/

The cervical spine is most mobile; thoracic vertebrae contribute to every direction; the lumbar region supplies much flexion and extension but little rotation; the sacrum is essentially fixed. Breathing in can reduce spinal compression and change available range. Hips are strong ball-and-socket joints yet often have old injuries; engage abdominal and hip muscles rather than pulling a hypermobile femur passively. Knees primarily flex and extend and tolerate lateral shear poorly. Ankles combine several joints with limited range and frequent prior sprain.

Warm the back, hips, knees, ankles, and feet with cat/cow, gentle twists, hip and ankle circles, high knees, heel-to-butt movement, and varied foot loading. Save deeper forward bends, backbends, splits, and partner-assisted stretches for warmed bodies and use slow force. Every person's path into a position matters; compare one side at a time and asymmetric entries.

Still legs contribute less muscular pumping to venous return. Prolonged restriction, especially standing, can lead to dizziness, nausea, fainting, or panic when movement resumes. Contract quadriceps/calves periodically, move again gradually, and keep a catcher or secure support within reach. In a prone hogtie, lifting a knee before drawing the ankle toward chest brings the spine into backbend; this increases intensity and shortens sustainable time. Torsion begins from an aligned spine and moves shoulders and hips oppositely without forcing lumbar rotation.

### Torso, breath, and positional asphyxia

Source: https://rope365.com/torso/

The diaphragm divides thoracic organs from the abdomen. Upper true ribs are anchored more robustly than lower false and floating ribs; the abdomen protects organs mainly with muscle. The solar/celiac plexus below the sternum is a vulnerable nerve region where impact or a hard knot can disrupt diaphragm function.

Chest pressure limits rib/intercostal expansion; abdominal pressure limits diaphragm/belly breathing. Applying both, adding a gag, or folding the torso can sharply reduce reserve. Forward bends compress abdominal contents toward the diaphragm; backbends with arms behind stretch breathing muscles and can make exhalation difficult. Agree on a non-verbal distress signal and construct respiratory restriction for immediate removal. If someone faints, remove all obstruction, use a recovery position, and call emergency services if they do not wake within seconds.

Hyperventilation can also cause tingling, dizziness, and fainting. Stay calm, release the rope, and obtain medical help if it does not resolve promptly or recurs. The page mentions bag breathing, but contemporary first-aid practice can differ and underlying emergencies can resemble panic; do not use this corpus as a substitute for current first-aid training.

### Neck: a seconds-scale emergency

Source: https://rope365.com/neck/

The neck contains a small mobile cervical spine, airway cartilage, hyoid bone, swallowing and voice structures, carotid arteries, jugular veins, vagus and pressure-sensing nerves. Airway compression can fracture slow-healing cartilage and create painful air hunger. Vascular compression can produce unconsciousness in seconds and brain injury, stroke, seizure, coma, or death if maintained. A fixed rope takes longer to release than a martial-arts hold, can tangle when unloaded, and may shock-load fragile structures when connected to a limb or anchor.

Never self-tie neck pressure alone; a safety call or video observer cannot release it fast enough. A cutter helps only if a single accessible strand can be cut before harm. Quick-release knots can fail to release or tangle. If exploring touch near the neck at all, begin with fingers and a broad folded rope held in two hands as an extension of the hands, apply less pressure than by hand, avoid knots, stay below the person's agreed threshold, and maintain a seconds-scale exit. The front and sides require very little pressure; the muscular back/base is not risk-free. Consent must explicitly cover both physical danger and trauma triggers.

## Equipment and hardpoints

Source: https://rope365.com/knis-equipment-primer/

Inspect equipment before every use against a defined risk profile; the person being tied should also be welcome to inspect it. Prefer products with traceable ratings and reputable distribution over visually identical, uncertified copies. Practice with the exact rescue tool. Shears work on loose or untensioned rope but fatigue hands; rescue hooks cut tensioned line quickly but struggle on slack rope. A retracting lanyard can keep a cutter reachable when one arm supports a body. A marlinspike releases jams but is supplemental. General knives, snips, and box cutters tend to require two hands or expose skin to a blade.

For rigging hardware, webbing/slings must fit the surface and load. Nylon stretches more than Dyneema and can soften shock to trees or wood; abrasion protection matters on rough surfaces. A swivel handles repeated rotation that a sling cannot. Larger round contact surfaces are gentler to rope but add pulley friction. Locking, round-profile carabiners avoid gate-opening and sharp I-beam/D-shape edges that can damage small bondage rope. Avoid triaxial loading unless every component and load case is understood. Wood gym rings dislike concentrated carabiner loading; unknown welded or drop-shipped rings deserve skepticism. Aluminum is light and corrosion-resistant but tends to fail more suddenly than deforming steel. Rigging plates organize lines but can rock, require connectors, and may be harder to grab in an emergency. Mats reduce ordinary impact; only a real crash pad is designed for harder falls, and none guarantees protection from concussion.

Source: https://rope365.com/diy-freestanding-hardpoints/

Freestanding frames most often injure through tipping. Leg splay, unequal loading, torsion, dynamic movement, and a high center of gravity require bridles/lashings, wide feet, ballast or floor anchoring, and repeated inspection. Tripod center loads can twist the crown; inspect lashing or welds and distribute mounting where possible. Never treat a frame as a swing merely because it resembles one.

For trees, reject dead, diseased, or insufficiently sized limbs, spread load with protectors, prefer more dynamic slings to static shock loading, and use removable friction hitches rather than constricting the tree. Flat-foot wood frames should be wider than tall, with feet roughly two-thirds or more of height, through-bolts that can be inspected, and no protruding fasteners. Full power racks need adequate height, depth, steel thickness, and ideally floor anchoring. A-frames and pagodas need tip control; pagodas also need torsion control and a central/swiveled load path for dynamic use. Bamboo or pipe tripods need correct species/material, crown lashing, load-pole selection, and leg bridles. DIY cost or apparent sturdiness never replaces engineering competence, rated components, conservative loading, and an exit plan.

## Practice curriculum: tension, movement, and partnership

### Tension as structure and communication

Source: https://rope365.com/tension/

Tension affects both solidity and the felt dialogue. Practice presence by holding opposite ends and moving with eyes open and closed; uniformity by wrapping while the recipient voices every variation; intensity by making intentionally loose and firm versions; shifting by moving an existing wrap and watching skin; holding by relying briefly on tensioned friction; dressing by sliding a finger under rope to redistribute line and skin; and vibration by wiggling a line or applying a vibrator at different tension and locations. The goal is reproducible control, not maximum tightness.

### Movement and balance

Source: https://rope365.com/movement/

Rehearse descent from standing without bound hands, then with supported leaning, before using rope to guide someone toward the floor. Move around a stationary partner rather than torquing their limbs for convenience; reverse roles and constraints to expose inefficient habits. Guide with torso, legs, forearms, forehead, or rope while preserving the person's balance and communication. Practice balance with harmless objects, then controlled imbalance from symmetric to asymmetric positions. Repeat movements with graceful, quiet, playful, or forceful intent to see how intention changes contact—without letting theatrical force exceed joint or fall limits.

### Partnership and aftercare drills

Source: https://rope365.com/partnership/

Alternate sessions in which the tied person narrates everything and the tyer narrates everything; then try quiet communication only after non-verbal emergency signals are established. Simulate a faint, nerve symptom, interruption, or cutter use with expendable rope. Explore distance through a leash, convey an emotion through handling, and share supported balance. Build an “aftercare menu” of food, drink, warmth, touch, massage, stretch, and solitude, then observe how needs change with the scene and person.

### Intimacy and a bed as a floorwork structure

Source: https://rope365.com/intimacy/

Intimacy may be erotic, emotional, sensory, or ritual. Treat rope and the tyer's body as extensions of negotiated desire, experiment with breath synchronization, close handling, and attentive untying, and discover which signals create safety and arousal for these particular people rather than copying a generic performance.

Source: https://rope365.com/bed/

A bed offers a soft plane and multiple anchors; a rope frame can run under a mattress when no posts exist. Confirm the bed cannot slide, collapse, or trap a line. Practice pulling paired wrists or ankles in different directions, four-corner spread positions, transitions that change one anchor at a time, and a web over the body. Preserve access to releases, prevent rolling off, and remember that softness does not remove circulation, joint, or breathing risks.

## Practice curriculum: upper- and lower-body positions

### Hands in front and behind

Source: https://rope365.com/hands-in-front/

Front positions are generally more accessible and sustainable. Explore: straight parallel arms in a front armbinder; crossed or parallel wrists anchored near belly; horizontal forearms in a reverse box/straightjacket tie; palms together at chest in front prayer; crossed arms over chest; a rope-held self-hug; and wrists near face with forearms either together or independent. Compare elbow mobility, wrist rotation, breath, and escapability rather than presuming “in front” means harmless.

Source: https://rope365.com/hands-in-the-back/

Warm shoulders and find each person's range before binding behind. Bunny ears place wrists behind the head and may join crossed, praying, separate, or parallel forearms. Reverse prayer with fingers upward is demanding; palms out, fingers sideways, or fingers downward offer different and sometimes more accessible versions. Wrists may attach low at hips or butt. Strappado holds arms parallel and can create internal shoulder/latissimus nerve pressure even with good rope placement, so monitor hands and release fast. An upper-body stem can also draw bent elbows toward the torso.

### Elbows, asymmetry, and indirect neck restriction

Source: https://rope365.com/elbow-ties/

Elbow-oriented positions include: upper arms together behind with wrists free or apart; a waitress pose with elbows behind and wrists in front; wrists in front while elbows are pulled back; parallel bent “T-Rex/mantis” arms; demon/tengu wrists near shoulders with elbows down; angel/tenshi elbows drawn back like wings; and forearms around the neck. Arm and shoulder warm-up, loose tension, nerve checks, and individual range are essential. A forearm self-choke needs enough wrist separation that the neck remains free and a rehearsed immediate release.

Source: https://rope365.com/asymmetric-arms/

Pull joined wrists toward different points around chest, hip, or shoulder; leave one arm free in a one-arm box tie; position one chicken-wing arm; combine one elbow up with the other down in a riffle/teppō; or draw a strappado to one side. An upward elbow fights gravity and fatigues, so the upper arm requires especially quick release. One-sided forms are valuable adaptations for injury and asymmetric mobility, not merely visual effects.

Source: https://rope365.com/neck-restriction-ties/

This chapter intentionally catalogs dangerous indirect neck arrangements: arms together in front connected near the neck, knuckles or palms against neck, one or two arms wrapped around it, forearms pressing from both sides, and elbow-up positions that tighten the contact. It instructs readers to study breathing and neck anatomy first, negotiate non-verbal signals, keep the cutter close, avoid a fixed tight neck line, never work alone, and release immediately. These designs can become lethal from body movement even when no rope initially compresses the airway or vessels.

### Build upper-body attachment structures

Source: https://rope365.com/upper-body-structures/

Shoulder straps prevent torso wraps from falling and, when doubled, distribute force more broadly. A stem becomes stronger when wrapped, when its bight is integrated, or when surrounded by a tight net such as a kikkō field. A second stem can join two wraps on the opposite side, giving front and back attachment options. A waist line resists vertical sliding and adds an anchor but also affects abdominal breathing. Mountain/tasuki Xs connect shoulder straps, while diamonds redirect load obliquely and form attachment nets. Each principle can be symmetric or asymmetric and should be load-tested in the direction it will actually be used.

### Hips, legs, sitting, and combined restrictions

Source: https://rope365.com/hips-and-butt/

A hip harness combines waist, hips, and upper thighs for decoration or attachment. It may extend into an upper-body harness, add side handles for asymmetric pulls, frame or compress buttocks, or form a two-column “booty basket” between waist and thighs. A side basket joins waist and one leg; a monoblock/package folds both legs toward waist. These wide structures can be strong enough to move a person, but strength also means deep hip/groin nerve pressure is possible. Test each handle direction and keep crotch contact separately negotiated.

Source: https://rope365.com/lower-body-structures/

Lower-body studies include: a rope skirt around thighs; a full-length mermaid with parallel or crossed legs; a calf binder; decorative “stockings” around individual legs; crossed-ankle/agura sitting; soles-together butterfly; and lotus, shoelace, or side-crossed variations. Offset ankle bones rather than stacking malleoli against one another. A straight-leg wrap may become far tighter when the knee later bends, so evaluate anticipated movement before finishing.

Source: https://rope365.com/sitting/

Floor positions range from relaxed cross-legged meditation to kneeling/seiza, hero pose with feet beside hips, side-sitting with ankles together, and pigeon/swan with one leg forward and the other drawn back. A cushion or block can make kneeling/hero accessible. Combine upper- and lower-body structures only after the unsupported pose is comfortable, and account for balance while the tyer moves around a person who cannot reposition freely.

Source: https://rope365.com/restrictions/

Whole-body restriction can pull opposite legs in a “running person,” cross one leg over another in a windmill pin, fasten straight arms to hips, join arms to knees, bend the body into S-curves, compact it into a ball/package, or fold it into a shrimp/ebi. For the shrimp, use a chest structure instead of a neck line and plan fast release because forward fold limits breathing. Standing S-shapes need a catcher. The central exercise is to identify which joint or line actually prevents motion and remove unnecessary pressure elsewhere.

Source: https://rope365.com/exposed/

Exposure is a negotiated effect, not an automatic property of a pose. A crab joins arms and legs on each side; open-knee/M/moon forms hold knees apart; a tuck joins wrists behind knees; a beast tie joins all four limbs forward; legs-to-chest and side-opening variants expose lower body; and a butt-up/momo form folds wrists between ankles and fixes the waist. Change orientation—back, side, belly, seated—to discover new pressure before adding sexual activity.

Source: https://rope365.com/body-challenges/

Physical challenge can emphasize forward fold, backbend, arm stretch, splits, spinal torsion, whole-body mummification, or endurance. Warm up, increase range gradually, distinguish productive exertion from joint/nerve pain, and make every endurance tie quickly releasable. Mummification adds heat, panic, circulation, breathing, and lengthy-exit hazards; incorporate several independent release points and keep a cutter ready.

### Small extremities

Source: https://rope365.com/feet-and-toes/

Shoes alter ankle mechanics and may offer attachment points; a foot harness spreads pulling over the foot; soles provide tickle and pressure-point play; feet can be joined side-by-side or sole-to-sole; lines between toes add intense local contact; individual toes can be pulled; and ankle/toe lines can point or flex the foot. Small bones, nerves, and easily escaped digits demand thin-force restraint, frequent checks, and low tension. A “gravity boot” is described as useful in floorwork even though similar structures later appear in suspension.

Source: https://rope365.com/hands-and-fingers/

Hands may be paired palms-in or out, held in fists, pinned open, joined by selected fingers, placed in a wrist lock, interlaced, or sculpted. Historical open/closed-hand symbolism is context, not a safety rule. Finger lines and backward wrist pulls generate large joint leverage from little force; proceed slowly, keep knots off vulnerable digital nerves, and remember that even a small hand injury can disrupt daily life.

## Technique expansion: storage, columns, frictions, hitches, and knots

### Storage systems

Source: https://rope365.com/storing-rope/

Choose a bundle for its next use. An overhand hank is simple and adaptable. A dense bar-wrapped transport bundle resists opening but deploys slowly. A figure-eight performance bundle with a quick tab exposes the bight and avoids tangles but can loosen in transit or wrinkle in storage. A chain sinnet suits slick braided rope; a reverse bundle feeds the tail progressively and keeps rope off a dirty floor. Historical ebi-musubi and kainokuchi braids combine portability with rapid release. For long storage, hang folded rope over a horizontal pole or make a broad low-pressure coil; ventilate and oil natural rope and avoid tight bends that set deformation.

### More single-column choices

Source: https://rope365.com/more-single-column-ties/

Select a cuff by collapse resistance, release under load, bight availability, speed, material, and intended pull:

- Square/granny variants are fast but can capsize into a tightening form. Additional half knots add bulk and do not turn an unsuitable knot into a critical-load solution.
- A lark's-head start followed by reverse tension and a half hitch is solid and leaves no dangling bight, but takes two full pull-throughs and cannot release from the start.
- A reversed French/WykD fast bowline is quick and usually easy to release under tension but may capsize under high load or with slick rope.
- Somerville variants change handling: the “East/Struggler's” form is easier to tension but less compact; a Burlington begins with a half knot and is slower/bulkier. Different hand paths can produce the same topology with different tension quality.
- Pre-tied sliding cuffs such as cat's-paw/zip-snare forms enter and leave quickly on slick rope but are not stable structural anchors.
- Pulling only a bight through a finished knot can create quick-release versions; that sacrifices stability, locking, and use of the bight as an anchor.
- French, on-a-bight, and Portuguese bowlines demonstrate that “bowline” is a family. Learn one deeply, then compare under real material and load.

### Multiple columns and lashings

Source: https://rope365.com/more-columns/

A two-column tie often starts with a trusted single-column and adds a cinch; the base knot retains its usual benefits and weaknesses. A thick connecting bar can become a handhold that unloads wrist cuffs. Capturing the second column later permits position changes without rebuilding the original anchor.

Square lashings bind columns near 90 degrees; diagonal lashings suit uncertain angles or gaps; shear lashings hold parallel columns while permitting their angle to change. Practice on poles before bodies, then remove hard turns and frapping pressure that pinch skin. An infinity form uses opposing figure-eight paths and may capture a bight to distribute force. A bar wrap deliberately separates wrists or ankles. Tripod/infinity principles can extend to three or more columns, but every added cinch multiplies pressure and exit complexity.

### More frictions and fabric-like structures

Source: https://rope365.com/more-techniques/

Design frictions by changing straight/diagonal, over/under, and number of turns; dress the rope along its own curve to maximize contact. Extend body weaving into hip fields, knots, or decoration. Braids/sinnets form broad stable cuffs without continuous tension. A serpentine/clam uses alternating catches around a removable stem; chain/slip stitches build quick body wraps without pulling the full rope through. A rope reeved through another line or carabiner supplies mechanical advantage, making it easy to create dangerous tension; carabiners reduce friction and therefore reduce felt warning. Finish with sword wraps, inline cuffs, half-hitch chains, sinnets, or bundles chosen by measured remaining length.

### More hitches

Source: https://rope365.com/more-hitches/

Multi-loop hitches such as Y hangers connect two or more points and distribute load. Mid-rope cuffs may use reverse-Somerville or captured-overhand structures that remain stable without continuing tension. A true Munter adds controllable friction to a moving line, whereas crossing hitches arrest a crossing. Locks can combine Munter plus half hitch, half hitches on opposite stem sides, cleat-like forms, or split tails tied off later. Gripping hitches hold slick poles by tightening and can become dangerous constricting cuffs on a body; they can also fail when unloaded and should not be casually repurposed as life-safety lines. Adjustable hitches move after tying. Farrimond and tumble forms add quick release, again trading security for speed.

### More knots and their functional families

Source: https://rope365.com/more-knots/

Slip knots and nooses form fast adjustable loops but tighten and are unstable on the body. Handcuff knots create two slipped loops yet share that constriction hazard. Fixed loop knots create end or mid-line attachment points, pulley eyes, and multi-loop anchors; doubling the bight can increase material but does not certify a life-safety system. Stoppers such as thistle or Matthew Walker forms finish three-strand rope compactly. **Bends** join ropes; the sheet bend is a widely useful alternative to the lark's-head and square connections taught earlier. Flower/triple-crown families add petals by repeating an interwoven principle. An exploding knot such as a lapp form is intended to detach completely even after the tail was used; test its exact orientation and accidental-release behavior before relying on it.

## Practice curriculum: handling, problem-solving, and creativity

### Flow and handling drills

Source: https://rope365.com/handling/

Practice finger-hooking at multiple angles with every finger; throw or place the tail where the next movement needs it; keep a short tail straight so it enters a narrow gap without snagging; and deliberately tie upward against gravity while a standing partner has fall protection. Exaggerate motion to study useful amplitude, then decompose a familiar sequence and perform it at half and quarter speed. Change rhythm with music, breath, or a metronome. Finally rehearse the same pattern with imaginary rope: differences reveal which gestures communicate and which merely manage material.

Source: https://rope365.com/multitasking/

Build one-handed lark's heads, slips, columns, and familiar patterns; switch dominant and non-dominant roles; temporarily tape safe fingers out of use; and manipulate rope with torso, feet, or mouth without compromising hygiene. The “super middle” is a second fold that creates two working pairs from one rope and can begin symmetric chest or arm restriction, but requires both sides to be managed at once. Practice parallel ropes together, then split them between hands. The practical outcome is the capacity to support a body or maintain contact while the other hand safely handles rope—not tricks for their own sake.

### Designed obstacles and failure discovery

Source: https://rope365.com/sabotage/

Use poor but body-safe practice rope, add harmless snags or a bell, substitute scarves/belts/towels, wear gloves, tie a known pattern blindfolded, limit the tyer's own movement, navigate placed obstacles, or work in a tight space. These drills expose reliance on sight, ideal material, and open space. Do not sabotage rescue equipment, load-bearing components, communication, or the partner's safety; inspect with sight before any blindfolded tie is loaded.

Source: https://rope365.com/challenges/

Tie around someone who must remain lying still; reduce noise; keep rope off a “lava” floor; work behind the body; reverse the normal construction order; add large amounts of rope while preserving release routes; or alternate between escape and retention goals. An escape game can slide lines toward neck or joints and must have hard stop conditions. Reversing a pattern is useful only when the function of every component survives the new order.

### Creativity and craft

Source: https://rope365.com/creativity/

Design a wearable rope garment; mix colors, materials, and diameters intentionally; apply one motif to several body regions; let a location influence the construction; randomize body-part pairs; improvise within a planned loop structure; or begin from safely placed random rope. Randomness supplies a prompt, not permission to skip negotiation, anatomy, or inspection.

Source: https://rope365.com/crafting/

Use rope to draw words or shapes, make a negotiated toy or leash, gift-wrap an object, prototype on a doll or stuffed garment, adapt to an unusual object, fill a room with an interconnected web, or twist twine into a rope sample. Object and dummy work is valuable for topology and composition but cannot teach tissue pressure, nerve response, breath, or consent.

### Rope as physical exercise

Source: https://rope365.com/work-out/

Warm every joint, dance while guiding with rope, borrow controlled grappling from wrestling, create strength/endurance holds, balance on one foot with a catcher, deepen an already safe stretch, and finish with rest or meditation. Wrestling plus loose rope adds entanglement and fall risk; balance work with tied hands always needs immediate support. Rope should scale an exercise the body can already perform, not force range it lacks.

## Lineage, history, aesthetics, and objects

### Hojōjutsu and prisoner-tie concepts

Source: https://rope365.com/hojojutsu/

Japanese rope-restraint traditions are often grouped as **torinawajutsu/hojōjutsu**. **Hayanawa** describes fast capture methods; **honnawa** describes more formal, secure restraint after capture. The Edo period (1603–1867) is presented as a major period of codification. Hojōjutsu is an indirect rather than simply continuous ancestor of erotic Japanese bondage: surviving evidence for erotic use is limited, while later rope artists deliberately studied and incorporated its forms.

Capture work prioritized wrists, arms, neck, speed, and control, often accepting nerve and choking hazards that are unacceptable in consensual modern practice. Full-body capture could connect clothing, feet, or neck to prevent escape. Formal ties encoded school, prisoner status, alleged crime, and shame. Open frictions could preserve dignity for high-status or not-yet-convicted people; closed versus open hands could signify resistance versus admission. Recurring motifs include hishi diamonds, jūmonji crosses, and kikkō hexagons. Schools hid complexity on the back, both as signature and to obstruct escape.

Transport ties often finished with a leash, sometimes chain-sinneted so a fleeing person gained slack before a sudden stop—an intentionally injurious tactic that should not be recreated literally. A “tie over tie” replaces quick capture with formal structure and removes the first rope. Disappearing ties use chain/slip principles so a prisoner can walk out while another holds the tail. Modern study should extract topology, history, and meaning while relocating lines away from neck and nerves, preventing struggle, and keeping a cutter ready.

### A documented but non-linear rope-bondage lineage

Source: https://rope365.com/history/

Rope began as a practical technology for hunting, building, farming, sailing, transport, arboriculture, theater, and rescue. Knots and colors acquired culture-specific meaning; Shinto rope and historical prisoner colors must be read in their own era and region rather than projected as universal symbolism.

Visual restraint appears in ancient and early-modern art, often as slavery, punishment, crime, or war rather than consensual eroticism. Edo-period ukiyo-e sometimes depicted captured or tortured figures. Tsukioka Yoshitoshi (1839–1892) became known for violent prints including rope. Itō Seiu (1882–1961) drew, painted, and photographed bound women and is described as a pioneering figure in kinbaku. Ozuma Kaname and Go Mishima are later notable illustrators.

European and American fetish art developed related strands. Carlõ published domination/submission illustration in the 1930s and influenced John Willie, creator of Sweet Gwendoline. Tom of Finland emphasized homoerotic bondage; Eric Stanton drew dominant women; Gene Bilbrew became an early Black professional fetish artist. These lineages interacted through traveling magazines rather than descending from a single national origin.

Stage practice changed what restraint needed to do: a theatrical tie could be escapable while prioritizing visibility, safety, emotion, and timing. Japanese rope performance became a distinct form, with Osada Eikichi prominent from the 1960s. Early erotic rope photographs include work by Charles-François Jeandel around 1890–1900 and the Paris Ostra/Biederer studio in the 1930s. Print periodicals such as Japan's *Kitan Club* (1947–1975) and the United States' *Bizarre* circulated techniques and aesthetics internationally.

The source identifies *Utsukushiki Imashime* (1953), authored by Minomura Kou with a second volume associated with Tsujimura Takashi, as an early published kinbaku book. Urado Hiroshi's 1972 *SM Play* is identified among early tutorial books. Later photography, pornography, film, music video, conventions, and online video accelerated cross-pollination.

Historical study should ask who taught whom, how a pattern changed, whether a source was police restraint, torture, fantasy, stage, private play, or photography, and what safety/consent changes are required now. Rope bondage carries histories of slavery, coercion, punishment, racism, and sexual exploitation alongside craft and art. Learning lineage therefore includes correcting myths, crediting innovators, and refusing to romanticize violence.

### Aesthetic study

Source: https://rope365.com/aesthetics/

Explore minimal restraint, deliberate visual chaos, and imperfection informed by kuzushi or wabi-sabi; draw geometry that responds to the body's form; add negotiated ornaments; stage a scene with clothing, architecture, and light; or create a credited tribute. Aesthetic “mistakes” may be accepted visually but never when they alter load, breath, nerve pressure, or release.

### Furniture and poles

Source: https://rope365.com/furniture/

Begin object bondage by sitting on a stable chair or other furniture and identifying structural—not decorative—members, tip direction, pinch points, sharp edges, and exits. The source offers only this opening exercise; it does not provide engineering criteria for furniture as a human-load anchor.

Source: https://rope365.com/bamboo-postures/

A pole can spread arms in a cruciform, sit behind elbows in a waitress-like pose, rest behind knees as a movable “trapeze,” press across hips/belly as a lever, or run between legs. Behind-knee pressure threatens peroneal branches; belly leverage affects organs and breath; crotch contact needs explicit agreement. Different lengths and diameters change leverage. Tie the pole with known lashings, inspect splinters and ends, and never equate a bamboo body prop with a rated suspension beam.

### Deliberate pain

Source: https://rope365.com/pain/

Pain preferences are sensation-specific and intensity raises injury risk. Studies include abrasive skin drag, tight muscle compression, very thin twine, nipple capture, hot/cold rope, wet rope, and kneeling on textured objects. A stick twisted inside a wrap acts as a Spanish-windlass-like tension multiplier and can create nerve-damaging pressure abruptly. Water may shrink rope, reduce swimming, and turn rescue into a seconds-scale problem. Heat, wax, flame, ice, and circulation restriction add burns or tissue injury. Progress in small reversible steps, keep pressure off nerves, and never confuse high pain tolerance with tissue tolerance.

## Intimate, psychological, predicament, and group practice

### Crotch and head

Source: https://rope365.com/crotch/

Negotiate direct and indirect genital contact, stimulation, fluids, hygiene, partner exclusivity, and desired pressure. Vary crotch-line thickness and placement, add controlled tension or knots, secure a vibrator or dildo in a purpose-built harness, tie external genital tissue only with extreme care, or design covering/chastity. Tissue, hair, circulation, urethra, and small sensory nerves make local injury possible; keep lines observable and immediately removable.

Source: https://rope365.com/head/

Head wrapping must solve a round, slippery shape without migrating to eyes, jaw, airway, or neck. Mouth play may involve rope, cloth, a bit-like stick, or jaw positioning and requires hygiene plus a non-verbal signal. Sensory play may add or remove sight, sound, smell, or taste; never block both breathing routes. Lines can connect head to a limb or form a cage; hair can be incorporated with load spread across a broad section. Tongue tying and forced eye/nostril ideas are exceptionally fragile and under-described in the source; the page supplies prompts, not safe step sequences.

### Mind and predicaments

Source: https://rope365.com/mind/

Psychological play can use anticipation, voice, isolation, consensual humiliation, objectification, fear, mirrors, shame, or beauty. Mental-health self-awareness and communication matter as much as physical technique. Define triggers, desired emotional direction, how to stop without shame, aftercare, and delayed check-in. Fear sometimes signals real danger rather than a fantasy to overcome.

Source: https://rope365.com/predicaments/

A predicament links choices: moving relieves one line but loads another, maintaining a posture avoids a different sensation, opposing anchors pull in several directions, a weight must be moved, or movement activates stimulation or a consequence. Hair, toes, crotch, inserts, breakable objects, and self-choking sharply increase consequence. Because movement changes every load unpredictably, watch continuously and keep cutters and releases accessible. The self-choking prompt is documented but not safe to infer into a construction; neck compression can become fatal before a complex tie is released.

### More than one person

Source: https://rope365.com/tied-together/

For two or more tied people, negotiation must cover contact between every pair, not only with the tyer. Plan what happens if two people need release simultaneously. Studies include securing an embrace, random limb combinations, geometric body shapes, “twins” who move together, symmetric tying of two people, transitions, and interdependent predicaments. A participant's movement can unexpectedly load another's neck, joint, or balance, so separate emergency exits are preferable.

Source: https://rope365.com/games/

Group games include a bound crawl/sprint, body-safe tug of war, mouth-held drawing, a telephone game that copies unseen ties, timed untying, alternating “rigger chess,” and a web connecting everyone. Establish shared safety rules, individual boundaries, spotters, and a way for anyone to end the game without losing. Avoid genital/insertion tug-of-war variants suggested as intensity options; competition and spectators make it especially easy to override pain or consent.

### Review and sharing

Source: https://rope365.com/sharing/

After sustained practice, review what changed in skill, desire, boundaries, fears, and risk profile. Teaching, making a tutorial, photography, performance, life drawing, and community work demand deeper understanding and explicit consent for recording or audiences. Revisit elementary wraps and columns with new awareness. Continue learning after becoming a teacher, credit sources and partners, and do not present a personal solution as universal.

### Partial coverage

- **Exact knot hand sequences:** the public text usually states topology, function, direction, tradeoffs, and checks, but many movements are in photographs or video. This corpus does not invent turns that were not present in text. It therefore supports conceptual comparison and inspection better than blind step-by-step reproduction.
- **Furniture and bamboo:** Rope365 supplies body-position prompts and a separate equipment essay supplies general frame safety, but furniture engineering and bamboo species/strength evaluation are not comprehensive.
- **Suspension-adjacent structures:** the equipment primer discusses rated connectors, slings, swivels, rings, plates, crash pads, and rope contact diameter; the hardpoint essay discusses frames and trees. These are component-selection and failure-mode notes, not a full suspension curriculum.
- **Historical attribution:** the site names people, publications, and periods and encourages genealogical study, but frequently notes that historical evidence is incomplete or contradictory. Hojōjutsu is presented as an indirect influence rather than a simplistic origin story.

### Not substantively covered by Rope365

- No complete suspension, partial-suspension, transition, or dynamic-drop curriculum; no suspension line-placement sequence; and no rescue-lowering procedure.
- No quantitative upline system design: working-load limits, safety factors, rope strength loss at bends/knots, redundancy calculations, fall factors, shock-load models, hauling ratios beyond the basic pulley concept, or lifecycle inspection criteria are not developed.
- No engineering method for evaluating ceiling joists, concrete/steel anchors, drilled or chemical fasteners, tension-between-walls/ceiling systems, door-frame anchors, or unknown indoor hardpoints. A qualified structural professional and current rated-system documentation are needed; visual inspection alone is insufficient.
- No exhaustive certification standard or product test record for frames, connectors, rings, textiles, bamboo, or trees. Product prices and volatile shopping links were intentionally removed.
- No image-derived reconstruction of “coming soon” material, galleries, silent demonstrations, or video-only steps. Media-only prompts remain identified as gaps.
- No medical diagnosis or treatment protocol. The anatomy pages are educational, sometimes explicitly works in progress, and current clinician/first-aid guidance takes precedence.
