# Evidence digest: controlled static tests of eight loop-knot families (2020)

## Use boundary: laboratory evidence, not a safety recipe

This is a manually curated digest of **expert-tied, deliberately dressed loop knots tested slowly to rupture on synthetic polyamide low-stretch kernmantle rope**. “Expert-tied” here describes the authors’ controlled research setting, their stated emphasis on correct tying and dressing, and their photographic documentation; the paper does not identify every tyer or quantify between-tyer variation. The evidence is static and specimen-specific. [APP-138-404: abstract; introduction; §2 Material and Methods]

The study does not validate natural-fiber rope, bondage rope, body contact, dynamic loading, cyclic loading, uplines, or human suspension. It does not supply a safe-knot rule, a load or rating recipe, retirement guidance, or permission to transplant a laboratory ranking into another rope system. The authors themselves identify low-speed static loading, low-stretch rope, and a restricted set of loop knots as boundaries requiring further research. [APP-138-404: §3.4 Future Research]

Numerical knot-efficiency values and rankings are intentionally absent. They would look portable even though the paper’s central statistical finding is that efficiency changes with the rope population being tested. Source figures, source tables, tying diagrams, standards text, trademarks, vendor claims, equipment brands, and operational recommendations are also excluded. [APP-138-404: introduction; §3.3.1; §3.3.4; §3.3.9]

## The tested material, preserved without trademarks

The authors tested polyamide low-stretch kernmantle ropes that the source classified under two types of the EN 1891 standard, plus a polyamide accessory cord classified under EN 564. Naming those classifications identifies the specimens; no standards requirements or manufacturer declarations are reproduced. The article reports a mix of new and previously used ropes made by similar technology. [APP-138-404: §2.1 Ropes; Tables I–II]

The exact neutral specimen inventory is:

- **G:** type A low-stretch kernmantle rope, 10.5 mm, manufactured in 2015, new and unused. This was the common reference rope on which all eight knot families were tested. [APP-138-404: Table I; §3.3.9]
- **B:** type A low-stretch kernmantle rope, 10.5 mm, manufactured in 2006. The table supplies no mission or wear-stage entry for this specimen. [APP-138-404: Table I]
- **EW:** type A low-stretch kernmantle rope, 10.5 mm, manufactured in 2014, previously used for work at height while painting vertical structures; the source records color affecting both sheath and core. [APP-138-404: Table I]
- **L1:** type A low-stretch kernmantle rope, 11.0 mm, manufactured in 2006, previously used for speleological rescue; the table supplies no separate wear-stage entry. [APP-138-404: Table I]
- **ER:** type A low-stretch kernmantle rope, 10.5 mm, manufactured in 2008, previously used for speleological rescue; its sheath was worn but the source reported no defect penetrating to the core. [APP-138-404: Table I]
- **L2:** type B low-stretch kernmantle rope, 9.1 mm, manufactured in 2004, previously used for mountain rescue; the table supplies no separate wear-stage entry. [APP-138-404: Table I]
- **G8:** polyamide accessory cord, 8 mm, manufactured during 2015–2018, new and unused. It was used only in a restricted cross-load comparison alongside one rope. [APP-138-404: Table II; §3.3.8]

The codes above are the article’s abbreviations, not product recommendations. Manufacturer names, product trademarks, declared breaking strengths, impact-force values, fall counts, mass, elongation, shrinkage, sheath-slip values, and construction recipes are omitted. A manufacturing year or prior mission is a specimen-history field, not evidence for a storage life, retirement interval, or present safety condition. [APP-138-404: Tables I–II; §2.1 Ropes]

The article says its ropes spanned roughly one to twelve years of age at testing and included work-at-height, speleological-rescue, and mountain-rescue histories. Those histories were not randomized treatments, and the specimens differed in diameter, construction type, wear, and manufacturer. The old-versus-new observations therefore cannot isolate calendar age from use history or other rope properties. This digest retains the inventory but excludes the paper’s storage and lifespan claims. [APP-138-404: §2.1 Ropes; §3.1 Rope Without a Knot]

## Measurement and tying controls

The tests used a horizontal, hydraulic tensile apparatus in stable laboratory conditions. Opposed cylindrical friction grips held the rope without a knot at the grip; for relevant loop tests a polished steel ring represented the attachment. The moving grip advanced at 180 mm per minute, making this a slow-static displacement test rather than an impact or fall-arrest simulation. [APP-138-404: §2.2 Tensile Tests]

Force was measured with load sensors connected to a control system. The machine was independently calibrated every two years, and the stated relative measurement error was below two tenths of one percent over the force range used. Calibration limits instrument error; it does not remove specimen variability, tying variability, fixture effects, or model error. [APP-138-404: §2.2 Tensile Tests]

The authors used one methodology across the knot families, emphasized correct tying and dressing, and retained photographic documentation. They distinguished loading direction, the I and O dressed geometries, and rupture from untying. The paper does not report a blinded tying protocol, named tyers, inter-tyer replication, a quantified dressing force, or a direct experiment on dressing quality. Its future-research section explicitly says the effect of improper dressing had not been seriously measured. [APP-138-404: introduction; terminology; §3.4 Future Research]

## Why repeated measurements and ratio statistics matter

Straight-rope breaking strength varied substantially between nearby pieces of nominally the same rope. The authors therefore treated both unknotted and knotted breaking strengths as distributions rather than fixed numbers. A single rupture result cannot characterize either population, and comparisons assembled from different ropes or test methods are not exchangeable. [APP-138-404: introduction; §3.1 Rope Without a Knot]

Knot efficiency is a ratio: the breaking strength of a knotted specimen divided by the breaking strength of its unknotted rope population. The two inputs are directly measured, but the efficiency distribution is derived. The authors modeled each strength population, derived the distribution of their ratio, and used a central confidence interval from that ratio distribution. Because the ratio distribution is asymmetric, the mean efficiency is not generally the ratio of the two sample means. [APP-138-404: §3.2 Statistical Properties of Knot Efficiency]

This distinction is not cosmetic. A ratio-of-means shortcut can bias the result, especially when either strength population is variable. The paper’s uncertainty intervals and replication counts must remain attached to each rope, knot, geometry, and loading comparison. Results from separate specimen populations cannot be combined merely because they share a knot name. [APP-138-404: introduction; §3.2 Statistical Properties of Knot Efficiency]

For the figure-eight and overhand families, the authors found evidence that measured efficiency decreased as the tested rope’s unknotted static breaking strength increased. They did not establish this relationship for every loop knot, bend, or hitch. The finding directly defeats the idea of one context-free efficiency number for a knot. [APP-138-404: §3.3.1 Figure Eight Loop; §3.3.4 Overhand Loop; §3.4 Future Research]

## Loading and geometry labels

In the paper’s **standard-load** arrangement, opposing forces acted through the standing part and the loop. In **cross-load**, the principal opposition was between the standing part and working end, leaving the loop comparatively lightly loaded and changing the internal stress path. These are experimental boundary conditions, not instructions for loading a knot in practice. [APP-138-404: §3.3 Loop Knots in General]

The authors also separated two dressed forms called **geometry I** and **geometry O**. The labels encode whether the standing part or working end occupies the inside position where they turn around the loop collar. They are structural labels for already dressed specimens, not step-by-step tying directions. Some knot families permit both forms; for the bowline as tested, the paper did not apply the I/O distinction. [APP-138-404: §3.3 Loop Knots in General; §3.3.5 Bowline]

The paper additionally used **ring-load** for forces applied through the loop. This condition matters because a specimen may reorganize and untie rather than rupture. Combining rupture and untying into one “strength” endpoint would compare different failure processes. [APP-138-404: introduction; §3.3 Loop Knots in General; §3.3.7 Bowline on a Bight]

## Eight loop-knot families and the bounded evidence

### 1. Figure-eight loop

The figure-eight loop was tested under standard load in I and O geometries and under cross-load. The I/O standard-load comparison used 81 measurements across five ropes and did not resolve a difference between geometries at the authors’ chosen significance level. A broader standard-load program used eleven ropes and revealed enough between-rope variation to motivate the efficiency-versus-rope-strength relationship. These results describe the tested populations, not a universal retained-strength value. [APP-138-404: §3.3.1 Figure Eight Loop]

### 2. Double figure-eight loop

The double figure-eight loop was tested under standard load in both geometries. The I/O comparison used 22 measurements on one rope and did not resolve a geometry difference. A 44-measurement comparison on that same rope did not distinguish the double-loop family from the reference figure-eight condition. One-rope equivalence at the experiment’s resolution is not proof of equivalence on other ropes or under other loading. [APP-138-404: §3.3.2 Double Figure Eight Loop]

### 3. Figure-nine loop

The figure-nine loop was tested under standard load in I and O geometries. A set of 19 measurements on one rope produced a detectable geometry difference. The authors themselves called for independent verification with multiple ropes. The source’s numerical ranking and operational recommendation are excluded because the geometry result is based on one rope population and slow-static loading. [APP-138-404: §3.3.3 Figure Nine Loop]

### 4. Overhand loop

The overhand loop was tested under standard load in both geometries and under cross-load. Its I/O comparison used 24 measurements on one rope and found a detectable geometry difference, again with a call for independent multi-rope verification. A wider standard-load analysis used 108 measurements across five ropes; high dispersion in used-rope results prevented some distinctions. A separate cross-load comparison with the figure-eight family used 48 measurements across three ropes and did not resolve a difference at the chosen threshold. [APP-138-404: §3.3.4 Overhand Loop]

### 5. Bowline

The bowline was tested under standard load and ring-load. In the ring-load tests it did not provide a rupture endpoint: the knot untied. Once the specimen outcome is untying, a rupture-based efficiency calculation is not the relevant statistic. The paper’s force thresholds and life-critical recommendations are excluded. [APP-138-404: §3.3.5 Bowline; §3.3.7 Bowline on a Bight]

### 6. Left-hand bowline

The left-hand bowline was likewise tested under standard load and ring-load. It also untied under ring-load rather than breaking. Its untying process differed quantitatively from the bowline’s, but those force values are not portable ratings and are omitted. The experiment supports keeping “untied” distinct from “broke”; it does not establish safe use under ring-load or cyclic conditions. [APP-138-404: §3.3.6 Left-Hand Bowline; §3.3.7 Bowline on a Bight]

### 7. Bowline on a bight

The bowline on a bight was tested under standard load in geometry I. A 51-measurement comparison did not distinguish the standard-load efficiencies of the bowline, left-hand bowline, and bowline on a bight at the selected threshold. A larger comparison against the reference family drew on 101 measurements across three ropes, but the numerical differences and rankings are excluded. The limited tested geometry and rope populations remain part of the result. [APP-138-404: §3.3.7 Bowline on a Bight]

### 8. Alpine butterfly loop

The alpine butterfly loop was tested only under cross-load. Its main comparison used 32 measurements on one rope and one accessory cord; a narrower direct comparison used 18 measurements on the same rope. Because the family lacked a standard-load program and broad multi-rope replication, neither the paper’s ranking nor its field recommendation is retained. [APP-138-404: §3.3.8 Alpine Butterfly Knot]

## Infrared and electron-microscopy observations

The authors recorded rupture with high-speed infrared imaging and separately measured the emissivity of the whole reference rope and its core so that camera signal could be interpreted as surface temperature. Before rupture, the warmest observed region of standard-loaded loops was near the entry of the standing part. At or immediately after rupture, localized surface temperatures approached the melting region of polyamide. [APP-138-404: §2.3 High-Speed Infrared Thermal Imaging; §3.3.10 Electron Microscopy and Thermal Imaging]

The temporal resolution was not sufficient to determine whether the rapid temperature rise caused rupture, rupture caused the rise, or both processes interacted. The article explicitly leaves that causal order unresolved. Therefore the thermal observation is a near-failure mechanism clue, not evidence for a usable temperature limit, inspection threshold, or body-contact prediction. [APP-138-404: §3.3.10 Electron Microscopy and Thermal Imaging]

Electron microscopy compared intact polyamide microfilaments with filaments torn in tensile tests. Intact fibers appeared cylindrical; broken ends were thickened and showed flake-like surface structure. The authors proposed that rapid heating, deformation, friction, and cooling could form those ends, but presented the explanation as a hypothesis. Product-specific microscope settings, images, and dimensional measurements are excluded. [APP-138-404: §2.4 Electron Microscopy; §3.3.10 Electron Microscopy and Thermal Imaging]

## What the experiment does not establish

The paper covers loop knots, not bends, hitches, or friction hitches. It focuses on low-stretch polyamide kernmantle ropes and one accessory cord, not dynamic climbing rope or natural fiber. Its grip moved slowly; the effect of realistic faster or impact loading remained unknown. Diameter, wetness, knotability, sheath smoothness, dressing quality, and other candidate variables were not systematically isolated. [APP-138-404: §3.4 Future Research]

The static-breaking-strength relationship was demonstrated only for the figure-eight and overhand families. Several geometry findings came from one rope; the alpine-butterfly program used only one rope and one cord; all-family comparisons shared a single new reference rope. Ring-load untying is a different endpoint from rupture, and comments in the article about cyclic behavior do not turn these slow-static tests into cyclic validation. [APP-138-404: §3.3.1–§3.3.9; §3.4 Future Research]

The study is valuable as a model of careful classification: identify the exact rope population and history, control dressing and loading geometry, calibrate the apparatus, replicate, propagate ratio uncertainty, and record the failure mode. It is not a lookup table for selecting a knot or assigning a retained-strength percentage. [APP-138-404: introduction; §2 Material and Methods; §3.2 Statistical Properties]
