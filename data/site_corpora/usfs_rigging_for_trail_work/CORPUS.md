# Trail-rigging mechanics: a bounded technical digest

Source identity: David E. Michael, Jedediah J. Talbot, and John S. Glenn, *Rigging for Trail Work: Principles, Techniques, and Lessons from the Backcountry*, USDA Forest Service, National Technology and Development Program, August 2024, publication 2223-2806-NTDP. Citations below use the printed manual page, not the PDF viewer page.

Corpus status: non-Q&A, manually synthesized training text. The canonical government-package URL is retained in the manifest rather than taught as a fact.

## Scope boundary

This is a digest of a Forest Service manual about moving trail-work materials. It is not a human-suspension manual. It does not certify bondage rope, a human-suspension system, a tree, a limb, a ceiling, a joist, a beam, an indoor hardpoint, a frame, a tripod, an anchor, or any item of hardware. No numerical value below is a design value for suspending a person. Do not infer indoor-hardpoint adequacy or tree adequacy from this digest. Human-support systems require standards, equipment, competent inspection, and structural or rigging expertise appropriate to that application. Manufacturer instructions and a qualified evaluation control over this summary.

The source repeatedly treats rigging as high-consequence work with potential for serious injury or death. It calls for an adequately trained and experienced lead rigger and says a reference manual cannot replace training. Its examples, design-factor preferences, field heuristics, and regulatory references belong to trail and material rigging unless a cited authority explicitly says otherwise. (USDA manual, pp. 1–2)

The digest preserves transferable mechanics—force direction, line tension, bend geometry, ratings, inspection, planning, communication, and failure-zone reasoning—while omitting construction recipes for improvised anchors and support frames. It also omits figures, photographs, expressive tables, vendor material, and product promotion. Numeric facts are restated only when they illuminate the mechanics and retain their assumptions.

## A system-first mental model

Rigging is a system, not a bag of independently strong parts. A useful analysis begins with the load and its intended path, then follows every segment that carries or redirects force: attachment, line, sling, connector, block or device, anchor, supporting structure, braking element, and operator interface. Each change in direction can change both the magnitude and direction of the load seen elsewhere. The least capable part or configuration limits the system. (USDA manual, pp. 1–2, 61–65, 67–69)

A conservative plan does four things before loading:

1. identifies every load-bearing component and its condition;
2. determines the maximum credible force and direction at each component;
3. verifies that the component is used within its manufacturer-rated configuration and working load limit;
4. assumes something can fail and keeps people out of the resulting paths of motion. (USDA manual, pp. 67–69, 79–82)

Fail-safe thinking is broader than adding a large number to a strength calculation. It includes predictable load paths, redundant or controlled consequences where appropriate, compatible components, an escape route, a stop command, and a plan for a slipped load, lost anchor, severed line, jammed device, or communication failure. A design factor cannot compensate for an unknown component, an unsuitable structure, poor orientation, hidden damage, or a person standing in the failure path. (USDA manual, pp. 1–2, 79–82, 105–108)

## Core vocabulary

**Force** has magnitude and direction. A number without a direction is not a complete description of a rigging load. Forces that meet at a connector, block, or support must be combined as vectors. (USDA manual, pp. 61–65, 127–168)

**Static load** is applied without significant acceleration. **Dynamic load** changes through motion or acceleration. **Shock load** occurs when slack or motion is stopped abruptly. The manual gives broad field estimates—ordinary dynamic effects may add roughly 10 to 30 percent to a static load, while a severe shock can multiply it far more—but these are warnings about uncertainty, not design multipliers or human-suspension values. Preventing slack and sudden stops is more reliable than guessing a shock factor. (USDA manual, pp. 61–63)

**Minimum breaking strength (MBS)** is a new-product laboratory value supplied for a defined component or rope construction. It is not a working recommendation, and it does not describe worn, knotted, wet, chemically exposed, heat-damaged, or poorly configured gear. (USDA manual, pp. 15–17, 50–53)

**Working load limit (WLL)** is the manufacturer's maximum recommended load for a component in a specified configuration. Different hitches, directions, angles, bend radii, terminations, and environments can change the applicable limit. The marked WLL is useful only when the component can be positively identified and its instructions are available. (USDA manual, pp. 15–17, 23–30, 50–53)

**Design factor** is the ratio between breaking strength and the planned working load:

`design factor = MBS / planned load`

or, when a design factor is selected for a defined application:

`allowable working load = MBS / design factor`

The source discusses factors from 5:1 through 12:1 and generally prefers 10:1 for its own fiber-rope trail applications. That preference depends on material-handling context, rope history, abrasion, knots, personnel exposure, and Forest Service practice. It is not a universal rule and does not validate a human-support system. (USDA manual, pp. 15–17, 50–53)

**Mechanical advantage (MA)** is the ratio by which a machine multiplies an input force. Ideal MA trades a longer input travel for a smaller input force. Real systems produce less advantage because rope bending, sheave bearings, surface contact, and devices add friction. (USDA manual, pp. 63–65)

**Block** means the shell and attachment that enclose one or more pulley sheaves. A **sheave** is the grooved wheel that supports the moving line. A directional block changes the line's direction; a moving-block arrangement can also provide mechanical advantage. The attachment carrying a block generally sees the vector sum of the entering and leaving line forces, not merely the suspended object's weight. (USDA manual, pp. 37–43, 111–115, 127–168)

**Bight** can mean a curved section or loop of rope. In rigging language it also names the dangerous region created by tensioned lines and their included angle. A person inside that region can be struck or trapped if the line, load, anchor, or connector moves. (USDA manual, pp. 79–82, 111)

**Fly zone** is the area into which a tensioned line, connected component, or load may travel during failure. It is not just a thin line behind an anchor. It may include a teardrop-shaped recoil region, an angle swept by a redirect, the bight, and the load's possible roll, swing, fall, or slide path. (USDA manual, pp. 79–82, 112)

**D/d ratio** compares a sheave or bend diameter `D` with rope diameter `d`. A larger ratio generally bends the rope less sharply. Acceptable values depend on the exact rope and device, so a generic ratio is a screening clue, not an approval. (USDA manual, pp. 39–43)

## Selecting fiber rope

Diameter alone does not identify rope strength. Two ropes of the same diameter can differ greatly because of fiber, construction, heat treatment, strand geometry, coating, condition, and intended use. Selection therefore starts with a manufacturer specification for the exact product and an application that the manufacturer supports. Record the rope's identity and history rather than relying on color or feel. (USDA manual, pp. 11–17)

Important selection properties include:

- breaking strength and the manufacturer's working guidance;
- elongation under both ordinary and peak load;
- abrasion resistance and the ability to inspect abrasion;
- heat resistance, including friction-generated heat;
- response to water, ultraviolet exposure, chemicals, dirt, and repeated cycling;
- flexibility, knot or termination behavior, and minimum bend radius;
- tendency to rotate, flatten, creep, or recoil;
- compatibility with sheaves, grooves, connectors, and braking devices. (USDA manual, pp. 11–17)

### Construction changes behavior

A single braid is built as one braided structure and may resist rotation. A double braid uses a braided core with a braided cover; depending on the product, both structures or mainly the core may carry load. A laid rope commonly has three helically twisted strands. Tighter, harder lay can improve wear resistance while making the rope less flexible and harder to knot. A plaited rope interlaces paired strands, often in an eight-strand pattern, to produce a flexible, rotation-resistant line. A kernmantle or sheath-and-core rope puts most load in an inner core and uses an outer woven sheath for protection and handling. Construction names describe architecture, not fitness for an application. (USDA manual, pp. 11–13)

Inspection must match construction. Surface inspection alone cannot reveal every core defect in a double braid or kernmantle rope. Conversely, a damaged sheath can be a retirement condition even when a core is not visibly broken because the sheath protects, positions, or shares load according to the design. Follow the product maker's inspection and retirement criteria. (USDA manual, pp. 18–20)

### Material comparisons are contextual

The manual names flax, jute, abaca, sisal, hemp, and cotton as plant-derived rope fibers, then says natural-fiber rope is comparatively weaker and unsuitable for the trail-rigging loads in that manual. This is a statement about its material-moving application. It does not establish whether any particular natural-fiber rope is suitable or unsuitable for bondage, and it does not supersede the construction, condition, and purpose of a specific rope. (USDA manual, pp. 13–15)

The source characterizes nylon as strong and elastic, with good energy absorption but potentially hazardous recoil. It reports that some nylon loses about 10 to 15 percent of strength when wet. That range is product-dependent: it must not be assigned to every nylon rope without a manufacturer statement. (USDA manual, pp. 13–15)

Polyester is described as lower-stretch than nylon, with good abrasion, ultraviolet, and wet-condition performance. Those useful general tendencies still do not identify a product's rating or compatibility. (USDA manual, pp. 13–15)

Polypropylene is lightweight and floats, but the source flags ultraviolet degradation, relatively low melting temperature, and poor suitability where line or device friction can produce heat. High-modulus polyethylene (HMPE) offers high strength for its mass and relatively little recoil, while raising separate concerns about abrasion and heat. Manufacturer data are necessary because blends, covers, coatings, and constructions can materially change these traits. (USDA manual, pp. 13–15)

The disciplined conclusion is not that one fiber is always best. It is that material, construction, device geometry, environment, and failure behavior have to be evaluated together for the actual task.

## Ratings, units, and condition

The source uses pounds-force and kilonewtons. Approximate conversions useful for checking an order of magnitude are:

- `1 kilogram ≈ 2.2 pounds` for mass conversion near Earth's surface;
- `1 kilonewton ≈ 224.8 pounds-force`;
- `45 kilonewtons ≈ 10,116 pounds-force`. (USDA manual, pp. 15–17)

Conversions do not add precision. A rounded load estimate, an uncertain angle, and an aged rope remain uncertain after conversion. Keep mass and force concepts distinct, retain the original units, and state whether a value is measured, calculated, rated, estimated, or merely illustrative.

An MBS-to-WLL calculation is meaningful only if its inputs describe the actual component. Common invalid shortcuts include:

- using diameter to guess MBS;
- treating a catalog MBS as a worn rope's remaining strength;
- applying a straight-line rating to a knot, tight bend, choker, side load, or incompatible sheave;
- assuming several anchors or sling legs share force equally;
- adding a second component and calling the system redundant without analyzing common failure modes;
- treating a regulatory factor from one industry as a universal design rule. (USDA manual, pp. 15–17, 23–30, 50–53, 127–168)

The manual mentions a 5:1 criterion in connection with a particular OSHA construction-rigging provision. A regulation has a defined scope. Its appearance in this manual is not authority to apply it to recreational human suspension, entertainment flying, arboriculture, rescue, fall protection, or any other domain. (USDA manual, pp. 50–53)

## Knots, bends, and terminations

A knot, bend, splice, eye, or hardware termination changes how fibers share load. It introduces curvature and often concentrates pressure. In general, a tighter bend radius produces higher local stress and greater strength loss, but the actual retained strength depends on rope construction, material, knot, dressing, loading pattern, and test method. The source provides a comparative knot table; this digest deliberately does not turn those percentages into universal constants. (USDA manual, pp. 16–18)

For a load-bearing knot, use **tie, dress, set**:

- Tie the intended structure correctly.
- Dress it so strands follow the intended path without hidden crossings, capsizing, or accidental extra turns.
- Set it under controlled tension so slack is removed and the final geometry can be inspected. (USDA manual, pp. 16–18)

Correct initial tying is not the end of inspection. Cyclic loading can tighten, creep, roll, or loosen a knot. Adequate tails, a suitable backup when required by the system, and repeated inspection are part of the termination. A knot that is difficult to untie may also hide damage after use. The source advises against storing load-bearing rope in knots because prolonged tight bends and concealed contamination complicate inspection. (USDA manual, pp. 16–20)

An eye protected by a correctly sized thimble reduces chafing and maintains a supported bend around a connector. A thimble does not repair an incompatible connector, overload, poor splice, or side-loaded attachment. Splices and manufactured terminations must match the rope and should be made or verified to the applicable specification. (USDA manual, pp. 43–45)

End finishing varies by fiber. The manual discusses heat-based finishing for some synthetics; this digest omits that procedure because the wrong heat treatment can damage fibers or create a hidden weak zone. Finish and retire rope according to the product maker and application-specific practice.

## Rope handling, storage, inspection, and retirement

Rope management preserves both condition and situational awareness. Pay out and recover a line using a method appropriate to its construction so twists and kinks are not forced into it. Keep rope away from grit, sharp edges, moving machinery, heat, chemicals, and uncontrolled traffic. Store it clean, dry, ventilated, protected from sunlight, and separated from substances that could contaminate it. Do not stand on it or drag it unnecessarily across soil. (USDA manual, pp. 18–20)

Before, during, and after use, examine the entire line rather than only the most convenient section. Look and feel for:

- cuts, broken yarns, glazing, fuzzing, flat spots, and abrasion;
- hard, soft, lumpy, narrowed, or unusually stiff regions;
- raveling, hockling, twist, distortion, and core movement;
- discoloration, melting, chemical attack, or a burnt or unusual odor;
- embedded grit or a powdery interior;
- inconsistent diameter or a sheath that no longer sits normally on the core;
- damaged eyes, stitching, splices, labels, and end terminations;
- unknown history or any event outside the product's intended loading. (USDA manual, pp. 18–20)

The source gives field heuristics concerning percentages of visibly broken fibers and signs after wet or oily loading. Those heuristics are not reproduced as universal retirement thresholds. Modern rope makers publish construction-specific criteria, and uncertainty itself can justify quarantine. Inspection cannot calculate remaining strength with precision.

When a load-bearing rope is retired, mark it and separate it immediately so it cannot return to service by mistake. If disposal is required, render it unmistakably unusable for load bearing. A rope log can record identity, manufacture and in-service dates, construction, dimensions, MBS, use, inspections, possible damage, and condition comments; the log supplements rather than replaces hands-on inspection. (USDA manual, pp. 15–20, 105–108, 123–124)

## Slings and load attachment

A sling's capacity depends on its material, construction, hitch, angle, bend, edge protection, temperature, environment, and condition. A tag or permanent identification should connect the item to its manufacturer and configuration-specific ratings. Color alone is unreliable because color conventions vary among makers and products. (USDA manual, pp. 23–30)

Common configurations behave differently:

- A vertical hitch carries a load on one direct sling leg.
- A choker passes through or around itself and tightens on the load, changing both sling capacity and load control.
- A basket supports the load with two portions of the sling, but only favorable geometry and a stable load permit the expected sharing.
- Multi-leg bridles divide load according to geometry and stiffness; they do not automatically divide it equally. (USDA manual, pp. 23–30, 129–133)

The load's center of gravity should remain below and under control of the support geometry. If an attachment is off-center, the object can rotate until its center of gravity moves under the effective support. Rotation can shock the system, loosen a choke, move an edge protector, or put an unplanned direction into a connector. (USDA manual, pp. 27–30, 63–65)

Protect synthetic slings from cuts and abrasion at edges. Protection should stay in place under the expected motion and remain removable so the sling can be inspected. Padding changes contact; it does not certify the underlying anchor, edge, or sling. Remove a synthetic sling from service when its load-bearing core is exposed or when required identification is missing or illegible, subject to the maker's criteria. (USDA manual, pp. 23–30)

The manual covers wire-rope and chain slings as well. Those sections are not converted into technique instructions here because their inspection, terminations, and ratings require material-specific training. The transferable rule is to use only identified, compatible, inspected lifting components in exactly the configurations supported by their manufacturers.

## Blocks, sheaves, redirects, and connectors

A block may change direction, provide mechanical advantage, or both. Selection must account for rope material and diameter, sheave diameter, groove profile, bearing efficiency, attachment rating, side plates, connector orientation, and the force created at the support. A block strong enough in one direction can be unsuitable when tilted, twisted, or side loaded. (USDA manual, pp. 37–43)

The sheave groove should support the intended rope without pinching it or allowing it to bear only at narrow points. A groove that is too small can squeeze and abrade the rope. One that is too large can fail to support the rope evenly. Synthetic rope can flatten, so compatibility cannot be judged from nominal diameter alone. (USDA manual, pp. 39–43)

The source offers broad trail-work fallback ranges: a sheave diameter around 16 to 20 rope diameters for some wire rope and about 5 to 10 diameters for some fiber rope. It also estimates roughly 10 percent friction loss per sheave in field planning. These are heuristics, not cross-domain specifications. The rope and device manufacturers' limits and measured system behavior control. (USDA manual, pp. 39–43, 63–65)

A shackle or clevis is a U-shaped connector closed by a pin. It should be positively identified, inspected, and loaded in an approved orientation. Side loading can sharply reduce capacity or permanently distort the body and pin. The manual illustrates approximate reductions of 30 percent at 45 degrees and 50 percent at 90 degrees, but reductions vary by model and manufacturer. Those examples are not a generic derating formula. (USDA manual, pp. 45–48)

Rigging plates can organize several attachments and reduce connector crowding or unintended side loading when used as rated. They do not make attached lines independent, guarantee equal load sharing, or certify the structure behind the plate. (USDA manual, pp. 48–50)

Friction and braking devices can increase control as the line contacts more surface, but the exact holding force and heat depend on the device, rope, wraps, angle, speed, operator, contamination, and manufacturer instructions. More wraps can also make feeding difficult or accumulate damaging heat. A lowering system needs enough controllable range for both the expected load and credible variation. (USDA manual, pp. 50–56, 99–103)

Avoid unmarked or improvised load-bearing hardware. Markings should be legible enough to connect a part to its rating and instructions. Inspect for deformation, cracks, corrosion, sharp edges, worn pins, damaged threads, incomplete closure, and evidence of overload. A component that cannot be identified or whose history is unknown cannot inherit a rating merely because it looks similar to a rated part. (USDA manual, pp. 43–53)

## Force, work, mechanical advantage, and friction

Mechanical advantage conserves work in an idealized system. If an ideal arrangement reduces the input force by a factor of four, the operator must pull approximately four times as much line for the same load travel. Real input is higher because every bend, bearing, and contact adds loss. (USDA manual, pp. 61–65)

`work = force × distance`

This relationship helps detect impossible expectations. A compact device cannot reduce effort, reduce travel, eliminate friction, and preserve speed all at once. A practical plan also accounts for the force needed to accelerate the load, ground or surface resistance, line stiffness, and the input needed to lower or brake safely. (USDA manual, pp. 61–65)

The number of rope parts that visibly support a moving block can estimate ideal mechanical advantage, but it does not alone determine anchor force or operator force. The standing end location, redirect angles, friction, and whether a component moves all matter. Trace each line segment and draw force arrows before assigning a number. (USDA manual, pp. 61–65, 127–168)

Smooth loading is a control measure. Acceleration requires additional force, while sudden deceleration can produce shock. Keeping slack out of a loaded system, taking up tension gradually, and stopping before a component reaches an obstacle reduce uncertainty and help observers notice movement or deformation. (USDA manual, pp. 61–63, 79–82)

## Angle geometry and line tension

Trigonometry makes the cost of shallow line angles visible. The source uses:

`sin(angle) = opposite / hypotenuse`

`cos(angle) = adjacent / hypotenuse`

`tan(angle) = opposite / adjacent` (USDA manual, pp. 127–129)

For a symmetric sling with `n` identical legs, each making angle `θ` above the horizontal at the load and sharing a centered load `W` equally, the tension in each leg is:

`T = (W / n) × csc(θ) = W / (n × sin(θ))`

This formula assumes symmetry, equal angles, a centered static load, idealized equal sharing, and no shock. It is not valid merely because a system has several visible legs. Unequal leg length, stretch, position, motion, friction, or load attachment can invalidate equal sharing. (USDA manual, pp. 129–133)

For two symmetric legs and a total load of 1,000 pounds-force, the idealized tension in each leg is approximately:

| Angle of each leg above horizontal | Tension in each leg | Each leg as percent of total load |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 90° | 500 lb | 50% |
| 75° | 518 lb | 52% |
| 60° | 577 lb | 58% |
| 45° | 707 lb | 71% |
| 30° | 1,000 lb | 100% |
| 15° | 1,932 lb | 193% |
| 5° | 5,737 lb | 574% |

The table is a mathematical illustration, not a capacity table. As a leg approaches horizontal, its vertical component becomes a small fraction of its total tension, so the required tension rises rapidly. The same principle explains why trying to make a suspended line nearly level can create very large anchor and line loads. (USDA manual, pp. 129–133, 155–168)

For a symmetric suspended-line example with a centered load and equal line angles on both sides, the same two-leg relation applies:

`line tension on either side = W / (2 × sin(θ))`

Here `θ` is the angle each side makes above horizontal at the load if the force triangle is defined that way; always state the angle convention. More sag usually creates a steeper line angle at the load and lowers idealized line tension. Less sag moves the line toward horizontal and increases tension. Real lines add stretch, pretension, distributed self-weight, moving-load effects, and unequal geometry, so an ideal formula is a reason to seek engineering input, not a substitute for it. (USDA manual, pp. 155–168)

The manual itself warns that vector calculations are reference tools rather than the primary design basis for field systems. Variable anchors, supports, materials, installation, and site conditions can make a neat calculation falsely reassuring. Preserve uncertainty alongside every result. (USDA manual, p. 141)

## Redirect and support reactions

When a line enters and leaves a block with approximately equal tension `T`, the block attachment carries the vector sum of those two line forces. The resultant depends on the change in direction. It can approach `2T` when the two loaded segments pull nearly in the same resultant direction at the block, and it can be much smaller when their vectors oppose. Never estimate the block or anchor load by looking only at the hanging load. (USDA manual, pp. 135–153)

For a symmetric vector pair, components can be resolved with sine and cosine. Which function represents the lateral or inline component depends on where the angle is measured. Draw and label the triangle before calculating. The source contains a typographical slip on page 149: the worked geometry implies that the lateral component is `F × sin(θ)` and the inline or downward component is `F × cos(θ)` for the angle convention shown, although the surrounding formula text repeats sine. (USDA manual, pp. 147–150)

The effect of an attachment force on an upright support also depends on height. A simple bending moment about the base is:

`bending moment M = perpendicular force F × lever arm r`

Raising an attachment increases `r`, so the same lateral force produces a larger bending moment. Compression down the support and lateral bending are different failure demands. Line direction, support geometry, base restraint, guying, defects, and material properties all affect the actual response. (USDA manual, pp. 145–153)

The source has other caption, labeling, and angle-unit inconsistencies in its appendix. This digest resolves only the cases supported by the equations and worked numbers; it does not silently treat every extracted label as authoritative.

## Anchors and supporting structures: principles only

An anchor is part of a load path into a substrate or structure. Its adequacy depends on the material, condition, installation, direction of load, edge distance, geometry, environment, repeated loading, and how it is connected. Weight or apparent solidity alone does not establish holding power. (USDA manual, pp. 43–45, 71–77)

The manual discusses soil anchors, buried objects, rocks, stumps, trees, spars, A-frames, and tripods for remote trail work. Those field procedures are deliberately not reproduced here. They are not instructions for supporting people and must not be transferred into a bondage, entertainment, rescue, climbing, or indoor-hardpoint design. Freestanding and improvised support systems require engineered components, stable geometry, lateral-load control, retention against spreading or overturning, competent supervision, controlled proof or acceptance procedures, and an exclusion zone appropriate to their intended domain. (USDA manual, pp. 71–77)

For soil-dependent anchors, holding behavior can change with soil type, density, saturation, slope, installation disturbance, and load direction. For rocks, shape, seating, fracture, pivoting, and the direction of wrap matter; mass alone is not a rating. Multiple anchors will not necessarily share equally because stiffness, pretension, geometry, and movement determine which one takes load first. (USDA manual, pp. 71–75)

For living or formerly living wood, species, root architecture, decay, cracks, cavities, soil, moisture, lean, load direction, attachment height, and surrounding damage can all matter. The source offers rough field relationships between tree or stump diameter and holding power, but a visual estimate and diameter rule do not certify a tree or limb. Tree and branch assessment is a specialist task, especially when a person could be exposed. (USDA manual, pp. 71–77)

Movement detected while a system is test-loaded is evidence to stop, unload, and reassess. Absence of obvious movement is not proof that an anchor has adequate capacity or fatigue life. Monitoring is a check for some failure modes, not a strength test that validates every hidden part. (USDA manual, pp. 71–77, 79–82)

For indoor hardpoints, this source supplies no acceptance method. Ceiling finishes conceal structure; a visible eye, pipe, joist, beam, truss, slab, or fastener can be unsuitable even when it supports a static object. Loads can include multiple directions and dynamic peaks. A qualified structural evaluation and hardware installation suitable for supporting people are outside this manual and required before reliance.

## Planning a rigging operation

A useful site survey asks whether rigging makes the task safer and more controllable at all. It then records the object, estimated or measured weight, center of gravity, intended path, required lift or drag, travel distance, terrain, obstructions, weather, anchors, available equipment, operator skill, braking needs, and consequences of a stopped or failed move. No diagram can replace seeing the site. (USDA manual, pp. 67–69)

Sketch the physical layout. Label the load, anchors, line segments, blocks, connectors, slings, control or braking point, power source, and expected loading and unloading locations. Add force arrows and candidate failure paths. Record component sizes and lengths in an equipment list. A sketch makes hidden assumptions reviewable: whether a redirect increases anchor load, whether a line will rub, whether the load can rotate, whether enough travel exists, and whether an operator stands inside a bight. (USDA manual, pp. 67–69)

Define roles before tensioning. One lead coordinates the operation, but anyone should be able to call an emergency stop. Assign the person who operates the pull or lowering device, the person who watches the load, and observers for anchors or redirects. Keep responsibilities compatible with sight lines and communication; do not make one person watch several hidden hazards at once. (USDA manual, pp. 79–82, 105–108)

Plan both the intended move and the transition states: taking the initial strain, breaking friction, passing an obstacle, changing direction, stopping, lowering, unloading, and recovering gear. A system can be stable at rest and unstable while a choke tightens, a center of gravity passes an edge, or a line becomes nearly horizontal. (USDA manual, pp. 63–69)

## Preload inspection and controlled test

Inspect with the system at low or zero tension. The manual recommends tracing the whole system twice. On the first pass, examine the operation as a whole: task, load path, terrain, people, communication, escape route, line corridor, and likely failure zones. On the second, inspect every component in order: identity, condition, connection, closure, orientation, maximum anticipated force, force direction, WLL, and interaction with adjacent parts. (USDA manual, pp. 79–82)

Useful questions include:

- Is the load and its center of gravity understood?
- Can the load roll, swing, tip, drop, or snag?
- Does each part have a legible identity and application-specific rating?
- Are rope, sheave, groove, sling, and connector compatible?
- Are pins secured and connectors free of cross-load or side-load conditions?
- Are edges protected without hiding inspection-critical areas?
- Is a braking method needed if power or tension is lost?
- Can the operator stop and hold throughout the full range?
- Is every person outside the bight, recoil path, redirect sweep, and load path?
- Is there a clear command system and a practical emergency plan? (USDA manual, pp. 79–82, 105–108)

After inspection, the source calls for a controlled test load with everyone clear. Apply load gradually, watch for unexpected motion, sound, distortion, settling, slip, line tracking, heating, or shifting protection, then unload and inspect again. A successful test at one load and direction does not certify the system for a higher load, a different direction, repeated cycles, or a human-support application. (USDA manual, pp. 79–82)

## Work zones, communication, and stopping

Establish exclusion zones from the force geometry, not convenience. Keep unnecessary people away from the load and every tensioned segment. Nobody should stand beneath a suspended material load, in the bight, between a moving object and a fixed object, or where an anchor, block, connector, line, or load could travel on failure. Preserve an escape route that does not cross a tensioned line. (USDA manual, pp. 79–82, 105–108)

Closed-loop communication reduces ambiguity. Address the operator, give one agreed command, require the operator to repeat it back, and continue only when the command is understood. Establish hand signals or radio language in advance, including an unmistakable stop. Commands should describe an action and persist until changed rather than inviting improvisation. (USDA manual, pp. 105–108)

If anyone loses sight, communication, control, or confidence, stop in a controlled state. Stop for unexpected movement, unusual noise, a migrating sling, a damaged protector, a riding line, a changing anchor, device heat, or any person entering the exclusion zone. The right response to uncertainty is not to pull harder.

## Hazard analysis, emergency readiness, and review

A job hazard analysis links each task step to hazards and mitigations. It should address the site, equipment, weather, communication, personnel, medical response, and how rescuers could reach the location. The prework briefing makes those decisions shared. The manual's Forest Service forms and policy references remain agency-specific; the transferable practice is a documented, task-specific discussion before exposure. (USDA manual, pp. 105–108)

Emergency readiness includes first-aid capability, a reliable way to summon help, the location and access route, a person responsible for coordination, and a plan that remains workable when the primary operation has failed. It should not depend on the same device, line, anchor, power source, or communication path whose failure creates the emergency. (USDA manual, pp. 105–108)

After the operation, inspect and account for equipment, quarantine anything suspect, and record unusual loading or damage. Conduct an after-action review: what was planned, what actually happened, what changed, and what should be revised before the next operation. Near misses are evidence about the system rather than proof that the method was adequate. (USDA manual, pp. 105–108)

## Source-specific errata and interpretation notes

The source is technically valuable but not free of typographical or editorial inconsistencies. This digest preserves the following corrections explicitly rather than silently teaching the errors:

- On manual page 128, the reciprocal identity printed as `cos(A) = 1/sin(A) = c/a` conflicts with the triangle and later calculations. The intended reciprocal function is `csc(A) = 1/sin(A) = c/a`.
- On manual page 133, prose refers to an angle “more than 30 percent.” The table and surrounding discussion show that the unit is degrees.
- On manual page 149, surrounding geometry and worked examples support lateral component `F × sin(θ)` and inline or downward component `F × cos(θ)` for the angle convention shown; the formula text repeats sine for both.
- Some appendix figure labels and captions on pages 152 and 160 are ambiguous, internally awkward, or inconsistent. This digest relies on the stated geometry and reproducible numbers rather than copying those figures.

These corrections do not turn the appendix into a design standard. They make the source's mathematical lesson legible while keeping its own warning: ideal vector calculations can create false confidence when field conditions and component behavior are uncertain. (USDA manual, pp. 127–168)

## Compact decision discipline

For any high-consequence rigging question, use this order:

1. **Define the domain.** Identify the applicable standard, competent person, and manufacturer instructions. Do not transfer a trail-work rule into human suspension.
2. **Define the load case.** Include direction, center of gravity, motion, slack, braking, and credible abnormal states.
3. **Trace the load path.** Include every line segment, redirect reaction, connector, sling, device, anchor, and supporting structure.
4. **Resolve geometry.** State angle conventions, calculate vector components, and examine shallow-angle tension and bending moments.
5. **Verify identity and ratings.** Use configuration-specific WLLs, not appearance, diameter, or an unrelated MBS.
6. **Account for condition.** Inspect the actual item, its history, environment, bends, knots, edges, heat, and cyclic use.
7. **Control consequences.** Establish exclusion zones, escape routes, braking, communication, and an emergency stop.
8. **Test cautiously within the authorized domain.** Load gradually with people clear; unload and inspect. A test does not broaden certification.
9. **Record and review.** Log gear events, quarantine uncertainty, and revise the plan from observed behavior. (USDA manual, pp. 1–2, 15–20, 37–53, 61–82, 105–108, 127–168)
