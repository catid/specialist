## Advice for Rope

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/advice

### Community conduct and playing with someone new

Categories: consent_scene_management, bottoming_skills

Do not touch people, their clothing, toys, or rope without explicit permission; do not interrupt or invite yourself into an active scene; do not assume that observing an activity grants you the same access; obtain consent and follow venue rules before photography; and use only labels a person accepts.

With a new partner, obtain local references, negotiate the planned activities, exchange relevant medical and mobility information, discuss known risks and injury, agree on safewords and check-ins, and prefer a public setting or a trusted observer. Warning signs in a partner include defensiveness about expertise, pressure to suppress self-advocacy, isolation from the community, controlling whom someone else may see, poor response to feedback, or blaming a bottom when a planned tie conflicts with that person's limits.

### Self-advocacy, warning signs, and emergency readiness

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, bottoming_skills, emergency_procedures

Speak up about discomfort or boundary violations, notify event staff promptly when appropriate, and choose ending a scene over risking injury. Evaluate proposed risks for yourself. Tingling, numbness, or unusual sensation calls for modification or removal; periodically compare normal feeling and strength. Light-headedness, visual spots, ringing ears, cold sweat, nausea, or incoherence can precede fainting or vomiting: prevent a fall, move to the floor and horizontal, loosen/remove rope, and obtain appropriate help.

Avoid strenuous rope when dehydrated, hungry, exhausted, ill, overheated, or very cold. Never leave a restrained person unattended, avoid neck loading that could choke, avoid impaired play, and do not suspend without training and appropriate equipment. Keep tested safety shears within reach and plan for sudden unconsciousness, fire, darkness, and evacuation. The handout highlights upper-arm rope, suspension, mixed/distraction play, outsiders joining a scene, and group play as circumstances needing extra caution. It also warns that past hardpoint use is not proof of present safety and that weak jute can fail as a main support line.

## The Somerville Bowline: Original Photo Sequence

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2010/04/27/the-somerville-bowline

### Single-column sequence

Categories: ties_knots_frictions, rope_handling, lineage_history_people

The author says the cuff derives from Ashley #1445/Myrtle Hitch rather than a true bowline and names it for its Somerville origin plus French/Portuguese-bowline design goals. After several body wraps, turn the center bight one full circle around the standing pair in its natural direction. Pull the bight while leaving the standing part slack so the loop transfers onto the standing part. Take the bight over the X at the loop base, beneath the entire cuff, and back through the loop in the same direction as its first entry. Tighten by drawing the standing loop. The finished cuff should not shrink when either end is pulled. Unload before undoing; loosening the standing loop first is easiest.

### Double-column sequence

Categories: ties_knots_frictions, pattern_architecture

Wrap both columns and form the same standing-part loop. Take the bight over the loop X, down between limbs, across the outer rear cuff, up between limbs, and through the loop; this cinch sets spacing. Before tightening, circle only the front/top half of the cuff and pass the bight through the loop a third time so pulling the bight cannot deepen the cinch. Then snug the standing loop.

## The Slipped Overhand Cuff: Photo Sequence

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2010/04/28/the-slipped-overhand-cuff

### Loaded second-column sequence

Categories: ties_knots_frictions, rigging_mechanics, rope_handling

Form a slipped overhand in the loaded standing part so pulling the free end enlarges its loop. Circle the new column with the working end and pass through that loop; holding the end now maintains load. Adjust the loop to finish just above the column and set final line tension. Reverse around the column, pass through the loop, and repeat alternating sides for the desired number of wraps. Finish by splitting the pair into a square knot, or use half hitches while carefully preserving the last wrap's equality.

## Lark's-Head Double Column: Original Photo Sequence

### Cinching and balanced finishes

Categories: ties_knots_frictions, pattern_architecture

Begin as for the single column, then circle the rear cuff between the two columns; this cinch sets spacing. Splitting ends around the front and square-knotting needs little gap but loads mainly the rear and can feel tightening. A more balanced finish passes through the starting doubled bight and half-hitches the opposite front half. With room, two opposing hitches create a clove-hitch-like front lock around the cinch.

## Lark's-Head Single Column: Original Photo Sequence

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2010/05/02/larks-head-based-single-column

### Construction and direction of pull

Categories: ties_knots_frictions, rope_handling

Form a lark's head on the column, reverse direction, and add wraps to the desired width. At the start, identify the interlocked single-line and doubled bights; pull the working rope through the doubled one. Reverse, lay a loop over the cuff, pass beneath the full cuff, and rise through that loop; dress and tighten. For a pull along the column, orient the under-cuff exit toward the pull. The opposite orientation can roll and bunch the cuff, levering the knot into tissue; reverse the finishing hitch when the pull direction changes.

## Reverse Somerville Bowline

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2011/05/31/reverse-somerville-bowline

### Why the reverse form exists

Categories: ties_knots_frictions, pattern_architecture

This difficult cuff solves a second-column problem where a simple pair of half hitches would load only the first wrap and cinch unless the rope returns to the harness. Reverse Somerville can end at the cuff or continue farther down the limb. It is slower, easier to overtighten, and vulnerable to unequal first-wrap loading, so it should not replace the normal Somerville by default. Its construction is video/photo dependent.

## Attaching Support Lines with a Half-Tatu Hitch

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2012/04/27/attaching-support-lines

### Single point and hanger variants

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, ties_knots_frictions, rigging_mechanics

From the doubled center, draw a new doubled bight beneath the attachment while the original center bight stays on its side. Fold the single center bight against the doubled bight to align three loops. For a carabiner version, clip all three loops plus the standing pair, orienting the broad basket away from the harness. This intermediate is not body-stable: go to the top, return through the carabiner, and only then lift. Plan enough rope that emergency lowering does not pull the unstable intermediate against the body.

Without hardware, pass a standing-line bight through the three loops, daisy-chain once or twice, and pull the full rope through the last link to lock; a further return through the three loops adds leverage. Extend the same principle across several harness points. A hardware hanger can self-equalize as angle changes; the chained form has more friction and largely preserves its set angle.

If the rope breaks at its center, the entire attachment releases. Inspect and use support line with adequate strength; the source's use of 8 mm hemp with a 6 mm harness illustrates margin, not a universal approval.

## Chest-Loading TK Version 3 (Historical)

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2013/05/21/version-3-chest-loading-takate-kote

### Superseded tension architecture

Categories: lineage_history_people, pattern_architecture, rigging_mechanics

Version 3 is explicitly historical. Its enduring design lesson is that chest-side sections must start tighter than arm sections; otherwise arm bands consume chest slack under load. An over-shoulder start creates a firm stem independent of loose arm bands. Interleaved structural locks prevent side loading from moving slack between arm and chest elements, and arrange tightening to act on kannuki rather than arm bands. Side suspension requires watching upper inner-arm pressure and hitch migration. CTKv4 supersedes the actual construction.

## Takate-Kote Wrap Positioning Survey

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2014/09/08/takate-kote-wrap-positioning-survey-results

### Findings and limits

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, pattern_architecture, teaching_evaluation

The retrospective survey analyzed 53 responses and removed 12 that appeared confused, noncompliant, or reported fewer than five suspensions. It is small, self-selected, and observational. The author concludes there is no universally safe TK band placement. Upper bands showed fewer radial problems when very high, but comfort and structural constraints still apply. Lower-band risk appeared possible from just below the deltoid/triceps gap to above the elbow, without a single clean danger point. Most respondents reported comfort or safety problems even at their preferred placement.

The data contradicted claims that the entire upper half of the arm is relatively safe and that high upper kannuki explain most generalized/ulnar symptoms. Closely spaced bands had too few observations for a conclusion. The deltoid/triceps gap appeared undesirable, possibly from circulatory effects distinct from radial vulnerability just below it. Lower bands dominated reported radial problems only when upper bands were already sufficiently high. These are survey associations, not anatomical prediction for an individual.

## Mechanical Advantage and Friction in Suspension Lifts

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/blog/2015/mechanical-advantage-and-friction-in-suspension-lifts

### Measured model results

Categories: rigging_mechanics, uplines_suspension_hardpoints, teaching_evaluation

Using hemp, sandbag loads, Black Diamond RockLocks, and a bathroom scale the author estimated ideal downward pull for a 100 lb load. With a bottom carabiner, modeled 1:1 through 5:1 pulls were 159, 97, 78, 70, and 65 lb; with rope-on-rope bottom contact, 159, 119, 103, 99, and 96 lb. With a 10% friction increase intended to approximate real use, values became 169/106/87/78/74 lb with a carabiner and 169/134/119/116/114 lb without. These are one experimental model, not certified design loads.

Consequences: use 3:1 when rope rubs rope; extra ratio mostly adds jams. With hardware, returns beyond 3:1 diminish rapidly but can improve control for a heavier bottom. Pulling upward avoids the top redirect and the model estimated 3:1 at 51 lb with a bottom carabiner and 70 lb without. Ergonomics may erase some gain, so one hand up/one down can help.

### Friction variables, holding, and uncertainty

Categories: rigging_mechanics, rope_handling

Off-axis orientation raised measured 3:1 effort about 5% with a carabiner and up to 15% on a thick ring. A ring much thicker than the rope added as much as about 20% in limited tests; it should not be assumed to protect weak rope. Reversing line directions through one carabiner added roughly 4–8% in 3:1 and about 10% in 5:1. Separate, well-spaced 5:1 carabiners reduced about another 10%; chaining them together did not. Typical accelerated lifts measured at least about 20% above steady motion. Synthetic-versus-natural friction changed 3:1 effort up to about 20%, while natural-fiber differences were smaller.

Observed holding force was about 10–15% of load for 3:1 with a bottom carabiner and 0–3% without (with jam risk); 5:1 was about 1–2% with hardware and jammed without. Small setup changes shifted results 5–10%, and direct friction measurements disagreed with the fitted coefficient. The author estimated scale repeatability near ±1 lb. These unresolved discrepancies reinforce using the tables for qualitative comparison, not safety rating.

## Core Rope Curriculum Progression

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/crc

### Learning architecture and adaptation

Categories: teaching_evaluation, accessibility_adaptation, bottoming_skills

The curriculum uses an inverted-classroom model: students learn repeatable motions from media at their own pace, then spend supervised time on feedback, mastery, adaptation, and experimentation. Partners are encouraged to practice both tying and being tied, even if their play roles are fixed, because embodied understanding improves communication. The program states that bodies and identities are welcome and that ties should be modified to actual needs.

The eight-level progression is: basic floorwork; advanced/creative floorwork; chest- and hip-harness suspension basics; intermediate positions and emergency drills; adaptive suspension; athletic/high-risk upper-arm suspension; transitions requiring fast midair improvisation; and performance tricks that knowingly sacrifice comfort or safety for showmanship. Entry requires proficiency in prerequisites, and the page recommends beginning below the highest level one thinks possible. This is the site's teaching sequence, not an industry certification.

## Basic Safety

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/1

Source level: 1

### Risk awareness and fall prevention

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, consent_scene_management, emergency_procedures

Meaningful consent requires knowing non-obvious risks. The author favors the least hazardous technique that still creates the intended effect, because a larger safety margin leaves more attention for connection. Once hands are restrained, ordinary standing, sitting, walking, furniture, or a bed can become fall hazards. Assume a tied partner may lose balance, hop, struggle, or tip an object; maintain supervision and arrange a responsible watcher if the top must briefly step away.

Continuously ask what happens if someone faints, kicks, loses balance, equipment moves, power fails, or evacuation becomes necessary. This anticipatory habit is more general than any one pattern.

### Nerve placement and warning signs

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, bottoming_skills

Nerve compression is the site's most emphasized common injury. Pressure can crush a nerve against bone and may impair sensation or movement for months; severe recovery can take years, and an injured site may remain vulnerable.

- At the wrist, keep rope proximal to the groove between forearm and hand bones. Leave enough room that a migrating cuff passes onto the back of the hand rather than lodging in the groove. Sensory injury on the thumb side is commonly called handcuff neuropathy.
- Avoid concentrated pressure near knees and elbows, especially the ulnar nerve above the back of the elbow and the common peroneal nerve just below the outside of the knee.
- The radial nerve may be vulnerable across much of the back of the upper arm from below the deltoid toward the elbow. Individual anatomy makes a universally safe band position impossible to predict.
- In suspension, pressure near the front/crest of the hip may affect lateral femoral cutaneous branches, and pressure near the front groin crease may affect anterior femoral cutaneous branches. Extreme hip flexion can also create positional compression.

Tingling and numbness are common warnings, but sharp, burning, itching, cold, or other altered sensation may also occur, and an injury can sometimes appear without a felt warning. Sudden onset or symptoms confined to one part of a hand are especially concerning, but mild symptoms still deserve action.

### Functional check-ins

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, bottoming_skills, consent_scene_management

Check both sensation and motor function because missing sensation can be subtle and opposing motions may use different nerves. The source's practiced partner hand check is: the top places an index finger in the palm; the bottom squeezes it; the top then lightly encloses the fist while the bottom pushes the hand open. Practicing beforehand establishes the person's normal strength and a nonverbal “okay” response. A self-check for the radial nerve places two fingertips on the back of the thumb while the thumb presses upward, comparing sensation and strength.

If symptoms occur, remove the mechanical cause and avoid reloading the area. The author's suggestion that an NSAID immediately after a nerve injury might aid recovery is explicitly uncertain, contraindications matter, and it should not be treated as established medical consensus; persistent or function-limiting symptoms warrant professional evaluation.

### Circulation, neck risk, and escape tools

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, emergency_procedures

Purple or pale color, coldness, puffiness, and swelling are more specific circulation changes than tingling. The site argues that tingling is more often nerve compression than “only circulation.” For digits or genitals it gives a conservative source-specific suggestion not to maintain substantial restriction beyond roughly 10–15 minutes; this is not a universal medical threshold. Most body cuffs should accept a finger without being so tight that they stop flow.

Front or side neck pressure can rapidly impair brain blood flow, while front pressure can injure the airway. The author advises becoming expert in both rope and breath play before even considering their combination, because rope makes force harder to judge and concentrate. Never leave a tied person alone and never self-bind alone: positional asphyxia, gag obstruction, vomiting, fire, and many other events can become fatal without assistance.

Keep safety/EMT shears accessible and test them on the actual rope. Rescue hooks require practice, sharpness, and a design that cannot admit body parts; animal-skinning tools with large blade openings are inappropriate.

## Getting Started with Rope

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/107

### Preparing to play and choosing instruction

Categories: consent_scene_management, teaching_evaluation, accessibility_adaptation

Learn boundaries, negotiation, partner selection, and venue safety before learning patterns. Rope education has no universal quality standard, and even prestigious venues can host confident teachers whose explanations are wrong or omit important risks. Compare sources, test claims for internal sense, and ask instructors why a method works; an inability to explain the mechanism is a warning that material may be repeated without understanding.

A style that is painful, inaccessible, or emotionally wrong for one body or relationship is not proof that the person has failed. Rope can be adapted to different bodies and moods, and learners should keep looking for or designing an approach that fits rather than accepting a local style as universal.

## Advanced Nerve Safety

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/116

Source level: 2

### Why “safe numbness” is a poor model

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, bottoming_skills, teaching_evaluation

The page rejects the common binary that numbness from lost circulation is harmless while numbness from a compressed nerve is dangerous. A pressure band strong enough to markedly restrict a whole limb also presses on nerves, and ordinary pins-and-needles commonly results from direct nerve pressure. Thus visible color change does not explain away altered sensation. Rapid, intense, focal symptoms may imply higher pressure on one exposed nerve, but slower and diffuse symptoms lie on the same risk spectrum rather than becoming harmless.

Risk rises with both pressure and time, varies by person and day, and can accumulate across repeated hard use. The author frames extreme short-career rope versus moderate long-career rope as an explicit risk-tolerance choice, not a moral judgment.

### Mechanical pressure, ischemia, and uncertainty

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention

The source distinguishes two coupled mechanisms: mechanical strain of the nerve and impaired flow through the small vessels supplying it. Medical tourniquet literature is used only as indirect evidence; bondage-specific research is sparse, the author is not a physician, and the page adopts a precautionary interpretation where uncertainty could hide greater risk.

Dark red or purple color often means venous return is restricted more than arterial inflow, so blood pools while oxygenated blood still enters. Complete restriction trends pale/blotchy and cold. Diabetes, Raynaud's, and other vascular conditions can lower baseline circulation and may make ischemic nerve effects possible with less rope pressure; the page infers increased risk but does not claim a measured bondage threshold.

The page describes, rather than medically endorses, a self-demonstration using twine on a little finger to compare venous pooling with complete restriction. Because deliberately occluding tissue is not necessary for learning and individual vascular risk is unknown, this corpus retains the observation—color/cold can change before numbness—without presenting the experiment as a recommended drill.

### Injury types and recovery claims

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention

The source distinguishes neurapraxia, in which myelin damage disrupts conduction while the axon survives, from axonotmesis, in which the axon must regrow outward from the injury. It reports 6–8 weeks as a typical neurapraxia recovery range with earlier improvement often visible, and roughly 1 mm/day as a commonly quoted axonal regrowth rate for axonotmesis. These are general medical descriptions used by the site, not a diagnosis timetable for an injured partner. Lack of quick improvement, weakness, or persistent altered sensation calls for professional care.

### Takate-kote and upper-arm risk

Categories: anatomy_injury_prevention, pattern_architecture, teaching_evaluation

The site identifies the box tie/takate-kote (TK) as the pattern associated with most reported debilitating rope injuries, while acknowledging that its popularity confounds any rate comparison. Experts disagree widely on upper/lower band height, spacing, angle, kannuki, and loading. The author's retrospective survey found no placement known in advance to be safe for every person. Even a hypothetical “correct” TK that becomes dangerous after a small construction error lacks the margin and independent checks expected of a safety-critical system. Use therefore requires explicit informed consent to its residual upper-arm risk rather than assurances that expertise makes it safe.

## Basic Suspension Safety

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/123

Source level: 3

### Readiness and one-failure margin

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, teaching_evaluation, emergency_procedures

The page's governing principle is to remain more than one human or equipment error away from catastrophe. Use external redundancy where a single mistake cannot drop a person and internal redundancy through pauses, identification checks, and abort plans. Being able to reproduce a harness is not suspension readiness; readiness is calm response when events leave the planned path and enough floor-rope fluency that knots and feeding consume little emergency attention.

Early suspensions should have nearby assistance and an experienced monitor, keep a static chest line to the ring throughout, leave arms free during entry/exit, avoid high-nerve-risk or highly painful ties, and use a mat. An experienced bottom can recognize abnormal loading and improve feedback, but does not transfer the rigger's responsibility. Feeling chronically rushed, adrenalized, awkward, focused on the next technical step instead of the partner, or unable to recover from a small deviation indicates that the chosen difficulty exceeds current capacity.

### Preflight and informed consent

Categories: consent_scene_management, bottoming_skills, anatomy_injury_prevention

Check present food, water, illness, sleep, soreness, bathroom needs, and temperature. Ask about medical conditions, mobility limits, current and old injuries, allergies, medication, and emergency devices; learn where an inhaler or epinephrine device is and how the owner expects help. Ask which prior positions worked, the hardest previous tie, injuries, and bad scenes.

Explain likely skin impressions, bruising/petechiae, burn, nerve compression, fainting, hardpoint uncertainty, drop risk, and possible equipment failure. The page reports impressions often fading within hours, bruising/petechiae sometimes lasting weeks, and severe burns months; these are author-provided ranges, not medical guarantees. Practice motor/sensory checks. At the space, inspect the hardpoint, identify trip and human hazards, and locate visible reachable cutters.

Mats protect head and joints but can slide, trip at an edge, or be too soft for balance. Large interlocking flooring reduces walking; the author favors dense wrestling/gym mats roughly 2–5 cm (1–2 in) for durable cleanable public use. Those dimensions are preference, not a universal impact rating.

### Control during changes

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, rigging_mechanics, rope_handling

Protect the head first. For beginning technique, attach the chest to the ring before other loading and keep it attached. Before changing a support line, positively identify the rope and every component it influences; predict the sequence of body forces; ask whether a mistake could put the head on the floor; detach any head/neck support before changing other geometry; and decide how to abort mid-change. Run this check while the line is still secured, not after it is fully opened.

Use completion of a change—and the urge to “do something” after a quiet period—as prompts to check sensation, strength, consciousness, and comfort. Anticipate that sex or impact play can distort time and attention; choose a safer rope system when distraction is planned.

### Fainting, falls, and exit

Categories: emergency_procedures, anatomy_injury_prevention, bottoming_skills

Watch for light-headedness, spots, ringing ears, cold sweat, nausea, or unexpected incoherence. Prevent a fall and move toward horizontal. A conscious person may first sit with knees raised; an unconscious person or someone not improving seated should be laid flat. If consciousness does not promptly return, check breathing/circulation, use an appropriate recovery position when indicated, and summon emergency medical care. These source steps are not a substitute for current first-aid training. The author's suggestion that an experienced pair might continue after early symptoms fully resolve is source-specific and should not be treated as medical consensus.

After a fall with possible spine or head injury, the page says not to move the person before medical assistance unless another immediate danger requires it. Bring an inverted/horizontal person upright gradually. The author keeps the chest line lightly loaded for at least about one minute after a standing exit because delayed fainting can occur; that timing is his practice, not a guaranteed safe interval. Stay close until cognition and balance return; do not allow immediate driving, climbing, or unassisted walking while the person remains altered.

## Hardpoint Evaluation

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/124

Source level: 3

### Evidence, inspection, and load history

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, rigging_mechanics, bottoming_skills

Hardpoint failure is uncommon but abrupt and catastrophic. Both participants should inspect before scene headspace; a bottom has equal standing to ask how safety was determined. A defensive or evasive answer is a partner-screening warning.

Reject the “two big people hung from it once” argument. One successful overload proves only that failure did not occur then and may itself accumulate fatigue, like repeatedly bending a paper clip. Past use by a hundred people is the same weak evidence. Direct inspection immediately before use is the preferred method: see every load-path component, verify assembly and rating, and look for corrosion, deformation, looseness, abrasion, or wear. The site's design target is minimum breaking strength at least ten times expected load; this is the author's conservative rigging rule, not a substitute for an engineer or product specification.

When direct access is impossible, evaluate provenance: installer qualifications, whether human suspension was the intended use, the date and scope of inspection, use or tampering since, and whether information comes from the responsible professional rather than venue folklore.

### Frames, tripods, and eye bolts

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, rigging_mechanics

Freestanding frames are inspectable. Under a controlled test load, reject or investigate structural deformation, loose joints, walking feet, or instability. A tripod can tip when swing angle moves the load outside its base; determine the permitted envelope and follow its rated load direction. Lightweight tripods are usually top-loaded only—do not hang body weight from one leg.

Reject bent-rod eyes with a gap. Use only appropriately rated forged, cast, or welded closed eyes. Through-bolts should expose the nut side for inspection, ideally with a locknut; isolate rotation with a swivel so the bolt cannot unscrew. Lag eyes disappearing into wood cannot be inspected and are highly suspect. Ordinary eye bolts are designed for in-line pull; only correctly installed rated shoulder eyes permit limited angular loading. The page's examples list a 1/4-in bent eye at 200 lb WLL, a 3/8-in welded eye at 1,000 lb, and a 3/8-in forged eye at 1,200 lb, but product-specific ratings and installation govern.

### Ratings, slings, shackles, and concrete

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, rigging_mechanics, ties_knots_frictions

Working/safe load limit already includes a manufacturer's design factor, often only 3–5 for non-human lifting. The author's method is to recover estimated breaking strength by multiplying WLL by the stated factor, then apply his tenfold human-suspension margin; absent a stated factor, he pessimistically estimates MBS as three times WLL.

Around a beam, use rated chain or climbing-rated webbing at least 1 inch wide. Inspect chain corrosion, webbing abrasion and edge protection, connector closure/rating, sewn-stitch wear, and water-knot tails. Avoid a tight shallow sling triangle: small included angles multiply leg tension. Anchor shackles require the pin secured against rotation and force centered correctly on the pin; friction against the pin can unscrew it. Because bondage installations commonly omit seizing or side-load them, the author rarely uses them and, when vertical space forces the choice, uses redundant pairs.

Concrete ceiling anchors are technically demanding and cannot be validated by surface appearance. Wrong anchor type, poor placement, weak concrete, or simultaneous multi-anchor failure can remain invisible until release. The author recommends avoiding them unless installation and substrate are professionally documented.

### Hoists, expected load, and mitigation

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, rigging_mechanics, emergency_procedures

Most users cannot inspect a hoist internally. Electric systems need a no-power lowering plan. Wire-rope hoists can reverse-wrap after a jam, move upward on a down command, then release several feet suddenly; an overhead hoist also becomes a falling mass if its mount fails. Use only equipment labeled as a hoist, not a ground-pulling winch. The author therefore favors rope and pulleys for an adjustable ring.

For a downward pull redirected at the hardpoint, the site conservatively assumes the ring can see the bottom's weight plus the rigger's available downward force, approximated by the rigger's body weight; its example is 150 lb bottom + 200 lb rigger, followed by the tenfold target, or 3,500 lb MBS. This is a source rule of thumb; real vector force depends on geometry, friction, acceleration, and technique.

A brief post-rig test hang is useful to remove knot slack and observe equalization, not to prove strength. If evidence remains weak, stay low, avoid inversion, add suitable matting, or tie on the floor. “Partial” suspension can become full with one limb shift. Combining two uncertain anchors is not automatically redundant: load sharing and angles are complex, and two weak points remain weak.

## Support-Line Selection

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/134

Source level: 3

### Safety factor and load estimate

Categories: uplines_suspension_hardpoints, rigging_mechanics, rope_materials_maintenance

Main-line failure can produce a catastrophic fall. The site observes that published breaks disproportionately involve 5–6 mm jute and argues from a tenfold lifeline safety factor. Its illustrative deratings—50% for knots/small bend radius and 30% each for manufacturing variation, treatment/wear, and dynamic load—multiply a nominal 10:1 margin down to about 1.7:1 for remaining unknowns. These percentages are explicitly the author's estimates, not measured universal factors.

In a static six-line hanger, a 10:1 system factor would require each line to break above about 1.7 times body weight if load shared perfectly. During lifting, however, the pair in the rigger's hands may carry the peak. The author therefore uses rigger body weight as an upper estimate of hand pull and recommends each doubled support rope have MBS at least five times rigger weight—for example, 750 lb for a 150 lb rigger. Geometry, acceleration, imbalance, knots, and actual technique can require a different engineering analysis.

### Material, diameter, and length choices

Categories: rope_materials_maintenance, uplines_suspension_hardpoints, emergency_procedures

The only natural-fiber main line the author routinely uses is roughly 8 mm hemp reported near 800 lb. He reports some 6 mm hemp breaks and numerous 5–6 mm jute breaks. Synthetic preferences include spun polyester/POSH, Hempex, Regatta Braid, properly test-validated Dyneema-reinforced jute, and rated coreless nylon/MFP. Reinforcement claims require break-test evidence; a token synthetic strand may add no meaningful strength. Nylon varies, abrades poorly when run fast against itself or rough surfaces, and must be selected from an actual rating.

Choose a synthetic close to 6 mm if it meets strength: thinner line cuts the rigger's hands; thicker consumes ring space. The author's standard main-line length is 30 ft, longer for high rings/tall riggers. The non-negotiable functional check is enough length to lower an unconscious person all the way to the floor without extension; do not repurpose leftover harness rope as an upline.

## Support-Line Handling

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/137

Source level: 3

### Mechanical advantage and friction

Categories: rigging_mechanics, uplines_suspension_hardpoints

A common line runs harness → ring → harness and optionally ring again. Three strands rise from the harness, so nominal mechanical advantage is 3:1; the final top redirect changes pull direction, not ratio. In a frictionless system a 100 lb load would require about 33 lb, but rope/ring and especially rope-on-rope friction can raise source-observed pull toward 80 lb. The same friction helps holding/lowering: the page's example estimates only about 15 lb to hold/control the already-lifted load. These figures are illustrative measurements, not design ratings.

With rope-on-rope contact at the bottom, prefer 3:1. With a carabiner at the harness, 4:1 or 5:1 provides some additional lift/control; meaningful 5:1 improvement requires separate well-spaced top and bottom carabiners. More passes without controlling friction add jam risk and rapidly diminishing gain.

### Lowering, dressing, and hand control

Categories: rope_handling, rigging_mechanics, emergency_procedures

Remove surplus securing passes before lowering; near-zero hand load makes the system jerky and prone to jams. Never lower with greater mechanical advantage than used for lifting, and do not go below 3:1 in the described system. A Munter may need removal if friction prevents smooth descent or if a reversible adjustment is desired.

Dress support lines as a continuous untwisted circle without diagonal crossings. On an asymmetric carabiner, place the highest-tension working rope nearest the spine/stem, where it naturally seeks the low point. The author consistently feeds toward himself at the ring and away at the harness to reduce decision load and top friction.

Keep one hand firmly tensioning the working end. Gripping the whole 3:1 bundle stops movement as an emergency brake, but routine dependence on it signals inadequate control. During hand transfer across a ring, feel tension and motion in the receiving hand before releasing the source hand; selecting the wrong strand may unexpectedly remove mechanical advantage. Practice with an inanimate load. If live control deteriorates, grab the bundle first, stabilize, and then recover. Main lines must always reach the floor.

## Chest-Loading Takate Kote v4

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/188

Source level: 6

### Scope, residual risk, and fit

Categories: pattern_architecture, anatomy_injury_prevention, teaching_evaluation

CTKv4 modifies the box tie so suspension load transfers chiefly to the ribcage rather than upper arms while preserving much of TK form and experience. It remains advanced TK suspension. The source advises at least two years of combined top/bottom suspension experience; that is an author threshold, not certification. The cuff needs only enough room for the bottom to swap arm order, not exaggerated looseness.

Traditional TK bands can compress the radial nerve and produce wrist drop. CTK reduces arm pressure in theory and by observed rope tension/marks, but has no controlled injury-rate evidence. Lower apparent arm load can be offset by overconfidence, construction error, or different injuries. Treat it with normal TK caution, equalize all four strands of each arm band, and use known past injury only as evidence of a dangerous site—not proof that another site is safe.

Arm bands should be near the loose end of stable TK tension; chest kannuki must be tighter so they engage before arm wraps. The source reports typical perceived load near 70% chest/30% arms and at most roughly 80/20; these are experiential estimates. Anyone medically advised to avoid rib pressure should not use it. Armpit bulk, very high band placement, rib shape, breasts, or shoulder-blade prominence may also make the design unsuitable.

### Mechanism and design logic

Categories: pattern_architecture, rigging_mechanics, ties_knots_frictions

Front half hitches stop the arm bands sliding inward. Under suspension pull, those stops press against tighter kannuki, transferring force onto ribs; if kannuki contain more slack than arm wraps, the mechanism engages too late. Make each first half hitch over-under and the second under-over so the raised exit lies toward the chest, where crossing is less hazardous and the kannuki controls it.

The initial shoulder route establishes a firm stem independently of deliberately loose arm bands. Version 4 splits the central stem into two side stems, removes bulk from the spine, increases usable lock space, and balances side-suspension forces. Three-quarter-moon and L locks structurally isolate arm bands from higher-tension kannuki; friction-only isolation is inadequate for large tension differences. Doubled shoulder lines weave across the front so their crossing can migrate and approximately equalize load. Moving the “third-rope” front work between upper and lower modules avoids adding pressure at the already-loaded lower-rib half hitches.

### Written construction sequence

Categories: pattern_architecture, rope_handling, ties_knots_frictions

Memory rules: work left first; before every arm band lock to the left stem; pass under stems; before each kannuki put a three-quarter-moon on the near stem; after each kannuki make an outer L lock; after right-side L locks add one full stem turn.

1. Cuff wrists. Go over left shoulder, under right breast, straight across back, under left breast, over right shoulder, then through the wrist cuff right-to-left behind its knot. Set upward wrist tension.
2. At upper-band height half-hitch the left stem. Circle left around chest and both arms; pass beneath both stems and circle the left stem.
3. Make the second upper band. Form its left and right front half hitches over-under then under-over, positioned just chest-side of each arm/chest gap. Equalize left strands after the first hitch.
4. Under both stems make a three-quarter-moon on the left stem, checking right-band equality midway. Form the left kannuki inside the hitch while skipping only the single top strand; tighten without pinching.
5. Pass under the left stem; L-lock arm bands and kannuki outside it. Under the right stem make a three-quarter-moon, form right kannuki, L-lock outside, then take one extra full turn around the right stem.
6. For the shoulder/front module, pass under the left stem and through the left elbow opening. Move both shoulder ropes over the breasts, shift their crossing below the upper bands, bring all slack forward, and spread away from the neck.
7. Weave the working rope under the left shoulder rope and over upper bands; go over left shoulder. Loop over-under the full horizontal bundle between stems and rise on the left. Cross over right shoulder back-to-front, weave over upper bands and under the earlier right shoulder rope, then return through the right elbow opening.
8. For lower bands, pass under both stems and circle the left stem; circle left around chest/arms; again go beneath both and circle left. Add the second lower band and its paired front half hitches, usually near the chest rather than far onto exposed ribs.
9. Under both stems, three-quarter-moon the left stem around only the two lower bands; add left kannuki, outer L lock, then repeat three-quarter-moon, kannuki, and L lock on the right, including only current rope and lower bands as specified.
10. Add the extra right-stem turn if room remains, then finish with one or two half hitches around a convenient lower central bundle.

### Attachments, alternatives, and troubleshooting

Categories: pattern_architecture, uplines_suspension_hardpoints, accessibility_adaptation

For face-up suspension, attaching only to horizontal arm/chest bands is the most tested. Including crossing shoulder ropes is described as experimental but apparently workable. A legacy side attachment inside kannuki is untested on v4 and should be abandoned if the upper inner arm feels pressure. In face-down use, loading over-arm bands directly without also passing under their kannuki restores arm loading and may be worse than an ordinary TK.

A partial lower-only CTK may accommodate kannuki intolerance or performance speed but accepts more risk. On larger breasts, route the initial rear cross high enough that hidden slack cannot creep forward, and consider lifting the lower front bands with the shoulder module. If arms still bear ordinary-TK pressure, tighten kannuki and/or loosen bands; if shoulder ropes finish wrong, change the initial rear-X height (higher generally finishes tighter). Stem locks should sit near but not catch under shoulder-blade edges in side loading.

The page labels CTKv1–3 historical. It explicitly states that comfort is not safety: lower expected discomfort may make abnormal nerve sensation easier to notice, but a comfortable tie can still injure without warning.

## Rope Material Selection

### How to interpret the comparison

Material labels do not determine a rope's properties by themselves. Fiber quality, lay, braid, diameter, treatment, and supplier can dominate. | Fiber | Source breaking-strength range | Handling and care claims |

### Natural and specialty fibers

Categories: rope_materials_maintenance, aesthetics_performance

The site's nominal 6 mm comparisons report hemp around 400–500 lb, jute around 200–300 lb, and cotton roughly 200–600 lb. Hemp often softens with use, handles washing better than jute, may need tensioned drying, and can become sticky in humidity; some common hemp has a chunky photographic lay. Jute is lighter, more supple, quick to feed, and higher-friction, but is fragile wet, requires re-treatment after washing, and has a shorter reported service life. Three-strand, coreless cotton can be a soft inexpensive starter rope, while common loose cotton can jam; avoid cheap cored cotton.

Flax/linen is described as combining hemp-like physical feel with a jute-like visual line. Silk and rayon sold as “bamboo” are soft specialty options with incomplete data. Alpaca is exceptionally soft but delicate and hard to clean. Coconut and sisal are intentionally harsh sensation materials rather than ordinary restraint rope. The page repeats community concerns that manila can be chemically harsh and splintering, while noting the author had not tested it. ### Cleaning, water, and dye

Categories: rope_materials_maintenance

The page treats nylon, MFP, and filament polyester as water-compatible and washable; hemp shrinks and can jam wet; jute may be used wet only with damage risk and must not remain wet. Hemp, jute, cotton, and flax can accept suitable dyes, while polyester and MFP are difficult to dye at home. The author favors fiber-reactive dyes for plant fibers and acid dye for nylon. ## Suspension Equipment Recommendations

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/241

Source level: 3

### Carabiners, rings, and plates

Categories: rigging_mechanics, uplines_suspension_hardpoints, rope_materials_maintenance

For most uses choose a rated aluminum climbing carabiner. Evaluate usable width and flatness at the basket, rounded cross-section for lower friction/wear, and a lock operable with one hand. The author's preference is the Black Diamond RockLock TwistLock; this is a personal recommendation, not the only safe choice.

### Swivels, tools, and frames

Categories: rigging_mechanics, emergency_procedures, uplines_suspension_hardpoints

A shackle/openable swivel mounted directly to a plate saves one carabiner of height; read the specific manual and opening limitations. Carry shears regardless of any rigging knife. A locking, fully tapered marlinspike may open a jam, but cutting is faster when release is urgent.

The author describes a timber A-frame made from swing-set brackets. His example has 90 in floor-to-beam clearance, a beam-parallel footprint equal to beam length plus 48 in, a 97 in perpendicular footprint, and 103 in legs. These dimensions are one build, not a plan. Quoted bracket guidance limits 4×4 legs to 8 ft unless feet are set in concrete, a 4×6 beam to 12 ft and three swings, and total load to 500 lb; verify the current manufacturer's instructions and engineer human-suspension use rather than treating these numbers as approval.

## Negotiation and Consent

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/272

### Consent stakes and communication failure

Categories: consent_scene_management, bottoming_skills

The page presents consent practice as lifelong and community-dependent, not something one checklist can complete. A consent violation can produce severe, enduring trauma even when many physical rope injuries heal more quickly. This comparison is the author's framing, not a claim that either outcome is predictable for an individual.

Do not treat a safeword as the sole safety system. Fear, shock, overwhelm, restraint, or a trauma response can make speech or deliberate refusal unavailable; freezing is a common survival response, not weakness or later indecision. Up-front agreement and active observation remain necessary because a partner may be unable to announce that a boundary has been crossed.

### Negotiation topics

Categories: consent_scene_management, accessibility_adaptation, bottoming_skills

Discuss, in a conversational or checklist form:

1. Each person's goals, desired feelings, and reasons for using rope. State genuine wants before compromising.
2. Relevant experience, including what worked, failed, or should not be repeated. Avoid combining a new partner with a new technical skill when a familiar activity could first establish communication.
3. Injuries, limitations, sustainable positions, preferred harness locations, inversion and spinning tolerance, and any unknowns the pair does or does not wish to explore.
4. Mood and touch: sexual or friendly, playful or serious, quiet or verbal, gentle or rough, and the amount of contact wanted.
5. No-touch/no-rope areas, places where marks are unacceptable, and wounds, tattoos, or piercings to protect.
6. Explicit extras. Hair pulling, face or mouth contact, neck/breath play, crotch or genital rope, penetration, genital contact, and often fingers, toes, or feet must not be assumed from a general agreement to rope or a “sexy” mood.
7. Whether struggling is wanted, when it may occur, and whether escape or containment is the desired ending. Active resistance while tying changes construction and greatly raises difficulty.
8. Known risks, safer equipment or technique alternatives, and the risk each participant brings. Both top and bottom can reject a risk even if the other accepts it.
9. Medical conditions, urgent medication and its location/use, and the possibility that the top—not only the bottom—could become incapacitated.
10. Safewords, ordinary-language meaning, gag-compatible signals with backups, desired check-in frequency, and practiced physical checks.
11. How pleasure, fear, silence, laughter, crying, or becoming nonverbal usually appears for this person.
12. Trauma triggers: what to avoid, how difficulty may present, and what helps, without requiring disclosure of the underlying history.
13. Immediate and later aftercare, drop for either role, follow-up contact, photographs, and the response to an injury.
14. Present readiness: food, hydration, sleep, temperature, illness, and bathroom needs.

### Common consent failure patterns

Categories: consent_scene_management, aesthetics_performance

Adding sex or penetration during a scene is particularly risky because arousal and scene headspace impair judgment and refusal. For a negotiated “maybe,” define the exact acts beforehand, ask again for an explicit enthusiastic yes, and defer to a later scene at any doubt. A safer progression for new partners is consensual sex without restraint on an earlier occasion before combining the two.

Group play diffuses responsibility and creates interactions that are hard to pre-negotiate. Define roles, increase detail and check-ins, and treat group scenes as advanced. Do not admit an unnegotiated drop-in player; the top must protect a restrained or altered partner from outsiders even if social pressure produces a momentary yes. Audiences can suppress feedback. Formal performance therefore calls for established partners, rehearsal, conservative choices, and little improvisation; instructors also hold power over demonstration volunteers. Mind-altering substances further degrade consent assessment.

## Whippings Overview

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/278

### Whipping material and method tradeoffs

Categories: rope_materials_maintenance, rope_handling, aesthetics_performance

A whipping is thread or twine wrapped around the rope end. Its low profile feeds cleanly, and colors can identify an owner's rope or encode length. Waxed natural twine is traditional; the author instead uses doubled heavy polyester top-stitch thread, strong enough to tension firmly. Thread that is too thick becomes bulky relative to the rope.

## Building a Rope Kit

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/3

### Rope dimensions, construction, and color

Categories: rope_materials_maintenance, rope_handling, teaching_evaluation

The Core Rope Curriculum generally uses 5–8 mm (3/16–3/8 in) rope; 6 mm (1/4 in) is a common beginner choice. Avoid kernmantle and other constructions with a braided sheath over a straight-fiber core: they handle poorly for body bondage and make bulky knots. Prefer three- or sometimes four-strand twisted rope, or a solid braid with no core. Dark rope makes strand paths and mistakes harder for a learner or instructor to see.

Build most of a kit from the longest length you can feed comfortably, then add shorter finishing ropes. Length is ergonomic: too much rope slows every pass, while ropes that are all short force frequent extensions.

### Sequencing and anatomical tradeoffs

Categories: pattern_architecture, anatomy_injury_prevention, bottoming_skills

The pattern uses a Figure-Eight Inline Double Column to keep bands from sliding while the rest is built. Bind elbows near the end so their spacing can be changed without dismantling everything. No band position between elbow and deltoid is universally safe: a lower position approaches the ulnar nerve; a higher position may require greater lever force and can affect the radial nerve.

Pinky-side tingling suggests possible ulnar involvement near the elbow and may improve by moving a band upward. Thumb-side symptoms suggest radial involvement and do not imply a predictable safe direction. Whole-arm numbness can reflect positional pressure on the brachial plexus/artery from drawing elbows back and together; usually increase elbow spacing rather than rationalizing the symptom as normal. Tight armbinders are particularly prone to nerve problems, and brachial-plexus injury can be profoundly disabling.

## Rope Coiling

Source URL: https://crash-restraint.com/ties/8

### Coil design goals and variants

Categories: rope_handling, rope_materials_maintenance

A useful coil leaves the center bight immediately accessible, drops the remaining rope with few tangles, and introduces little twist. Security in a bag conflicts with one-hand or flick release. The page's progression is: form a figure-eight bundle and tuck a bight beneath the binding turns for a quick but less secure coil; pass the bight through the bundle end for more security; or tuck a doubled bight through that end and push the binding turns toward it to regain pull release. A larger figure-eight speeds coiling but changes how the final pass-through behaves.

### Why centering is difficult

Categories: pattern_architecture, rope_handling, teaching_evaluation

Pulling hard to center the first band tends to leave the first/left side tighter because the opposite side gains mechanical advantage. The right can be deliberately tightened and some imbalance fed through the lock, but the whole band may then be excessive. Rotating a lightly tensioned band preserves comfort but can make the stem loose; lowering the wrists before starting can supply centering slack, and pinching the wraps together can encourage rotation. Bodies compress differently, so no single amount of pull centers every pair of wraps. The notes are troubleshooting observations, not a complete TK construction.
