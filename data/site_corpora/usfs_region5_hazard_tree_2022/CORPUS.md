# Hazard-tree inspection is not anchor certification

Source: Peter A. Angwin, Daniel R. Cluck, James Rosen, William C. Woodruff, Ashley E. Hawkins, Charles W. Barnes, Philip G. Cannon, and Sherry Hazelhurst, *Hazard Tree Identification and Mitigation*, USDA Forest Service, Pacific Southwest Region, Forest Health Protection Technical Report FHP Report RO-22-01, revised March 2022.

Rights and adaptation notice: this is a manually written, source-attributed digest of eligible federal text. It is not a reproduction of the report. All photographs, diagrams, figure artwork, source tables, forms, application screenshots, scoring layouts, and cited third-party material are excluded. The digest reorganizes and narrows the report to uncertainty, inspection context, defect interactions, target exposure, monitoring, and professional-referral boundaries.

## Scope: Region 5 hazard trees, not rope hardpoints

The report provides procedural guidance for USDA Forest Service employees in the Pacific Southwest Region, also called Region 5, and makes the material available to other forest-management practitioners. Its examples and rating judgments are rooted in California forests, National Forest System recreation sites, roads, facilities, local tree species, local disturbance history, and Forest Service responsibilities. It is not a universal arboriculture standard. (USDA Forest Service R5, 2022, pp. 2–5)

The source asks whether a tree or tree part might fail and strike a person or built target within an agency inspection-and-management cycle. It does not study a standing tree or branch as a load-bearing anchor. It contains no test of sling placement, bark compression, branch bending, dynamic loading, cyclic loading, shock loading, human suspension, bondage, or rescue loading. Its hazard ratings are not working-load limits. (pp. 2–7)

Tree health is not anchor capacity. A tree that appears vigorous, has no obvious defect, or receives a low hazard rating in a Region 5 program is not certified to support any load. A defect inspection and an engineered load-path assessment answer different questions.

This digest provides no tree, branch, sling, anchor, hardpoint, or load certification. It provides no construction or attachment instructions. Any contemplated load-bearing use of a tree requires both qualified arboricultural assessment of the living tree and qualified rigging or structural assessment of the proposed load path, components, forces, and consequences. Human suspension requires domain-specific expertise; this report cannot approve it.

## Failure cannot be predicted away

The report's most important statement is epistemic: even with high care, not every tree failure can be predicted. Some defects are hidden, trees and sites change, weather is variable, and inspection is a snapshot. The Forest Service cannot locate and mitigate every hazard tree. Professional judgment and a structured program can reduce risk, but cannot eliminate it. (p. 2)

The same limit appears later in the report. Tree failures will occur regardless of inspection intensity, and the actual location at which a tree or part comes to rest is always estimated. A post-catastrophe process may exclude genuine hazards or include trees that would not have caused harm. The source treats this imperfection as unavoidable rather than claiming that a score makes the future certain. (pp. 22, 36–39)

This uncertainty has two consequences:

- an inspection is not a guarantee that a tree will remain unchanged until the next visit;
- absence of a visible defect is not proof that no relevant defect exists.

Uncertainty is not a reason to ignore indicators. It is a reason to combine trained observation, site history, target exposure, documented judgment, monitoring, and specialist referral.

## Keep failure, defect, hazard, target, and loss separate

The report defines related concepts that should not be collapsed. (p. 3)

**A defect** is a flaw that reduces structural strength. A tree can have one defect, multiple defects, or a defect that is not detectable during an ordinary inspection. A visible feature is not automatically a defect, and a defect's significance depends on its extent, location, activity, interactions, and context.

**Failure** is mechanical breakage of the whole tree or a tree part. Failure describes what the tree does; it does not by itself describe the consequence.

**A target** is a person or built asset that could be struck. The report distinguishes stationary targets, such as occupied structures, parked vehicles, utilities, and sleeping people, from mobile targets, such as moving people or vehicles.

**Loss** is personal injury or monetary property damage. In the report's framework, mechanical failure produces a loss only when it affects a target.

**Hazard** combines the tree-side possibility of failure with the target-side possibility and consequence of impact. A defect without an exposed target is not the same management problem as the same defect over an occupied campsite. Conversely, a valuable or frequently occupied target does not prove that a nearby tree is defective.

A **hazard rating system** is an administrative prioritization tool. Region 5's system combines a target component and a detectable-defect component. It is not a physical measurement of breaking strength, an assurance of safety, or a substitute for judgment. This digest excludes the numerical score, action table, and species-specific rating rules to prevent use outside their program context. (pp. 3–7, 17–19)

## Inspection interval is a decision, not a universal calendar

The report frames a rating around whether failure might occur before the next inspection. That makes the inspection interval part of the question. Region 5 campgrounds, roads, and other sites do not all share one schedule. The source says frequency can vary with public use, tree-failure history, local insect, disease, weather, and fire impacts, site conditions, program priorities, resources, and the kind of facility. (pp. 3–4)

Within its agency setting, developed recreation sites are commonly inspected before their primary use season, with time for follow-up action. The report discusses more frequent full inspection at some sites, less frequent full inspection with interim observation at others, checks before reopening closed roads, and additional checks after major storms or fires. Those are Region 5 program examples, not a general promise that one annual visit is sufficient. (pp. 3–5)

The rationale for choosing or changing an interval should be documented and supported by local conditions. A calendar date should not override evidence of change. Major weather, fire, construction, erosion, flooding, new tree failure nearby, or a newly exposed target can justify reassessment before the routine cycle. (pp. 3–5, 10, 20–22)

For load-bearing questions, an ordinary hazard-tree inspection schedule is not enough. The proposed use creates a different loading question and needs its own current professional assessments.

## What a professional inspection integrates

The report recommends a systematic look at trees that can reach a target. In its Forest Service context, trained inspectors consider both the target and the tree, observe from different directions and distances, work under useful lighting, and document positive and negative observations. Local history and professional experience influence interpretation. (pp. 4–5)

This is not a do-it-yourself clearance checklist. Some relevant conditions are underground or internal. The source discusses specialized or invasive investigation, but also warns that drilling or cutting can wound a tree and should not be routine. This digest omits those procedures. Internal-decay testing, root investigation, disease identification, and tree-risk conclusions belong to qualified professionals using appropriate methods. (pp. 5, 7–16)

Forms cannot cover every situation. The source allows documented professional judgment when a form does not represent the observed risk, but that is not permission for an untrained person to override a result. Repeated uncertainty, regular need to override a form, or questions about a tree are reasons to obtain training or Forest Health Protection assistance in the source program. (p. 27)

## Target exposure changes the consequence side

Target assessment considers more than whether something is somewhere near a tree. The report combines whether a tree or part could reach the target, how likely an impact is if failure occurs, how severe the impact could be, and the value or vulnerability of what could be struck. (pp. 5–7, 17–18)

Exposure duration and frequency matter. A permanently occupied structure, an overnight sleeping area, a parking area, a place where people congregate, and a route used only briefly create different exposure patterns. A mobile target may spend less time in the potential impact area than a stationary target, but mobility does not make impact impossible. Traffic volume, occupancy, use pattern, and whether people stop in the area all affect exposure. (pp. 6–7, 17–18)

The size and height of the tree part, distance and direction, lean, slope, and obstacles affect possible impact and consequence. A falling tree can break before or on impact; parts can be deflected, slide or roll downhill, strike other trees, or become projectiles. Wind can alter both failure and dispersal. The report therefore treats any projected failure area as an estimate shaped by local conditions and judgment. (pp. 5–7, 37–39)

This digest deliberately excludes the report's numerical failure-zone multipliers. They are Region 5 public-land management conventions, not proof that everything outside a radius is safe, and not a method for choosing a human-suspension location.

## Defects should be read as interacting evidence

The report organizes visible and inferred defects into cracks; branch unions and forked tops; stem or branch decay; fungal fruiting bodies; cankers; dead trees, tops, and branches; beetle attack; root damage and disease; lean; and poor architecture. It repeatedly cautions that one category can change the meaning of another. (pp. 7–19)

Connected defects often matter more than isolated labels. A crack beside decay, a canker connected to a crack, a recent lean with root lifting, or a weak union with decay represents an interaction. Merely counting every unrelated imperfection is not equivalent. Region 5's point system distinguishes interacting defects from defects that do not affect the same failure mode. (pp. 7–19)

No compact digest can assign a rating from these signs. Species, defect location, remaining sound tissue, tree history, site history, weather exposure, target exposure, and the inspector's competence all change interpretation. The source's categorical examples are California and Region 5 guidance; they are not universal biological laws.

## Cracks: evidence of change, not one diagnosis

Vertical cracks can indicate that buckling or another failure process has begun, but cracks have multiple origins. The report discusses short-term mechanical injury, incomplete wound closure, frost, lightning, and other weather extremes. A crack may occur with substantial internal decay, with little decay, or without immediate failure. Location and association matter. (p. 7)

Features that increase concern in the source include a crack extending through a stem or branch, paired cracks around a decayed or hollow region, a crack at the base of a leaning tree, and contact with decay, a canker, or a weak union. These are prompts for professional evaluation, not a field formula. A bark fissure and a structural crack are not safely distinguished by name alone. (pp. 7–8)

Change over time is important. A documented crack can be compared at later inspections and after significant weather. Stability across one interval does not prove indefinite stability, and a tree without a visible crack can fail by another mechanism. (pp. 10, 20)

## Branch unions, old injury, and poor architecture

A branch union is where stems or branches join. Wood connection and bark inclusion influence the union. Included bark can prevent strong wood connection as stems expand, but simple U-shaped-versus-V-shaped mnemonics are incomplete: the source notes exceptions and associated features. Cracks, decay, cankers, pitch, relative stem size, old top loss, and loading history all affect interpretation. (pp. 8–9)

Forked tops can arise from ordinary development or after the original leader breaks or dies. Replacement leaders may be associated with hidden decay near the old injury. Epicormic shoots can arise after injury, pruning, decline, or environmental stress; large shoots on a decaying support deserve attention because their attachment and increasing mass can interact. (pp. 8–9)

Poor architecture can reflect past breakage or poor pruning. The report discusses acute angles, several branches from one point, codominant stems with included bark, sharp bends, unbalanced crowns, and weakly attached regrowth after topping. Nearby tree removal or pruning can also change wind exposure and crown balance. These features require whole-tree and site interpretation rather than automatic rejection or approval. (pp. 16–17)

## Decay, cavities, fruiting bodies, and cankers

Internal decay can be difficult to see. Crown health and vigor are not reliable measures of internal wood condition. The report lists possible indicators such as hollow knots, wounds, broken or dead tops, cracks, cavities used by wildlife, fungal fruiting bodies, and activity by wood-associated insects. None alone measures the extent or position of decay. (pp. 9–12)

Decay is a major contributor to failure, but the presence of decay alone does not tell the whole story. Structural significance depends on where decay is located, how extensive it is, how much and where sound tissue remains, whether a cavity opens outward, whether the affected part is horizontal, and whether lean, cracking, a weak union, or another defect adds load or reduces capacity. The digest excludes the report's numerical shell rule because the report itself describes exceptions and because the rule is not an anchor-capacity calculation. (pp. 9–11)

A fruiting body can signal a decay organism, but interpretation depends on the fungus, host, number and location of fruiting bodies, associated swelling or injury, and local failure history. Species-specific conk lists and rating thresholds are omitted. Correct identification and assessment require suitable expertise. (pp. 10–11)

A canker is an area of dead cambium or exposed sapwood and can be associated with fungi, insects, weather, fire, or mechanical injury. Underlying wood may be decayed. Cankers can expand, reduce effective cross-section, or concentrate failure near the affected area. Concern increases when a canker connects to decay, a crack, lean, or another defect. The source's species and circumference thresholds remain Region 5 rules and are not reproduced here. (pp. 11–13)

Fire injury can kill cambium beneath char, but char appearance does not always map consistently to cambial death. The source describes local confirmation before treating visible char as a reliable proxy. This is another example of why a surface sign is evidence rather than a universal diagnosis. (p. 12)

## Dead trees, tops, branches, and biological attack

Reliably predicting when a dead tree will fail is nearly impossible. Failure timing varies with the cause and time of death, structural and genetic features, decay progression, and site and weather. A dead top or branch matters to hazard assessment only when it can affect a target, but apparent persistence is not a load rating. (pp. 13–14)

Dead or partly dead material should be interpreted with evidence of decay, prior breakage, hanging or lodged parts, damaged bark, and changing attachment. The report contains species- and size-specific categories; those are omitted rather than generalized beyond California or converted into branch approval rules. (pp. 13–14)

Bark and woodboring insects can interact with drought, disease, fire injury, or existing stress. Some attacks kill trees; some provide entry for decay organisms; and some accelerate deterioration. Boring material, pitch, fading crown, woodpecker activity, and nearby mortality can have multiple causes. The report's insect, host, color, percentage, and rating criteria require local expertise and are not reproduced as a universal checklist. (p. 14)

## Roots and the site around the tree

Root problems are difficult to find because much of the system is underground. The report separates physical damage from biological root disease and decay. Physical problems include roots that are undermined, severed, loosened, cracked, broken, burned, exposed, or girdling the stem. (pp. 15–16)

Site history is essential. Soil compaction, erosion, fire, flooding or prolonged saturation, excavation and construction, prolonged heavy-equipment use, and repeated foot or vehicle traffic can affect roots or the soil that supports them. A tree may look unchanged above ground while its support environment has changed. (p. 15)

Possible signs of root or support change include:

- new soil cracks, mounding, or movement near the base;
- root-plate lifting, broken roots, or partial windthrow;
- a recent or increasing lean;
- exposed or undermined roots after erosion;
- crown thinning, loss of terminal growth, discoloration, or other decline;
- resin near the base, butt swelling, root or butt decay, or fungal fruiting bodies;
- nearby fading, dead, uprooted, or shattered trees.

These signs are neither necessary nor sufficient for one diagnosis. The source notes that a green, healthy-looking tree can still have root disease, while crown decline can arise from causes other than root decay. Disease centers and neighboring failures provide context, not automatic proof about every nearby tree. Species-specific disease rules and distance cutoffs are omitted. (pp. 15–16)

For a proposed load-bearing use, soil and root condition are only part of the question. They do not reveal branch attachment capacity, sling effects, load direction, dynamic force, or the entire load path.

## Recent lean and long-compensated lean are different observations

A lean can arise gradually as a tree grows toward light or responds to wind, slope movement, or soil conditions. Longstanding trees may develop an upswept leader and compensating growth. A recent change in lean, by contrast, can accompany support loss or active movement. (pp. 16–17)

The distinction is temporal, not a guarantee. Corrected growth is evidence that the tree has responded over time; it does not certify strong roots or future stability. An uncorrected or recently increasing lean is more concerning when paired with root lifting, soil cracking or mounding, saturation, construction disturbance, unstable soil, fire injury, decay, trunk cracks, an unbalanced crown, or a history of storm damage. (pp. 16–17)

A bowed stem can reflect wind, snow, or ice loading. Old compensating growth may indicate persistence, while cracks or other defects and recurrent loading alter the interpretation. Photographs and prior records can help establish whether a lean or bow is changing, but only a qualified assessment can decide its significance. (pp. 16–17)

## Weather, disturbance, and changing exposure

Inspection is conditional on the site as it exists at the time. Storms can add wind, snow, ice, and water loading. Saturated soil can change root support. Fire can injure roots, cambium, crown, and wood. Erosion or flooding can expose or undermine roots. Drought, insects, and pathogens can change mortality and decay. Construction, excavation, vehicle traffic, heavy equipment, and concentrated foot traffic can compact soil or damage roots. (pp. 3–5, 12, 14–17)

Changes around the tree can matter even when the tree itself was not cut. Removing neighboring trees can change wind exposure. Pruning can change crown balance and create wounds. A newly placed or more frequently occupied target changes the consequence side of the hazard problem. A site that was acceptable for one use pattern is not automatically acceptable after occupancy, traffic, topography, drainage, or vegetation changes. (pp. 3–7, 16–18)

Weather does not operate as a simple trigger with one universal threshold. The report says local storm and failure history should influence inspection priorities and that monitored trees may need review after significant weather. Wind direction and speed at a future failure cannot be known in advance. (pp. 3–5, 10, 20, 39)

## Monitoring needs a question, a record, and a trigger

Monitoring is not the same as deciding that a defect is harmless. A useful record identifies the tree and target context, describes the observed feature, records both positive and negative findings, gives the date and conditions, and preserves the reason for the professional judgment. Repeated observations can show change in cracks, lean, crown, roots, decay indicators, surrounding trees, or target exposure. (pp. 4–5, 20, 22, 27)

The source encourages documentation even when no hazard tree is found at an inspected public-use site. Records help compare conditions, track trends in insects, disease, and defects, plan follow-up, and learn from later failures. When a tree or part fails and affects a target, useful information includes the apparent failure reason, loss, target type, tree-to-target relationship, slope, aspect, lean, and other site and tree features. (p. 22)

The administrative form, mapping codes, mobile application, score sheet, and suggested-action table are excluded. The retained lesson is the observation trail, not a particular database or workflow.

A monitoring plan should identify what is being watched, who is qualified to interpret it, when routine reassessment occurs, and which changes or events prompt earlier reassessment. It cannot promise that failure will wait for the next scheduled inspection.

## Refer when the question exceeds local competence

The report directs Region 5 staff toward Forest Health Protection specialists for training, difficult diagnoses, and local insect, disease, and hazard-tree questions. In its mitigation section, it recommends consulting a certified arborist when local expertise is absent for work on large hardwoods and consulting professional arborists for specialized actions. The mitigation procedures themselves are excluded here. (pp. 20–23, 27)

That referral principle matters more than memorizing the report's California disease list. Internal decay, root disease, new lean, soil movement, fire injury, complex architecture, interacting defects, and valuable or heavily occupied targets can all exceed a casual visual assessment.

For any proposed tree-supported load, this corpus applies a stricter two-domain gate:

1. A qualified arboricultural professional evaluates the living tree, root environment, defects, disease, change, and foreseeable tree failure modes.
2. A qualified rigging or structural professional evaluates the actual proposed load path, directions, magnitudes, dynamics, components, attachment effects, redundancies, and consequences.

One professional role does not automatically supply the other. A certified arborist's tree-risk opinion is not a sling or human-suspension design, and a rigger's component knowledge is not a diagnosis of root disease or internal decay.

## What this report can and cannot support

The report supports these bounded principles:

- no inspection can predict every tree failure;
- risk can be reduced but not eliminated;
- defect, failure, target, hazard, and loss are distinct concepts;
- inspection interval depends on use, history, site conditions, disturbance, and program context;
- cracks, decay, cankers, dead parts, root problems, lean, and architecture need contextual interpretation;
- recent change and interacting defects can matter more than a static label;
- target occupancy and exposure affect consequence without proving tree failure;
- site and soil disturbance can change a tree without an obvious crown response;
- monitoring and records help detect change but do not certify future stability;
- uncertainty and questions beyond competence require qualified referral.

The report does not support:

- certifying a tree or branch for a sling, anchor, hardpoint, or human load;
- treating healthy foliage, corrected lean, or absence of visible defects as proof of capacity;
- converting a hazard score into a working-load limit or safety factor;
- selecting an attachment point, wrap, sling, knot, direction, or load;
- designing an anchor, backup, frame, or suspension system;
- using Region 5 species, disease, decay-shell, size, distance, or rating thresholds as universal rules;
- relying on a numerical failure zone as a guarantee that a location is safe;
- performing invasive testing, pruning, topping, cutting, felling, cabling, bracing, or mitigation from this digest;
- claiming that an arboricultural assessment alone approves rigging or that a rigging assessment alone approves the tree.

## Compact interpretation discipline

When using this source as background:

1. Keep the Region 5 and California scope attached.
2. Start with the impossibility of predicting every failure and the reduce-not-eliminate framing.
3. Separate the tree-side defect question from the target-side exposure question.
4. Ask whether a condition is recent, changing, connected to another defect, or associated with site disturbance.
5. Treat visual absence as limited evidence, especially for internal decay and underground roots.
6. Preserve weather, inspection interval, and local-history context.
7. Do not universalize species, disease, scoring, decay, or distance rules.
8. Do not translate hazard-tree reasoning into anchor capacity.
9. Require arboricultural and rigging or structural expertise for a proposed load-bearing use.
