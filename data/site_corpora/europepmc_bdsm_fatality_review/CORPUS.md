# Reading a forensic review of reported BDSM fatalities without inventing a risk rate

Source: Anouk Schori, Christian Jackowski, and Corinna A. Schön, “How safe is BDSM? A literature review on fatal outcome in BDSM play,” *International Journal of Legal Medicine* 136, 287–295, first published 12 August 2021, DOI 10.1007/s00414-021-02674-0, PMCID PMC8813685, PMID 34383118.

License and adaptation notice: the article is licensed CC BY 4.0. This is a manually written, attributed, safety-focused adaptation. It reorganizes and critiques the evidence, corrects the source's “BDMS” typographical slips, omits case narratives and technique detail, and marks statements that the review's design cannot support.

## Scope and safety boundary

This article is a forensic literature review of published deaths reported in connection with partnered BDSM activity. It is not a cohort study of BDSM participants, a surveillance study, a population survey, a clinical guideline, or a rope-technique manual. Its 17 cases are a set of cases found in publications, not a denominator of people, scenes, rope sessions, breath-control events, or hours of exposure. Nothing in the paper establishes the probability that a particular BDSM activity will cause death. (Schori et al., 2021, Abstract; Methods; Results; Strengths and limitations)

The review's selected reports are dominated by fatal neck-compression and asphyxia events. That supports a narrow safety conclusion: external neck compression, airway or oxygen restriction, and intentional loss of consciousness can have fatal outcomes, including when participants have prior experience. It does not support the claim that ordinary rope bondage in general has the same risk profile, nor does it provide a safer way to perform breath control. The source itself says the health risks of breath-control activity cannot be eliminated. (Discussion—BDSM activities; Results—Cause of death)

No case narrative, apparatus layout, body position, sequence of failure, or reconstruction is retained here. Such detail adds little to the valid inference and could become an unsafe instructional template. The useful material is the review method, aggregate counts, category distinctions, missingness, and the limits on causal or prevalence claims.

## The question the review attempted to answer

The authors sought published reports of non-natural deaths associated with consensually described BDSM activity involving more than one person. They were interested in reported causes of death, practitioner experience and education, and community safeguards. Papers solely about autoerotic death were excluded because those events involve a different participation structure. (Methods)

That scope creates several boundaries:

- **Partnered, consensually described BDSM cases** were the intended case class.
- **Autoerotic fatalities** were excluded from case collection, although the discussion compares them with the partnered reports.
- **Natural deaths during sexual activity** were discussed as a comparison but were not part of the 17-case set.
- **Non-natural deaths associated with sexual activity** formed a broader category in one included German postmortem study.
- **Published reports** were the discovery frame; the review did not search a population death registry or a BDSM-participant cohort. (Methods; Results; Discussion—BDSM activities)

“Consensual” in this setting is an inclusion description inherited from retrospective reports. A deceased person cannot be interviewed, and forensic records may have incomplete or disputed evidence about negotiation, intent, and what happened. The review itself discusses scene alteration, witness stress, stigma, and inconsistent statements as reconstruction problems. Therefore, inclusion in the review should not be treated as independent proof that every act in every case remained consensual. (Discussion—Causes of death)

## Search and classification method

The authors searched PubMed, Embase through OvidSP, and Medline through OvidSP for German- and English-language articles. Their query combined BDSM, sadism, masochism, erotic death, asphyxiophilia, sexual asphyxia, kink, and death concepts. Reports limited to autoerotic death were excluded. To make differently described asphyxia cases more comparable, the authors adopted a published forensic classification. (Methods)

The report does not provide several elements expected from a modern systematic review:

- a search date or complete database-specific search strategy;
- a registered protocol;
- duplicate independent screening or extraction;
- a flow diagram showing records found, deduplicated, excluded, and retained;
- a formal appraisal of case-report quality or risk of bias;
- a comprehensive grey-literature, registry, or non-German/non-English search;
- a method for detecting duplicate publication of the same incident. (Methods; Strengths and limitations)

The appropriate label is therefore a literature review and case compilation, not a complete census. Failure to find a case is not evidence that it did not occur.

## Evidence base and denominators

The review found 14 case reports and one study published between 1986 and 2020. Across those publications, the authors tabulated 17 deaths. One included study was a 25-year medicolegal postmortem series in Germany covering 1993–2017. That study contained 16,437 autopsies, including 74 non-natural deaths associated with sexual activity; three of those 74 were categorized as BDSM-associated. (Results)

Those numbers have three distinct denominators:

| Reported quantity | Numerator | Denominator | What it describes |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| Cases in the literature review | 17 | no exposed population denominator | cases located in selected publications |
| BDSM-associated cases within the German sexual-death subset | 3 | 74 | share of non-natural sexual-activity deaths in one postmortem series |
| BDSM-associated cases within all autopsies in that series | 3 | 16,437 | share of autopsies in that institution or study frame |

The second ratio is about a selected set of non-natural sexual-activity deaths. The third is about autopsies. Neither is the risk per BDSM practitioner or per scene, and neither supplies a general-population incidence rate. The 14 standalone case reports supply no denominator at all. (Results; Discussion—BDSM activities)

The paper describes the fatal outcome as rare and compares the selected BDSM reports with autoerotic and natural sexual-activity deaths. Such comparisons should be treated cautiously. The underlying studies use different case definitions, jurisdictions, periods, ascertainment systems, and denominators. A count of published case reports cannot establish that one activity is more or less common than another. (Discussion—BDSM activities; Strengths and limitations)

## Aggregate findings, with missingness attached

The following counts describe only the 17 published cases collected by the review:

- The review categorized 15 of 17 deaths as strangulation, or 88.2 percent of its case set.
- The two remaining reported causes were one suffocation or oronasal-occlusion case and one severe-hemorrhage case in which alcohol intoxication was also reported.
- A toxicology result for the deceased was mentioned in 13 of 17 cases. Eight of those 13 were reported positive, and five negative.
- In four cases, the partner reportedly tested positive for the same substance as the deceased.
- Experience information was sufficiently described for both participants in nine cases; the review characterized both participants in those nine as not new to BDSM.
- Safeword use was almost entirely missing: one case explicitly noted no safeword, while the other 16 did not report the variable. (Abstract; Results—Experience; Cause of death; Toxicological examinations; Discussion—BDSM communities and safeguards)

The source also states that drugs or alcohol were involved in 64.3 percent of fatal BDSM play. That percentage does not use all 17 cases as its denominator, because 64.3 percent corresponds to 9 of 14 rather than a fraction of 17. The text appears to count nine cases with a positive finding for at least one participant among 14 cases with some toxicology or intoxication information, but it does not clearly define that denominator. This digest retains the underlying reported counts and treats the 64.3-percent statement as denominator-ambiguous. (Abstract; Results—Toxicological examinations; source case table)

Aggregated age and sex descriptions do not solve the denominator problem. They characterize the published cases, not who practices BDSM or who is at risk. Small selected case sets should not be used to profile a “typical” victim or participant.

## Forensic category distinctions

The review separates four broad asphyxia categories: strangulation, suffocation, mechanical asphyxia, and drowning. Within its scheme:

- **Strangulation** is external neck compression and includes manual strangulation, ligature strangulation, and hanging.
- **Suffocation** covers mechanisms that obstruct respiratory openings or airways or deprive a person of an adequate atmosphere; the source distinguishes smothering from choking.
- **Mechanical asphyxia** is presented as an umbrella for position-related or external-compression mechanisms that prevent effective breathing.
- **Drowning** is treated separately as respiratory impairment associated with immersion. (Methods; source Table 1)

This vocabulary matters because colloquial “choking” is often used for external neck compression. In the review's forensic scheme, external pressure on the neck is strangulation; choking refers to blockage below the epiglottis. Using the mechanism rather than a scene label reduces ambiguity. (Discussion—Causes of death)

The source table contains a likely internal label inversion. Its row labeled “traumatic asphyxia” describes inability to breathe because of body position, while its row labeled “positional asphyxia” describes external chest compression by a heavy object. This digest does not silently teach either row as authoritative. A forensic or clinical answer should verify current specialist definitions rather than rely on that table alone.

Category discipline is also necessary at a higher level. Partnered BDSM fatalities, autoerotic fatalities, homicide, suicide, natural deaths during sexual activity, and deaths whose manner remains uncertain are not interchangeable categories. Scene appearance alone may not determine intent, consent, or manner of death. (Methods; Discussion—Causes of death)

## What the selected cause distribution does and does not show

Fifteen strangulation cases among 17 published cases show that lethal neck-compression events were prominent in the literature the authors located. The result is directly relevant to warnings about breath control and neck compression. It remains a distribution within a publication-derived case set. (Results—Cause of death; Discussion—Causes of death)

It does not show that:

- the selected 88.2-percent figure generalizes beyond this review's published cases;
- fatal and nonfatal BDSM incidents have the same cause distribution;
- rope bondage generally produces the same risk as neck compression;
- a nonfatal history establishes a safe threshold;
- a particular setup can be made safe by reproducing or modifying a reported case;
- experience, instruction, an observer, a safeword, or resuscitation knowledge removes the hazard.

Publication bias is especially plausible for unusual, forensically complex, or sensational mechanisms. Common but nonfatal injuries are outside the review by design. Fatal events that were not recognized, reported, published, indexed, or written in German or English could be absent. Therefore, the observed 15-of-17 concentration may reflect both genuine hazard and the way cases reach journals. (Methods; Strengths and limitations)

## Toxicology: association is not causation

Eight positive results among the 13 deceased persons with reported toxicology show that substances were detected in a majority of that documented subset. The case compilation does not include an uninjured or nonfatal comparison group, consistent testing panels, concentrations for every finding, standardized impairment assessment, or a denominator for substance use across BDSM activity. Missing toxicology is not equivalent to a negative result. Detection is not automatically proof of impairment, contribution, or causation. (Results—Toxicological examinations; Discussion—Toxicology)

The review's conclusion says alcohol or drug use “correlates” with fatalities. A set containing only fatalities cannot estimate a correlation between exposure and fatal outcome: it has no outcome-negative comparison group. At most, the paper documents co-occurrence in selected fatal reports. It is reasonable safety practice to avoid intoxicants when judgment, communication, motor control, monitoring, or emergency response matter, but the review does not quantify how much a substance changes fatality risk. (Discussion—Toxicology; Conclusion)

Some toxicology labels in the case source material are vague, and the paper acknowledges that particular labels were not clarified. Combining different substances, doses, tests, and partner findings into one percentage further limits interpretation. Answers derived from this source should describe reported positive toxicology in selected cases without assigning a causal percentage.

## Experience, knowledge, and safeguards

The review says both participants were not new to BDSM in all nine cases where experience for both was described. That observation argues against treating familiarity as immunity. It does not show whether participants were competent in the specific activity, whether their information was accurate, or whether a safety practice was followed. “Experienced” was inherited from heterogeneous case reports rather than measured with a common scale. (Results—Experience in BDSM)

The conclusion also associates “lack of knowledge” with fatalities, but the compiled data do not support that association. Knowledge was not measured consistently; experience was missing or partial in eight cases; and the nine cases with information involved people described as non-novices. There was no comparison group of nonfatal scenes. The defensible finding is that prior exposure or instruction did not guarantee prevention in the published cases. (Results—Experience in BDSM; Conclusion)

The article describes community education, mentoring, negotiation, first aid, safewords, traffic-light communication, emergency planning, and rapid-release readiness as safeguards discussed in BDSM communities. These are contextual recommendations, not interventions evaluated by the review. The case series cannot estimate their effectiveness. (Discussion—BDSM communities and safeguards)

Safeword data are a particularly clear example of missingness. One reported absence and 16 unreported cases cannot establish an association in either direction. A safeword is a communication tool, not proof that a high-risk mechanism is safe, and communication can fail when consciousness, speech, breathing, or motor control is impaired.

The article is internally inconsistent about pre-event instruction and resuscitation discussion. The results section describes two cases in which partners were instructed about breath control and taught cardiopulmonary resuscitation; the safeguards discussion refers to three cases in which instruction and resuscitation were discussed. Without a reconciled extraction table, this digest does not choose one count. It records the discrepancy and retains only the broader lesson that preparation and attempted resuscitation did not prevent every reported death. (Results—Experience in BDSM; Discussion—BDSM communities and safeguards)

## Forensic reconstruction and stigma

The review notes that post-event reconstruction can be distorted by emergency intervention, scene changes, fear, shame, witness trauma, or incomplete statements. Stigma may also discourage disclosure and medical help. These points justify careful, nonjudgmental fact gathering. They do not justify assuming that a scene was altered or that a surviving partner is deceptive. (Discussion—Causes of death; Strengths and limitations)

For corpus use, the transferable principles are:

- describe observed mechanism and evidence separately from inferred intent;
- distinguish what a report documented from what it omitted;
- preserve uncertainty about consent, chronology, and manner of death;
- avoid stigmatizing BDSM interest as itself diagnostic of a cause or intent;
- avoid treating community terminology as a substitute for forensic terminology;
- never reconstruct a dangerous technique as an instructional sequence.

The paper suggests that forensic investigators may seek contextual materials to understand an event. This digest omits its operational reconstruction detail because it is outside rope education and could encourage intrusive or sensational treatment of a death.

## Strong conclusions and overclaims

The source supports these bounded statements:

- The authors found 17 published partnered BDSM-associated fatal cases under their search and language criteria.
- Fifteen were categorized as strangulation under the review's chosen forensic scheme.
- Toxicology, experience, and safeword reporting were incomplete.
- Some fatal reports involved participants described as experienced.
- The paper documents the need for precise mechanism language and emergency readiness.
- The evidence base is small and shaped by publication and ascertainment. (Methods; Results; Strengths and limitations)

The source does not establish these stronger claims:

- a population incidence or per-scene fatality rate;
- that BDSM fatalities are rarer than another class under comparable denominators;
- that alcohol, drugs, inexperience, or absent safeguards caused the selected deaths as a group;
- that all 17 cases were independently verified as consensual throughout;
- that any safeguard makes breath control or neck compression safe;
- universal preventability;
- that the selected cases represent rope bondage as a whole.

The abstract and conclusion use preventability and correlation language more strongly than the review design warrants. A safety-oriented model should preserve the authors' recommendations while declining to present those causal claims as measured results.

## Practical evidence discipline

When this paper is used to answer a safety question:

1. Name it as a literature review of published fatal cases.
2. State the 17-case publication-derived denominator.
3. Attach missingness to every subgroup count.
4. Keep partnered BDSM, autoerotic activity, natural death, and broader sexual-activity death categories distinct.
5. Use mechanism terms such as external neck compression rather than ambiguous scene slang.
6. Treat the 15-of-17 result as a signal about selected fatal reports, not a prevalence estimate.
7. Describe toxicology as detection or reported involvement, not proven impairment or cause.
8. Say that experience and community safeguards did not eliminate risk; do not estimate their protective effect from this review.
9. Do not reproduce a fatal configuration or turn a case into a how-to example.
10. Emphasize that breath control and neck compression retain potentially fatal risk and are not made safe by a numerical threshold, safeword, observer, or prior success.
