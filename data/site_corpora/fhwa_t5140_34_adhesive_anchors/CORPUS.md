# FHWA adhesive-anchor failure and governance lessons

## Evidence identity and strict boundary

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: This digest is derived from the Federal Highway Administration technical advisory *Use and Inspection of Adhesive Anchors in Federal-Aid Projects*. The advisory is dated January 16, 2018 and addresses governance of adhesive-anchor systems in Federal-aid civil-infrastructure work. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence boundary: The advisory identifies its initiating context as the investigated ceiling-panel collapse in a Boston tunnel. It provides no casualty detail, so this digest does not characterize consequences beyond the documented collapse. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence boundary: This is civil-infrastructure governance evidence, not a design, installation, testing, or maintenance guide. It does not certify, size, select, rate, or establish capacity for a ceiling attachment, DIY hardpoint, rope anchor, or human-suspension system. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–4]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The advisory is agency guidance prompted by an investigated failure and concerned with industry governance. It is not a controlled experiment, a comparative product study, or a capacity dataset. Its useful contribution here is the failure chain and the allocation of qualification, installation, inspection, and owner responsibilities. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–3]

## Failure chain: time-dependent behavior and execution

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: FHWA links the tunnel ceiling-panel collapse to two interacting causes. It identifies inadequate creep resistance in the adhesive system as the primary cause and irregular overhead installation as a contributing factor. The event therefore cannot be reduced to either material behavior or workmanship alone. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The failure account makes time-dependent behavior part of system safety. An attachment can appear stable initially yet remain vulnerable when an adhesive system does not adequately resist creep under persistent tension. Initial installation and later sustained behavior are separate questions. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1, 3]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: Overhead installation adds execution difficulty. FHWA treats irregular installation under that condition as a meaningful contributor rather than background noise. A nominally qualified system does not erase installation sensitivity, and an apparently completed installation does not demonstrate that the required process was followed. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The advisory frames performance as a property of the anchor system used under its relevant condition, not as a generic property of an adhesive category. Qualification must therefore address the system and the condition in which it is intended to work. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–2]

## Sustained tension and creep

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: FHWA distinguishes a force that remains meaningfully persistent from a transient force. The governance lesson is qualitative: when tension persists, time-dependent creep must be considered rather than treating the force as a brief event. This digest omits the advisory's examples, classifications, and durations. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 2–3]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: A vertically overhead anchor carrying direct tension is one clear sustained-tension case in the advisory. FHWA also warns that orientation by itself is not enough to classify the condition, because sustained tension can arise in other orientations through the mechanics of a larger structural system. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 2]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The relevant question is how the whole structure transfers a persistent force into the anchorage, not merely which direction the anchor points. This system view prevents a horizontal orientation from being treated automatically as free of sustained tension. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 2]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: Operational labels do not settle whether a force is sustained. FHWA notes that a force commonly described as temporary can persist long enough to raise the same creep concern. Owners therefore need a defined method for deciding which conditions their engineers will treat as sustained. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 3]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The advisory treats creep as a long-term system behavior that can change what remains available from an initially established force. It does not support inferring future performance from initial force alone. The exact mechanics and examples are intentionally omitted from this digest. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 3]

## Qualification and trained installation

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: FHWA's governance concern extends beyond design calculations. The advisory identifies both system qualification and installer qualification as necessary controls. A sound governance scheme asks whether the system was qualified for the intended condition and whether the person performing the work was trained for that task. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: Installation training matters because anchor performance is sensitive to execution parameters. The advisory's high-level lesson is that trained personnel should carry out adhesive-anchor installation and should follow the qualified process. All detailed installation steps and third-party certification language are excluded here. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: Whole-system qualification and trained installation are complementary. Qualification evaluates an anchor system for a relevant condition; training addresses whether that system is installed through the intended process. Passing one governance gate cannot substitute for the other. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–2]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The advisory's revised posture rests on stronger governance of system evaluation and installation rather than on a claim that adhesive anchors are universally suitable. Necessity, intended condition, qualification, and execution remain linked decisions. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–2]

## Continuous inspection and owner accountability

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: FHWA identifies continuous inspection as a control for installations in orientations that resist sustained tension. In this digest, “continuous inspection” is retained only as a governance requirement; no inspection method, acceptance criterion, or field procedure is supplied. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The owner remains responsible for defining inspector qualifications. Naming an inspection requirement without defining who is competent to perform it leaves a governance gap. The advisory therefore separates the existence of inspection from the qualification of the inspector. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 1]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: For existing installations whose qualification for persistent tension is absent or uncertain, FHWA calls for an owner-controlled response. The response may involve qualified inspection, engineering evaluation or testing, and remediation or replacement, selected through professional processes appropriate to the structure. No procedure or threshold is transferred here. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 2]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The advisory links the treatment of existing anchors to the importance and redundancy of the civil-infrastructure application. That is a risk-governance principle, not a transferable formula. It says the owner should establish an appropriate confidence process rather than assume that continued presence proves adequate long-term performance. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 2]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: Owners also need guidance for identifying persistent versus transient conditions. This responsibility cannot be discharged by copying the source's examples because structural force paths, service conditions, and project governance differ. The retained lesson is to define the classification process and assign qualified roles within the applicable civil system. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF p. 3]

## Combined governance model and limits

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: The advisory's controls form a chain: recognize persistent tension and creep exposure; qualify the complete anchor system for its intended condition; use trained installation; continuously inspect the relevant work; define inspector qualifications; and place unresolved existing anchors into a qualified owner process. Each control addresses a different failure opportunity. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–3]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence: No single control in this chain substitutes for the others. The FHWA account joins time-dependent system behavior, installation sensitivity, independent inspection, and owner governance. It does not supply a shortcut around professional structural evaluation. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–3]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence boundary: All incorporated standards text, certification-program language, organization marks, product or material detail, exact load classes, durations, installation and testing procedures, the structural figure, and the reproduced load table are excluded. The retained prose neither reproduces those components nor claims equivalence to them. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–4]

Non-bondage civil-infrastructure evidence boundary: Nothing in this advisory digest evaluates a particular building, ceiling, fastener, substrate, hardpoint, rope system, or person-support application. A qualified professional must evaluate real civil infrastructure under the governing requirements; the digest provides no human-suspension capacity inference. [FHWA-T5140.34-2018, PDF pp. 1–4]
