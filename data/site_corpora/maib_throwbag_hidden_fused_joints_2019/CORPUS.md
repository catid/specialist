# Hidden fused joints in a throw-bag rescue line: a rights-filtered failure-analysis digest

## Source identity and evidence boundary

This digest covers the Marine Accident Investigation Branch report *Failure of a throw bag rescue line during a capsize drill on an unnamed rowing boat, Widnes, UK, 24 March 2018*. MAIB issued it as Report No. 2/2019 in January 2019, and GOV.UK published it on 31 January 2019. The retained evidence concerns a manufactured rescue product whose nominally continuous line actually contained hidden construction joints. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF p. 1]

This one rescue-product defect case does not validate a bondage-rope inspection or joining rule. It is a case investigation of a particular manufactured product and supply chain. It does not establish a general failure rate, certify an inspection method, or provide instructions for making or using a rope assembly in another setting. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1, 5–7]

The source is a short casualty report, not a controlled product-population study. MAIB combined examination of the failed item, follow-up information about comparable items, a commissioned comparison of intact and joined samples, and an analysis of production quality controls. The credited laboratory details and external publications are excluded here; only MAIB's Crown-authored qualitative synthesis is retained. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1–7]

## The apparent line was an assembly of segments

Close examination found that the rescue line was not one continuous manufactured length. It comprised several rope segments connected by thermal fusion. A connection between segments was the location that separated during the drill. The consequential discontinuity was therefore introduced during construction rather than arising solely from gradual wear of otherwise continuous rope. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1–2] [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF p. 5]

That distinction changes the unit of analysis. “The rope” was visually presented and sold as one line, but mechanically it contained intact spans and constructed joints. The load path crossed both types of region in series, so the assembly could perform only as well as its weakest discontinuity. A property of intact rope did not establish the behavior of the completed line. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1, 3, 5]

Thermal fusion is retained only as the report's identification of how the segments had been connected. The report evidence used here does not specify a joining process, process settings, acceptance criteria, or a method for reproducing such a connection. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1–3, 5]

## Intact spans and joined spans behaved differently

The commissioned comparison separated intact samples from samples containing a fused joint. MAIB's analysis reports a large qualitative difference: joined samples separated under much less load than intact rope samples. Their deformation response also differed. The joint was therefore not merely a cosmetic interruption; it created a mechanically distinct class of region. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5]

The finding does not supply a transferable numerical factor in this digest. It supports the narrower conclusion that testing an intact span cannot be assumed to characterize a finished assembly containing an undocumented joint. Material continuity is part of the test article definition. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5, 7]

The final separation occurred at a joint, consistent with the comparison between joined and intact samples. MAIB accordingly treated the construction discontinuity as the failure mechanism in this case. That conclusion is specific to the examined product; it is not evidence that every joint made by any process has the same behavior. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1–3, 5]

## The defect was not confined to the first item

Follow-up examination and notification identified other rescue lines with comparable fused joints. The report also records prior indications of similar construction discontinuities outside the single failed item. These observations make a one-off handling accident an inadequate explanation and point toward a recurring production-control problem. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1–2, 5]

Comparable defects beyond the initiating item strengthen the inference that the joint arose before use. They do not, however, reveal the prevalence of the defect across all products. The total production population and complete inspection history were not available, and some affected items could have remained unidentified. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 2–3, 5]

This is a useful distinction between recurrence and incidence. Finding the same defect in multiple items shows that the mechanism was repeatable or repeated. It does not by itself estimate how often it occurred, which batches were unaffected, or how detection probability varied among inspectors. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF p. 5]

## Why external visual checks missed the discontinuity

The importing organization used random visual checks on finished products. Those checks did not prevent joined lines from reaching users. A line can look like one product when coiled or packed even though its load path contains connections that require deliberate exposure and examination to identify. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 2–3, 5]

Visual quality control was limited in two separate ways. Sampling left many individual products unexamined, and external viewing was not a validated test of continuity or joint behavior. Increasing the care of an inspector would not, by itself, resolve either the coverage problem or the lack of a proven relationship between appearance and mechanical behavior. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5–6]

The report therefore distinguishes a visible finished product from a verified continuous load path. External appearance can identify some gross anomalies, but absence of an observed anomaly is not evidence that every internal or concealed region shares the properties of an intact span. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 2–3, 5]

Random inspection also cannot repair an uncontrolled process. Sampling can discover output defects, but a missed sample leaves the manufacturing mechanism unchanged. When the production step capable of creating a discontinuity is not understood and controlled, the next unexamined unit can repeat it. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5–6]

## Distinct safeguards answer distinct questions

The report's quality-control discussion separates several functions that are sometimes collapsed into “inspection.” Traceability, batch control, process validation, finished-product checking, and independent verification answer different questions. None is a complete substitute for the others. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5–7]

Traceability links an individual finished item to its production history. It makes it possible to ask where and when that item was made and which production records belong to it. Without a stable item identity, later evidence about a defect cannot be connected reliably to the relevant production history. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5, 7]

Batch control groups items that share a meaningful production context. It can bound an investigation and reveal whether comparable defects cluster around common inputs or manufacturing conditions. A batch label does not show that a process was capable, and it does not prove that each item in the batch is sound. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 2–3, 5, 7]

Process validation addresses whether the manufacturing sequence consistently produces the intended continuous assembly. It focuses on the process capable of introducing the joint, rather than waiting for a finished-product sample to expose the outcome. The source does not provide a fusion-process recipe or validation protocol; the retained lesson is only that process control and output sampling are different safeguards. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 5–6]

Finished-product checks address the condition of selected outputs. They can reveal a defect that process records missed, but random checks provide incomplete coverage and visual checks may not measure the property at issue. Their evidentiary value depends on whether the checked feature has been shown to predict assembly behavior. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5–6]

Independent verification asks whether quality claims and controls receive scrutiny outside the producer's own checks. MAIB concluded that manufacturer-only quality control lacked third-party oversight and could be compromised. Independence does not replace traceability or process validation; it tests whether those controls exist, are followed, and support the claims made about the finished product. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 5–7]

Together, these safeguards form an evidence chain. Item identity connects evidence to an object; batch identity connects objects to shared history; process validation connects history to a controlled manufacturing mechanism; output checks look for departures; and independent verification challenges the adequacy of the whole chain. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5–7]

## Case-study limits and appropriate use

The report demonstrates a latent construction discontinuity in one rescue-product supply chain and records comparable defects in other items. It does not determine the full affected population, compare every manufacturing process, or validate a universal visual indicator. The laboratory comparison establishes a contrast for the examined samples, not a material-independent rule. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 3, 5–7]

The digest intentionally provides no measurements, load values, quantitative comparison factors, material specification, joining technique, rescue procedure, product action, or service rule. Its training value is conceptual: an assembly can contain hidden serial discontinuities; intact material and joints must be treated as different test articles; sampling and visual appearance can miss the relevant property; and production evidence requires several noninterchangeable controls. [MAIB-THROWBAG-2019, PDF pp. 1–7]
