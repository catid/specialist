## Version and authority boundary

This is the Collective's first version, not a timeless or universal code. The PDF says version 1 was incomplete, expected practices and terminology to change, and anticipated later revision. It also acknowledges that the Collective did not represent the full diversity of the kink community as well as it wanted to. The reviewed sources therefore provide a historically situated ethics proposal from a named collective of educators and producers, not proof of present-day consensus. [KECC-HOME: Welcome; KECC-PDF: Frequently Asked Questions]

The KECC calls itself a practical roadmap rather than a precise legal code. It asks readers to apply judgment and good faith to the spirit of its commitments. The Collective did not claim to monitor adopters or centrally enforce compliance; adopters remained responsible for their own alignment and accountability processes. Adoption did not mean perfection, membership in the Collective, professional certification, or a guarantee that a class, educator, producer, venue, relationship, or activity was safe. [KECC-FULL: Introduction; KECC-PDF: Frequently Asked Questions and Adopting the KECC]

The source's use of words such as *professional*, *qualified*, *expert*, *high risk*, *safe*, or *best practice* does not create a licence, credential, clinical judgment, legal duty, or safety certification. The source says professionalism is an ethical mindset rather than a measure of income, and it explicitly recognizes that much kink education is delivered by expert amateurs because formal standards do not cover many kink topics. [KECC-HOME: Welcome; KECC-FULL: Professional competence — Discussion]

## How the document assigns responsibilities

Every main topic in the full version has three perspectives. The **educator** part addresses anyone who teaches and may sometimes inform leaders or performers. The **producer** part addresses people or organizations that hire or host educators. The **discussion** part explains the rationale, tensions, and harms the proposed conduct is intended to address. These roles should not be collapsed: an educator controls their teaching and personal conduct, while a producer controls selection, event policy, staffing, reporting structures, and institutional support. [KECC-FULL: Introduction — Organization]

The code is organized around a recurring systems idea. Students learn not only from explicit instruction but also from the conduct that educators and venues normalize. Booking confers status and access, classroom audiences add social pressure, and organizational silence can look like approval. Ethical education therefore depends on what educators model, what producers enable, how both disclose limits, and whether people can report harm without coercion or retaliation. [KECC-HOME: Welcome; KECC-FULL: Modeling consent — Discussion; Professional conduct — Discussion]

## Modeling consent

### Educator commitments

Version 1 describes excellent consent as informed agreement among people who understand their own capacity and the capacity of others involved. Agreement should be explicit or enthusiastic, free from coercion, force, and manipulation, and treated as continuous rather than a one-time permission. Doubt or confusion is a stop signal. The code distinguishes the form that agreement may take in public teaching or play from private situations, but it does not turn relationship history into unlimited permission. [KECC-FULL: Modeling consent — Educator]

Classroom touch carries no implied permission. The educator is expected to obtain specific verbal agreement from each person before touching them, avoid initiating a new negotiation in front of the classroom audience, include consent in every class, and give observers enough context to understand that a demonstration is consensual. Prior negotiation with a regular demonstration partner may be described or partially reenacted so the audience sees the consent process rather than merely being told it occurred. [KECC-FULL: Modeling consent — Educator and Discussion]

The duty to model consent is not limited to a scripted classroom moment. Version 1 argues that students also learn from an educator's public and private conduct, so a teacher who normalizes careless consent outside class can undermine what they teach inside it. This is the source's ethical position, not a claim that every aspect of private life is open to public inspection. [KECC-FULL: Modeling consent — Discussion]

### Producer commitments

The producer perspective places consent throughout the educational program rather than in a single opening statement. Producers ask educators to address consent in every class, create programs and policies that help attendees practice it, and hold staff and educators to a heightened standard while providing support to meet that standard. [KECC-FULL: Modeling consent — Producer]

### Why visible modeling matters

The discussion distinguishes *having* consent from making consent legible to students. A private negotiation may be ethically sufficient between participants yet pedagogically opaque to observers. In a class, the educator also has to prevent the audience from reasonably mistaking a demonstration for a violation or learning that consent is assumed whenever a teacher appears confident. [KECC-FULL: Modeling consent — Discussion]

## Student demonstrations: power, selection, and care

### The risk created by the classroom

Version 1 treats use of current students as demonstration partners as intrinsically high risk because educator status and classroom attention create a substantial power differential. Social pressure can make refusal or withdrawal difficult, especially after a demonstration starts. Pressure to be entertaining can make an instructor miss discomfort, and the apparent selection of volunteers for attractiveness can alienate other students. A nominally voluntary response is therefore not enough by itself to resolve the setting's power dynamics. [KECC-FULL: Using students for demos — Discussion]

### Educator preference order

The proposed preference order is to use another educator or an existing play partner first, arrange and negotiate with a suitable volunteer well before class second, and accept an in-class student volunteer only as a last resort with additional safeguards. Advance negotiation is to be written, specific about what the person is volunteering for, and specific about relevant risks. The hierarchy reduces situational pressure; it does not certify any demonstration as safe. [KECC-FULL: Using students for demos — Educator]

### Safeguards when students may volunteer

The educator is expected to announce in the class description and at the beginning that volunteers may be requested. Before asking, the educator explains what the demonstration entails and its risks. They do not call on a particular person, repeatedly pressure the room, allow one attendee to volunteer another, or tolerate heckling and coercion. If no one volunteers promptly, the demonstration is skipped. [KECC-FULL: Using students for demos — Educator and Discussion]

Selection should require unambiguous, enthusiastic agreement and the person's ability to perform what the demonstration requires. The volunteer may stop for any reason at any time. Safety, consent, and relevant ability are the primary selection criteria, while the source also encourages inclusive representation across gender, race, age, orientation, body shape, and physical ability. An existing power-exchange relationship does not erase the classroom issue; if it affects how someone was volunteered, the source says that context should be explained to the class. [KECC-FULL: Using students for demos — Educator and Discussion]

The educator accepts responsibility for care after the demonstration, remains available to provide needed aftercare, and has a formal plan communicated to the venue and class. The source uses *aftercare* as a required planning category but does not make one response appropriate for every person or teach a care procedure. [KECC-FULL: Using students for demos — Educator]

### Producer safeguards

Producers set clear standards for student demonstrations, make sure educators understand those standards, encourage advance selection and negotiation, and actively support a student who has a bad experience. The discussion expressly invites producers and educators to adopt rules more restrictive than version 1 when their circumstances warrant it. [KECC-FULL: Using students for demos — Producer and Discussion]

## Competence, qualifications, and personal risk profiles

### Teaching inside one's competence

An educator should teach only material they understand thoroughly and can teach correctly. If their approach departs from commonly accepted practice, they disclose that difference and explain the reasoning. When teaching a subject that belongs partly to a regulated or specialized profession—such as anatomy, mental health, trauma response, or legal and medical topics—they identify whether they possess relevant professional qualifications, had the material reviewed by a qualified professional, or are offering personal opinion. [KECC-FULL: Professional competence — Educator]

That disclosure does not turn professional review into endorsement of the whole class, and personal experience is not presented as a credential. The source explains that students often lack enough domain expertise to evaluate a teacher's claims independently, making accurate information and candid disclosure of limits central to informed choice. [KECC-FULL: Professional competence — Discussion]

### Producer evaluation

Producers seek educators with topic-specific expertise, distinguish confidence from competence, check that teaching stays within the person's competence, and require transparent credential disclosure. Version 1 rejects using educators who falsify or misrepresent credentials. No credential listed in a biography proves good consent, sound judgment, or complete safety; producer evaluation remains a broader process. [KECC-FULL: Professional competence — Producer]

### Respecting different risk decisions

The educator does not pressure a student to exceed that student's risk profile. Students may observe rather than perform an activity. For material the educator considers high risk, the source calls for clear risk communication and in-class negotiation before hands-on practice, along with respect for the venue's policies. These are autonomy and disclosure commitments, not a risk-ranking system or authorization to teach a particular technique. [KECC-FULL: Professional competence — Educator]

The code's discussion recognizes a structural problem: formal professional standards do not exist for many kink activities, and professionals in adjacent fields may have limits on what they can teach. It therefore expects diligence and honest limits from expert amateurs rather than inventing a certification the field does not possess. [KECC-FULL: Professional competence — Discussion]

## Inclusion, accessibility, and the learning environment

### Educator responsibilities

Version 1 asks educators to treat people respectfully and not discriminate on protected or marginalized characteristics, retaliate for a discrimination complaint, or create a hostile environment. When their conduct causes such an environment, they should apologize and correct course. Educators use a person's stated pronouns, ask when unsure, intervene around classroom microaggressions, and avoid imposing gender roles except where a genuinely gender-specific topic requires that context. [KECC-FULL: Inclusion — Educator]

Teaching should work across genders, body types, and physical abilities whenever possible. Educators should support different learning needs and actively develop ways for students with those needs to learn. Inclusivity is not merely demographic appearance: it affects examples, language, volunteer selection, physical participation, instructional format, and the ability to audit rather than perform. [KECC-FULL: Inclusion — Educator; Using students for demos — Educator]

The source describes microaggressions as indirect or subtle discriminatory acts whose harmful character does not depend on hostile intent. It distinguishes overt derogation, communications that demean a marginalized person, and statements that minimize or invalidate that person's experience. The useful teaching principle is to respond to effect and context, not to treat lack of intent as automatic resolution. The source's culture-specific examples are omitted because they are illustrative, not kink techniques or universal diagnostic tests. [KECC-FULL: Inclusion — Discussion]

### Producer responsibilities

Producers seek diversity among staff and educators, include varied perspectives in decisions, consider inclusion in event design and promotion, and remain open to feedback. Version 1 encourages tracking and publishing diversity information when feasible but acknowledges that not every organization can do so. [KECC-FULL: Inclusion — Producer and Discussion]

The event-level commitments include a published conduct code, refusal to tolerate bigotry or harassment, a formal route for reporting consent incidents and other problems, and at least one staff member trained to respond. The producer also avoids working with people whose beliefs are incompatible with fair treatment. The discussion recommends plans to support people who are triggered or experience trauma and training staff in diversity and inclusion when possible. [KECC-FULL: Inclusion — Producer and Discussion]

These provisions describe an aspirational event structure. A published policy or trained staff role does not prove accessibility, impartial handling, medical competence, legal compliance, or safety; actual implementation and community feedback still matter.

## Accountability contacts and circles

### Accountability as a learning process

Version 1 defines accountability as a process for addressing and repairing conduct that conflicts with stated values and commitments. It assumes people will sometimes fail, so the relevant questions include whether they invite feedback, acknowledge a mistake, apologize, repair what can be repaired, learn, and change patterns. Accountability is not equated with claiming perfection. [KECC-FULL: Accountability — Educator and Discussion]

An educator proactively seeks feedback from partners, students, peers, and community leaders; responds honestly to reports of injury or consent problems; participates in a relevant accountability process; and follows the processes of producers and organizations with which they work. They publicly identify their voluntary commitment and make an accountability contact available. The source also opposes nondisclosure agreements or threats of legal action used to prevent partners from discussing their experiences. [KECC-FULL: Accountability — Educator]

A producer likewise makes its commitment and contact route visible, gives that contact information to students and attendees, and publishes or develops a written process covering staff, educators, and attendees. Version 1 does not supply a central tribunal, investigation standard, sanction table, or proof that any local process is fair. [KECC-FULL: Accountability — Producer; KECC-PDF: Frequently Asked Questions]

### Contact and circle roles

In the source's provisional vocabulary, an **accountability contact** is a trusted intake person for safety or consent concerns and a repository for incident information. An **accountability circle** is a group that advises and, when needed, coordinates an accountability process. A circle member need not be a contact, and an individual or organization may have several contacts. The source warns that terminology was not consistent across the community and expected this area to evolve. [KECC-FULL: Accountability — Discussion]

The design suggestions try to reduce conflicts and blind spots. At least one contact should be independent of close romantic, play, friendship, or business ties. Multiple contacts let a reporter avoid someone they do not trust. Contacts and circle members should be diverse, approachable, and trusted by both the subject and community. One designated repository can help identify patterns across reports, while sensitive information should remain limited to people with a compelling need to know. [KECC-FULL: Accountability — Discussion; Professional conduct — Discussion]

These roles do not themselves establish investigator competence, due process, confidentiality, independence, or safety. They are a proposed communication and memory structure. Local policy must still address conflicts of interest, access, retention, response, support, proportionality, and the privacy tension discussed below.

## Boundaries between educators and students

### Conduct during teaching

The educator maintains a professional demeanor in public classes and private lessons. During class they do not cruise or engage in recreational play with a student with whom they have no existing relationship, and they explain existing partner agreements when those agreements provide necessary context for classroom behavior. They follow venue rules and maintain a formal policy for sex, play, or romance with students, developed with input beyond themselves. [KECC-FULL: Boundaries with students — Educator]

If an educator considers a relationship with a current or former student, version 1 calls for exceptional care, explicit discussion of the educational power differential and its consent risks, and scrupulous consent practice. Mentoring begins with negotiated boundaries around play, sex, and romance; any expectation that play or sex is exchanged for mentoring must be disclosed before the relationship begins. Disclosure does not by itself remove coercion or make an exchange ethical. [KECC-FULL: Boundaries with students — Educator]

### Policy choice and contextual risk

The Collective did not impose an absolute prohibition in version 1, but said a blanket prohibition would be preferable in most cases and strongly recommended that educators and producers consider it. The discussion identifies combined teacher/student and dominance/submission power differences, alienation of other students, reputational effects, status trading, and predatory access as concerns. Circumstances such as class intimacy and duration can change the risk. [KECC-FULL: Boundaries with students — Discussion]

Producers create and publish clear written standards and give them to educators before teaching. The discussion also applies conflict-of-interest reasoning to producers' relationships with educators: hiring a partner is not automatically unethical, but exchanging professional opportunity for sex or play is. [KECC-FULL: Boundaries with students — Producer and Discussion]

## Professional conduct and bidirectional vetting

### Educator candor and reliability

The educator gives complete, accurate answers during booking; represents experience and qualifications honestly; states logistical and compensation expectations clearly; arrives prepared and on time; and recognizes that conduct while working reflects on the host. [KECC-FULL: Professional conduct — Educator]

### Producer selection and support

Version 1 tells producers to use a real vetting process for staff and visiting educators, consider both professional and personal misconduct relevant to the role, and reject patterns of consent violation, manipulation, malice, or deception. New educators should receive coaching and responsibilities proportionate to developing expertise. When a producer chooses someone with a controversial consent history, the source favors accurate disclosure sufficient for attendees to make an informed choice. [KECC-FULL: Professional conduct — Producer]

Producers listen respectfully to community reports, take concerns seriously, and maintain a response process. They also treat educators fairly, communicate expectations and commitments, keep those commitments, share policies that exceed the KECC, and help educators understand local standards. Version 1 asks for an event policy concerning attendees known for consent or safety problems but does not prescribe a sanction, investigation, or exclusion procedure. [KECC-FULL: Professional conduct — Producer]

### Information used in vetting

The suggested intake topics include an educator's accountability contact; significant injuries; accusations of consent violation; accountability processes; exclusions from events; changes made to prevent recurrence; approach to consent and demonstration partners; subject-matter experience; qualifications; teaching experience; and whether they follow the code. These are disclosure prompts, not a scoring rubric. A denial, reference, credential, or completed questionnaire does not prove absence of risk. [KECC-FULL: Professional conduct — Discussion]

References supplied by an applicant are not sufficient on their own. The source recommends consulting more independent sources, including prior education coordinators. Information gathered during vetting should be handled confidentially and restricted to people with a compelling need. Individual network names and links from the source are omitted because they are not enduring conduct principles. [KECC-FULL: Professional conduct — Discussion]

Vetting runs in both directions. Educators may ask venues and producers about consent and accountability policies because teaching there can look like endorsement of the venue. Likewise, booking an educator gives that person status, access, and an apparent institutional endorsement. Both sides should therefore examine the relationship they are legitimizing, not only the class outline. [KECC-FULL: Professional conduct — Discussion]

## Disclosure, incident history, and privacy

### Educator disclosure and non-retaliation

Version 1 asks educators to give producers complete and accurate information about consent incidents they caused or were accused of, injuries they caused or were accused of, accountability processes focused on them, and sanctions or exclusions from kink groups. When disclosing, they protect the privacy of people who may have been harmed. The wording deliberately includes allegations as well as concluded findings; disclosure is not the same as adjudicating truth. [KECC-FULL: Disclosure and privacy — Educator]

Educators do not pressure people to remain silent, demand that partners refrain from discussing their experiences, or retaliate when concerns are shared directly or through others. They also respect private forums: they do not solicit information they should not receive, consume improperly shared information, or harass and interrogate people for discussing them privately. [KECC-FULL: Disclosure and privacy — Educator]

### Producer handling

Producers protect identifying information about attendees, restrict private material to people with a specific need, and put the privacy and safety of harmed people first when warning other producers about a potentially harmful person. At the same time, version 1 asks producers to be as open as possible about consent incidents or injuries at their events while protecting attendee privacy. [KECC-FULL: Disclosure and privacy — Producer]

### The unresolved tension

The discussion treats privacy and disclosure as a genuine conflict rather than a rule with an easy answer. Kink stigma and the intimacy of the subject can make privacy critical. Secrecy can also let a person with a harmful pattern reach new people who lack information needed for an informed decision. The source says no clear best practice had been established and expected this section to evolve. [KECC-FULL: Disclosure and privacy — Discussion]

Version 1's proposed balancing principles are to respect everyone's privacy where possible, recognize that educators accept heightened scrutiny, give greater weight to the privacy of people who may have been harmed, consider their wishes strongly, and also consider community safety, other affected people, and fairness. A person may respond to public allegations, but the response should be proportionate, non-punitive, respectful, and avoid renewed harm. [KECC-FULL: Disclosure and privacy — Discussion]

These principles do not resolve evidentiary standards, mandatory reporting, data protection law, defamation, medical confidentiality, or due process. The source is not legal advice and does not authorize public identification, private surveillance, retaliation, or disclosure beyond what is ethically and lawfully justified.

## Integrated interpretation

Version 1 can be read as a chain of responsibilities:

1. **State limits before authority is granted.** Educators disclose competence and producers distinguish expertise from confidence. [KECC-FULL: Professional competence]
2. **Make consent visible without manufacturing it in public.** Prior, specific negotiation and audience context matter; classroom pressure cannot substitute for agreement. [KECC-FULL: Modeling consent]
3. **Treat power as part of the consent environment.** Teacher status, audience attention, mentoring, booking, and institutional endorsement all change people's options. [KECC-FULL: Using students for demos; Boundaries with students; Professional conduct]
4. **Let a person decline participation without penalty.** Auditing, skipping a demonstration, and withdrawing remain available choices. [KECC-FULL: Professional competence; Using students for demos]
5. **Build inclusion into instruction and event systems.** Language, access, representation, reporting, staffing, and response design all affect who can participate. [KECC-FULL: Inclusion]
6. **Create routes for concerns before an incident.** Contacts, circles, public policies, and trained responders should exist before they are needed. [KECC-FULL: Inclusion; Accountability]
7. **Vet the status relationship, not just the syllabus.** Educators evaluate venues and producers evaluate educators because either association can communicate endorsement. [KECC-FULL: Professional conduct]
8. **Respond to mistakes with truth, repair, learning, and changed behavior.** A code commitment without a credible response to harm is incomplete. [KECC-FULL: Accountability]
9. **Hold privacy and informed choice together.** Disclosure should warn and support without casually exposing or revictimizing people. [KECC-FULL: Disclosure and privacy]
10. **Do not turn adoption into certification.** A signature, contact, policy, or questionnaire is evidence of a stated process, not proof of compliance or safety. [KECC-PDF: Frequently Asked Questions and Adopting the KECC]

## What this source does not establish

The reviewed KECC v. 1 does not establish current community consensus, legal requirements, professional licensure, a certification program, a centralized enforcement body, an investigation standard, or a guarantee of safety. It does not prove that an educator is competent because they adopted it, that a producer is accountable because a contact is listed, or that a demonstration is consensual because the class was told negotiation occurred. [KECC-PDF: Frequently Asked Questions and Adopting the KECC; KECC-FULL: Introduction]

This adaptation does not teach a kink, bondage, medical, rescue, or demonstration technique. Source examples naming particular practices were used only to understand competence and disclosure categories and were not converted into instructions. Decisions about medical or legal issues require appropriately qualified, current, jurisdiction-specific help.
